The Good Enough Dance

© Rev. Lora Brandis

Preached at the Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship

November 10, 2013

 

Several years ago two co-workers and I traveled from Dallas to Austin for an arts conference hosted by the Texas Commission on the Arts. The proposal for our small ethnic dance company to be included in the touring roster of Texas artists had been accepted and our ballet folklorico troupe had been invited to perform. Attending this conference was to be our reward for all of the hard work that had gone into preparing the proposal, which included extensive research on the costs of touring, something our small company had never done. This was the first step to being accepted on a larger artists’ roster created by the National Endowment for the Arts that paid for tours throughout the Southwest and the Midwest.

As the development director of the organization, I was used to writing grant proposals and speaking to potential funders to prove that our organization was “good enough” to receive support. It was tough for our tiny company to be able to afford the trip to Austin. We had been through a lot together, including the threat of having to dissolve our organization due to the lack of funds. All of us needed this reward. All of us needed to be reminded that we were good enough to stay in business.

Choreographer, performer, writer, and educator Liz Lerman was the keynote speaker for the conference. She spoke, she taught workshops and on the final day of the conference she created a community dance with all those in attendance, arts administrators and dancers alike. Of her own career Lerman, now in her sixties, says this, “I received critical acclaim and critical bruising. I got awards from the National Endowment for the Arts, from universities, from journals and newspapers and cultural institutions and a MacArthur "genius" grant. I also received more rejections than I can count or list or remember.”


So when I stood up to make a contribution to the community dance all those years ago at the Texas Commission on the Arts spring conference, Lerman must have known what I meant by saying, “those of us in the arts always struggle with being good enough.”

All of us struggle with being good enough, of course. In his book, The Examined Life: How We Lose and Find Ourselves, psychoanalyst and University College London professor Stephen Grosz points out that lavishing praise on a child does not lead to greater self-esteem. He cites recent research when he notes that “Often a child will react to praise by quitting – why make a new drawing if you have already made ‘the best’? Or a child may simply repeat the same work – why draw something new, or in a new way, if the old way always gets applause?” Why does one generation of parents over praise their children? Probably to make up for the extreme opposite styles of their own parents.

Those who were brought up without acknowledgement of any kind want to raise their children differently than they were raised, but this doesn’t work either according to Grosz. “In trying so hard to be different from our parents, we’re actually doing much the same thing – doling out empty praise the way an earlier generation doled out thoughtless criticism.” How do we teach self-esteem and how do we recover our lost self-esteem? By being present, Grosz says. All that is needed to recover a sense of “good enough” is to be present to our own inherent worth and dignity.

The religious response to “good enough” is found in our first principle. As Unitarian Universalists we affirm the inherent worth and dignity of every person, including ourselves. The religious response to good enough is also found in one of Jesus’ sayings about the lilies of the field, a saying captured by UU minister Lynn Ungar in her poem “Camas Lilies.”

Even now, unneeded and uneaten,

the camas lilies gaze out above the grass

from their tender blue eyes.

Even in sleep your life will shine.

Make no mistake. Of course

your work will always matter.

 

Yet Solomon in all his glory

was not arrayed like one of these.

 

Of course your work will always matter, but your work does not prove your worth. Your work isn’t the proof you need to declare that you are finally good enough. Praise you receive for your work can be a trap causing you to be less creative and to take less risk. Declare yourself good enough now and the praise won’t matter; only the work will matter.

I am in my fourth year as a minister and in my third year as your minister, having been called to the ministry after a career in nonprofit management including as development director of Dallas’ Anita N. Martinez Ballet Folklorico. It took me seven years to finish seminary – a process that included scrutiny by faculty, a career assessment led by a psychologist and the approval of two different bodies of the Unitarian Universalist Association. I answered questions, I read hundreds of books and I proved my competency in fifteen different areas including theology, world religions, human development and administration. I know a little bit about good enough.

So in January when I participated in a retreat with the board and the council I recognized a familiar drum beat. I heard the rhythm of the not good enough dance starting to play. Each group of the council was asked to tie its goals for the year to the long range plan. That means the board, the minister, the director of religious education, operations, outreach, membership and communications all need to clearly state what it is they hope to accomplish for the year. As people stood to make their contribution to the goals for the year, I began to hear the drum beat of “not enough time, not enough money, not enough people, not enough…not enough…not enough.”

We live in a perfectionistic culture that causes us to respond to “not enough” with over functioning and over compensating. When the work doesn’t get done exactly the way we think it should get done, we step in and do it ourselves. Taking over and correcting someone’s efforts is just as damaging to their self-esteem as offering hollow praise.


As your minister, I have discovered that the perfectionistic culture here mirrors my own need to prove that I am good enough. I am humbled by the opportunity to remind us all that whether we hang on to these buildings or have to reduce staff that somehow we are all "good enough" - that our shared ministry right now is exactly the ministry that is needed. And as we appreciate who we are as a congregation, amazingly pledges are increasing and worst-case scenarios are quickly fading out of the realm of possibility. Have you noticed that as we declare ourselves as generous, we become generous? As we declare ourselves as good enough, we become good enough. Our work will always matter.

Another religious response to “good enough” can be found in the Tao Te Ching, best explained to the Western mind in Benjamin Hoff’s classic The Tao of Pooh. When Hoff recounts the Chinese fable of the stonecutter who doesn’t recognize his own value, it is the famous English bear Winnie-the-Pooh that best illustrates what the story means. Pooh, the bear of very little brain, has a way of noticing the value in everyday life. Pooh can always see what is right in front of him. He knows this secret: “Tao does not do, but nothing is not done.” Winnie-the-Pooh knows the secret of good enough: “The Wise are Who They Are. They work with what they’ve got and do what they can do” (Hoff p. 64).

We are all good enough because nothing is wrong. This doesn’t mean that we lavish praise on our children, telling them that their tree is the best art we’ve ever seen. It also doesn’t mean that we point out everything that’s wrong with their tree. It simply means that we notice that the tree is green. As Stephen Grosz reminds us, “why draw something new, or in a new way, if the old way always gets applause?” Being present is hard. Being present requires vulnerability.

I am at my most vulnerable when I stand in the pulpit. It has become a bit of a joke that more than one person will come up to me after the worship service and say, "You really hit that one out of the park!" or "Another homerun!" I have mentioned this often enough that some of you are becoming self-conscious. One Sunday one member, looking for another way to say that said, "put a gold star on that one."

I’ve mentioned these comments enough now that I’m afraid many of you are becoming uncomfortable with that awkward moment as you walk in a line past me as I stand in the vestibule. I do appreciate the praise and I’d like to invite you into something more. If it means that all you say is “good morning” or “thank you,” that will be enough. It will be good enough, in fact. You could say, “There is a lot of blue in your picture,” and I will know what you mean.

It is enough to stand here and look at your faces. It is enough to hear your response in the stunned silences after some services. It is enough to see how you respond to the words in a hymn when we have to walk through yet another Sunday together struggling to make sense of the latest act of senseless violence in our world. I am at my most vulnerable in the pulpit because you are at your most vulnerable when you show up in this Sanctuary on a Sunday morning. This is not a performance. I do not need your applause. I need your presence because the work we do together matters.

The “good enough” dance I participated in all those years ago at the arts conference wasn’t a performance either. It wasn’t even a “good enough” dance. It had a lot more in it than just good enough. Participants shared what the conference had meant to them, what they had learned, what they would be carrying home with them. As they talked, they gestured, their weight shifted, they moved naturally in a dance that arose out of what was right in front of them. Words became gestures and gestures became choreography in a community dance not meant for the stage. “Good enough” became a movement of the hands to signify the quotation remarks around the phrase. The movement ended with the hands resting, crossed over our hearts. For weeks the three of us who had gone to Austin to be reminded that we were “good enough” to keep our organization afloat would spontaneously break into the dance, small movements to remind us of time spent with other struggling humans.

It’s been more than fifteen years. All I remember is the “good enough” part of the dance. And that’s, well…good enough.
