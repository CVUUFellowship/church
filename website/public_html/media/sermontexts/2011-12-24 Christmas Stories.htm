Christmas Stories

©Lora Brandis

Preached December 24, 2010

South Valley Unitarian Universalist Society

December 24, 2011

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship

 

Tonight we tell the ancient story and sing the carols of old. Gather round the tree. Look for the star. Light the candles. There are many stories to be told this night.

THE story as told in the biblical account proclaims, “to you is born this day in the city of David a Saviour, who is the Messiah, the Lord” (Luke 2:11). This is the way the story is being told throughout the world tonight, and for those who tell it in this particular way there is only one story to be told. If you don’t believe it then you are not part of the story, part of the real meaning of Christmas.

We tell the story somewhat differently here. Here we say there are many Christmas stories, not just the story of a child of lowly birth “whose uncommon life has been long remembered” (“Why Not A Star” Margaret Gooding).

Here we also tell the story of the tree. The tree’s story begins before the birth of Christ. In ancient northern Europe people decorated their houses with greenery to persuade the rest of nature, which was lying dormant in winter, to come back to life. The Christmas tree, an evergreen, is a reminder of that ancient custom, although the Christmas tree’s story is more directly related to the World Tree found in Norse mythology. Early Christians told the story of St. Boniface who cut down the Norseman’s tree and found a fir tree growing in its place. This proved to him that the Christian God replaced the Norse gods of the local tribe. Most Christmas stories are like that, you dig below the surface and you’ll find another story in the roots.

Eventually the tree travels from Northern Europe to America. Lots of stories are told about the first tree in America. Apparently many cities want to take credit for the first Christmas tree in our country. The best story to tell here on this night is the story of Charles Follen, a German immigrant to Boston who may have been the first in America to decorate a tree for Christmas in the early 1800s. Charles was the first professor of German at Harvard University, an outspoken opponent of slavery and a Unitarian minister.

Tonight we tell the ancient story and sing the carols of old. We tell the story of the tree and a Unitarian minister who brought this ancient symbol to this country.

Here we also tell the story of the star. We like to tell the science behind the story of the star here. It is now possible to map the night sky as it looked in Jerusalem the night that Jesus was born. Scientists can look at what astronomical events took place during that time. “Some wonder if the star of Bethlehem occurred when Jupiter and Saturn crossed,” writes the poet Timothy Steele. Most scientists think that this conjunction would not have caused anything spectacular in the sky above Bethlehem. The star could have been Halley’s Comet or maybe a supernova, which occurred in a nearby galaxy the night Jesus was born.

Except we don’t really know when Jesus was born. At first the early Christians didn’t even celebrate Jesus’ birth because they didn’t celebrate any birthdays. Celebrating birthdays was a pagan practice, not something the Christians looked favorably upon. Eventually Christians were surrounded by enough pagans who not only celebrated birthdays in general, they also celebrated the birthdays of the gods they worshipped. So a date needed to be set and eventually the date we have come to associate as Jesus’ birthday was picked – January 6th.

This is another story where we have to dig deeper to find its ancient roots. At first Jesus’ birthday was celebrated on the 6th of January because it already had significance for other Greek deities like Dionysus. Eventually the celebration was moved to the 25th of December as Christianity became centered in Rome, where their custom was to celebrate the birth of the Sun with the lighting of lights during the darkest of days.

Tonight we tell the ancient story and sing the carols of old. We tell the story of the star understanding science might explain its existence, knowing that poetry better explains the star’s significance.

Here we also tell the story of St. Nicholas, Santa Claus. In France he is Pére Noel, in England Father Christmas. “Santa is represented by Mother Star in Poland; by Kristkind in Bavaria; Jul-Nisse in Denmark; Kris Kringle in Germany, and by Grandpa Koleda in Bulgaria” (Celebrating Christmas, p. 17). In America Saint Nicholas becomes Santa Claus because of the Dutch Americans’ pronunciation. We know him best as the broad-faced, round-bellied, jolly old fellow from Clement Clark Moore’s poem.

The story I tell each year about Santa Claus is a story about my youngest daughter Zoe, who one Christmas Eve when she was three or four years old decided she would stay up and wait for Santa to come. We arrived home from church slightly after midnight. At the time I was a single parent and had a lot still left to do to get ready for Santa’s arrival. As I cleaned the kitchen and finished baking (Santa would expect some cookies and milk when he arrived), Zoe sat on the floor of the kitchen with a pillow and blanket listening attentively for the sounds of Santa’s sleigh. As I worked I glanced down every once and awhile and saw her alert little body slowly move towards the blanket, the pillow and the floor. By around 2am, Zoe was asleep and Santa still had not arrived. As I carried her to bed I thought I heard something, but I was too tired to investigate. The next morning we could tell that Santa had been there while we slept. Under the tree was a fully-assembled Barbie house complete with working lights and a buzzing doorbell.

Tonight we tell the ancient story and sing the carols of old. We tell the story of Santa Claus and the wonder of a child; the hope embodied by all children on this special night.

Here we also tell the story of the child called Jesus. It is one of the many stories we tell. We tell it to remind us that “each night a child is born is a holy night” (Sophia Lyon Fahs). We tell it to remind us that the potential to live an uncommon life is within each of us. We tell it to remind ourselves of the sacred power of all of the stories of Christmas, including our own.

Tonight we tell the ancient story and sing the carols of old. Gather round the tree. Look for the star. Light the candles. There are many stories to be told.
