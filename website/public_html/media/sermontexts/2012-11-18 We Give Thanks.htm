We Give Thanks

© by Lora Brandis

Preached November 18, 2012

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship

 

Thanksgiving Song

Words and music by Mary Chapin Carpenter

 

Grateful for each hand we hold, gathered round this table

from far and near we travel home, blessed that we are able.

Grateful for this sheltered place with light in every window.

Say, “Welcome, welcome share this feast. Come in, away from sorrow.”

Father, mother, daughter, son, neighbor, friend, and friendless.

Altogether, everyone, in the gift of loving kindness.

 

The words of this Thanksgiving song conjure up a traditional American scene: the meal as the center of giving thanks. The meal as the magnet, pulling us home to friend and family. The meal as the center of a celebration that welcomes all, drawing in family, friend and friendless into a circle of loving kindness - the circle that begins and ends in gratitude.

Three years ago was the last Thanksgiving I spent with my family. I have a new tradition now. Having moved from Texas, waiting for my husband to retire and join me here in California, and knowing how impossible it is to gather the family from near and far for two holidays each year, I am grateful for the sheltered place I find myself in this time of year.

Three years ago my sister insisted the entire family gather together. This was a rare request. We had given up on gathering our growing families in Houston at my mother’s house for both Thanksgiving and Christmas. We had created our own holiday that takes place sometime in January. Attendance at “Christmas in January” rises and falls over the years. New significant others are added and subtracted; children in college drift in and out as their schedules become more complicated, divorces and marriages, births and deaths have shaped our gatherings. Last year we welcomed my new grandniece. This year we may not gather at all.

Even as the Christmas holiday was becoming more difficult to keep, Thanksgiving became altogether impossible. So we gave up. Three years ago it was different.  It was the first holiday without my mother. My sister insisted that we all show up this one time.

I would like to tell you that we had a Thanksgiving gathering worthy of the Waltons, including the sweet sounds of “goodnight Johnboy” echoing through the halls. Knowing that we would be gathering at the house my nephew shared with two roommates at Texas A&M University, I had packed the double air mattress. My daughters stretched out on the living room couch. My nephews were in sleeping bags on the floor. Sharing the air mattress placed on the floor near them, my husband and I were pitched about on a rough sea each time our slightest move displaced the air beneath us. I’m sure one of us yelled out “goodnight Johnboy,” ironically acknowledging our gathered family in that room and scattered throughout the house.

This was perhaps the only way we could express gratitude for our gathered family that year. Our grief was all too new, too raw. We hid behind mountains of food; the only emotion expressed was anguish over the annual football match-up between our rival universities.

The following year I started my new tradition of sharing Thanksgiving with my church family. I joined five members of the Pocatello, Idaho Fellowship and drove up to the Idaho Falls church for their community Thanksgiving. I, thankfully, did not drive. I add thankfully with emphasis because truly the fact that I didn’t drive the icy freeway between Pocatello and Idaho Falls rose to the top of my gratitude list as we counted the cars that had slid off the highway and got stuck in the piled snow on the side of the road.

I say gratitude list like I actually keep one; I don’t. I have a friend who keeps a gratitude journal and highly recommends the practice. I know that many Facebook users have been practicing 30 days of gratitude this November. It is good that we set aside a whole day for thanks giving each year. All of these practices can remind us how to become a person who feels gratitude. “The Buddha said that two things are remarkably rare in this world: people who benefit others, and people who feel gratitude,” according to Joseph Goldstein (Insight Meditation, p. 145). We can reflect on our lives and generate authentic gratitude, but it takes practice.

The Apostle Paul, writing to the Thessalonians in what is probably the oldest Christian document in existence, tells them to “rejoice always, pray without ceasing, give thanks in all circumstances” (1 Thes 5:16-18). This is the portion of the letter where Paul tells the church at Thessalonica how to behave. He does this in all of his letters, particularly when members of the church are misbehaving. In this letter Paul tells the early Christians to be grateful, among other behaviors. Not only are they to be grateful, rejoice always and to pray without ceasing; they are also to refrain from quenching the Spirit, they are to test the word of the prophets by looking for what is good and they are also to trust that which calls them forward in their lives.

But, give thanks in all circumstances….really, all? That would have been my response if I had been among the churchgoers listening to Paul’s letter being read aloud after it had arrived. I have an easier time hearing “pray without ceasing” than I do “give thanks in all circumstances.” When I hear that I am to “pray without ceasing” I also hear the seventh step on the Noble Eightfold Path: right mindfulness.

Right mindfulness becomes a prayer when it leads us from attention to appreciation. Mindfulness awakens us to levels of perceiving where gratitude becomes possible. Gratitude flows naturally out of paying attention to what is.

The 13th Century Persian mystical poet Rumi tells us to “be grateful for whoever comes.” He uses the metaphor of a guest house to describe the human condition. He lists the unwanted guests of depression, meanness, and sorrow alongside the guests that are easier to entertain. We cannot shut out the unwanted guests. Gratitude gives equal weight to all who visit.

Author and UU minister Kate Braestrup writes:

            Genuine gratitude, the gratitude of prayer, must ultimately arise out of a profound recognition that things could be other than what they are.

            If you will forgive a small diversion into a kind of mysticism – (she writes) things could not be. At all.

            Yet here you are!

            And here I am! How cool is this? (Beginner’s Grace: Bringing Prayer to Life)

 

This is not to say that we express gratitude because “things could be worse.” We express gratitude out of a profound sense of existential astonishment. We are grateful just to exist. To express this existential gratitude we can start small. We can start by appreciating something that is easy to appreciate.

This is the logic behind metta meditation. In metta, or loving-kindness, meditation we begin with someone we already love, with someone we appreciate, someone for whom it is easy for us to be grateful. We could begin with ourselves; for some this is not the easiest place to begin. We may want to begin with ourselves. If we can love ourselves, be truly grateful for who we are, then gratitude naturally flows from us to all we encounter.

Starting with someone who is easy to love, we move on to someone who is neutral, then someone who is difficult to love. As we practice loving-kindness as a meditation, as a prayer without ceasing, genuine gratitude for all that is begins to be a part of who we are. Giving thanks in all circumstances begins to make sense.

Just as the unwanted guests of negativity, shame and malice show up on the metaphorical steps of our personal guest house, the holidays can bring unwanted reminders of broken relationships and unresolved pain from our past. Rarely do we give thanks in those circumstances, adding a page to our journal giving thanks for an abusive relationship or a lost job. Start with someone who is easy to love. Start with some thing that is easy to love.

I remember another Thanksgiving. My husband’s mother had fallen and broken her hip. She was in a nursing home for rehabilitation. Thankfully, she was only there for a short while before she was discharged to home care. The tradition on this side of the family was that Frank and I hosted the Thanksgiving dinner. Frank’s mother and father hosted Christmas Eve dinner, which always featured Cuban rice and beans and flan for dessert.

Frank’s mother liked a traditional Thanksgiving dinner so we stopped off at the local cafeteria before our planned visit with her. If you are wondering what kind of ungrateful children bring their mother cafeteria food for Thanksgiving, let me briefly explain that Texas has a long tradition of home style cooking that comes from well-known, well-loved cafeterias. We stopped at my mother-in-law’s favorite cafeteria to pick up Thanksgiving dinner for her that day. It was the quietest Thanksgiving dinner we have ever had.

If you don’t already know this, let me tell you a well-known fact that had escaped me until I married into the Peña family: Cubans are loud. Most holiday dinners include my father-in-law’s customary announcement of the news of the day; one year that meant he read to us from an article he had clipped from the newspaper listing everything he thought we needed to know about turkeys before he carved ours. Politics are always discussed, loudly, with differing opinions flying around the room like shots fired in a crowded bar.

This Thanksgiving was different. Frank, his brother, his father and I quietly visited while his mother ate her Thanksgiving dinner out of Styrofoam containers. Surprisingly, there were leftovers from this dinner for one. Frank and I gratefully ate them the next day, noticing how the detail of leftovers after Thanksgiving gave a sense of normal to such an awkward holiday.

Both our mothers are gone now. It would be easy to put at the top of our lists that we are grateful that they gave birth to us. That is the obvious place to start, the existential gratitude that all human beings can start with. I would rather start small, appreciating the trivial, like my mother’s sweater I wore to keep me warm while I wrote this sermon or my mother-in-law’s winter coat that I kept in the car for emergencies when I did drive the snowy roads by myself in Idaho.

On the way to the Idaho Falls church for Thanksgiving dinner the driver of our car spotted a red-tailed hawk. In her excitement, she steered in the same direction the hawk was flying. For a brief moment her car left the safety of the plowed and sanded part of the road. As she adeptly maneuvered us back onto the freeway, the car hit a patch of ice. Thankfully we did not add to the number of cars that day that had ended up headlong into a fence or stuck in a snowdrift. The slip was momentary, the hawk was gone and we were once again heading towards Idaho Falls.

As you can imagine, this story was told and told again to the group who gathered for the church’s community Thanksgiving. Each time the telling became more dramatic. At one point in the telling a scream was added to the part where the car hit the patch of ice. Those of us sitting in the back seat were supposedly responsible for this scream. I don’t think I screamed. What they heard coming from the back seat, if indeed they heard anything, was probably a deep exclamation of thanks giving.

That’s the way I chose to tell the story on our way home. Grateful for the hawk, the adeptness of the driver and yes, even for the ice. Grateful for the shared laughter on the way home, we gave thanks for all we had been given that day.

This Thanksgiving may we all be pulled into the circle of loving kindness, the circle that begins and ends with gratitude.
