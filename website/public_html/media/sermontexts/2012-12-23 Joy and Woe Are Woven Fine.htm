Joy and Woe Are Woven Fine

© by Lora Brandis

Preached December 23, 2012

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship

 

Joy and woe are woven fine,

A clothing for the soul divine.

Under every grief and pine

Runs a joy with silken twine.

Man was made for joy and woe;

And when this we rightly know

Through the world we safely go.

 

--William Blake, from Auguries of Innocence

 

This week I received a phone call from a reporter asking if I would be addressing the Connecticut tragedy in my Christmas message. The reporter was interviewing local clergy and, having grown up a Unitarian Universalist, she was eager to include our approach to the tragedy during what is supposed to be “a joyful time of year.” She was aware that most clergy had immediately addressed the mass shooting at Sandy Hook Elementary in Newtown, Connecticut last week, three days after it had occurred. What she wanted to know was will we continue to address the tragedy at a time of year when people are “supposed to be happy?”

I reminded her that Unitarian Universalists always worship within the context of how the world is now. I assured her that even without specifically referring to the Connecticut tragedy that it would be on our minds as we looked to this time of year not as a time when we are “supposed to be happy,” but as a time that we acknowledge the ancient wisdom in the symbol of light shining in the darkness, and the darkness does not overcome it. This is the time of year when the world turns from the darkness of the longest night to more light in the lengthening of each day. This turning from darkness to light, and back again, each year is not a choice. It is the way things are. We come to this “joyful” time of year with William Blake’s understanding that “joy and woe are woven fine.”

Joy and woe are woven fine – these words come to me this time of year as I imagine sitting with my own family around the Christmas table. They came to me again when the reporter asked me how we make sense of senseless tragedy. These words surface in my consciousness often, when I visit with someone in the hospital, dedicate a child to a church community, officiate at a wedding or allow the love of a community to wash through me as I lead a memorial service.

We know that joy and woe are woven fine because every Sunday we share joys and sorrows with each other. We write in a book and share our joys and sorrows out loud. We drop a stone in the water, watching the ripples, knowing that what affects one affects us all. We drop one last stone for all that is left unsaid, for the joys and sorrows we carry in our hearts and in our minds, in the depths of our beings.

“How do we make sense of this?” the reporter wanted to know. “Why does suffering happen,” we all want to know. The theological term for this is theodicy: “How can an all-powerful, supposedly loving God, allow suffering? Allow evil?” Eastern Orthodox theologian and author David Bentley Hart examines the Christian response to extreme suffering and loss in his book The Doors of the Sea: Where Was God in the Tsunami? Hart rejects what he labels as a “rational theodicy” that sees everything as a part of the divine plan. When recalling an interview he read about a Sri Lankan man who failed to save four of his five children in the tsunami, Hart writes, “Only a moral cretin at that moment would have attempted to soothe his anguish by assuring him that his children had died as a result of God’s eternal, inscrutable, and righteous counsels, and that in fact their deaths had mysteriously served God’s purposes in history, and that all of this was completely necessary for God to accomplish his ultimate design in having created this world” (p. 99). Hart is offering a harsh assessment of the sort of shallow theology we have been hearing a lot of lately in the aftermath of the Newtown tragedy. Many of us may have to sit with the shallow theology of our friends and relatives at a holiday meal in the next few days. It may be uncomfortable to call Uncle Fred a moral cretin, but it may also be unconscionable to remain silent. We pride ourselves on being a religion that embraces freedom, reason and tolerance. These dark days have taught me that tolerating bad theology may be worse than intolerance itself.

There are alternative theologies. In Omnipotence and other Theological Mistakes philosopher and Unitarian Universalist Charles Hartshorne replaced the understanding of God as unchangeable and all powerful with the idea of God as relational and all loving. God does not cause our suffering, but joins us in our suffering and is affected by it. Former president of the UUA, Rev. John Buehrens, says this about suffering: “Job marks a beginning in the Hebrew tradition – a reluctant one – in acknowledging that some suffering comes for no good reason at all. It just happens, impersonally. It comes out of the whirlwind, as part of the nature of things” (Understanding the Bible: An Introduction for Skeptics, Seekers, and Religious Liberals p. 106). Religious humanism looks to a human being with inherent dignity and worth, who is also capable of evil. The alternative to a shallow, rational theodicy is an understanding that we co-create this world. When suffering comes out of the whirlwind, it matters how we respond.

How we respond is grounded in our seven principles and our six sources. Our first source: “direct experience of that transcending mystery and wonder, affirmed in all cultures, which moves us to a renewal of the spirit and an openness to the forces which create and uphold life.” I am not sure we could go so far as amending this statement to say “the forces which create, uphold and destroy life” because we might not want to be open to the destructive forces. However, if we are to remain open to the creative forces of life we may also want to be deeply aware of the destructive forces that are a part of the natural world. We may want to be deeply aware of the potential for destruction in each and every one of us.

Hinduism recognizes the destructive and transforming nature of deity as Shiva and his consort Kali. In the Hindu pantheon Kali is associated with death and destruction and is seen as the goddess of time and change. Shiva is the third part of the Hindu Trimurti, in which the cosmic functions of creation, maintenance, and destruction are personified by the forms of Brahmā the creator, Vishnu the maintainer or preserver, and Shiva the destroyer or transformer. Out of destruction comes change, out of destruction … transformation. Joy and woe are woven fine.

Job-like we have all cursed the very day we were born. Like Job in the Hebrew Scriptures we have all encountered suffering that leaves us feeling, and sometimes actually, stripped of everything we once possessed and everything we once thought we were. In exaggerated folktale style, Job successively loses his wealth, his children, and his physical health until he is reduced to sitting among the ashes scraping his skin with shards of pottery.

Job’s friends try to convince him he must have done something wrong, some unknown sin has caused his suffering. In the end Job is forced to reject the shallow theology of his friends when he comes face to face with a god who speaks out of the whirlwind. Job is shaken from his simplistic understanding of the sort of puppet-master god who directs each and every human action. Job wakes up. He is transformed.

I am not saying that suffering is necessary for understanding Life more deeply. I am saying that our suffering can bring us to new understandings about Life, new ways of naming what we worship. Personally, I would like to worship something that can protect me from further suffering, but I don’t think that that god exists.

We worship here together knowing deeply that we are made for both joy and sorrow. And when we come back to this realization time and time again in the songs we sing, the joys and sorrows we share, the lighting of the chalice, the realization that what we are worshipping we are becoming, we can more fully participate in all of life. We were made for joy and woe; And when this we rightly know, Through the world we safely go. It is this understanding, born of experience that protects our hearts. We are not protected from further suffering by a god who intervenes in human suffering. We are protected by the lived experience of persistence and resilience.

William Blake also reminds us that “under every grief and pine runs a joy with silken twine.” This is wisdom that can also bring us comfort, but sometimes it is only in looking back on our suffering that we can recognize the thread of joy running through all of life. If we rush too soon to make meaning out of our suffering, we become like Job’s friends who are sure they know what god is up to.

In the poem “Winter Solstice” Rebecca Parker writes:

In the universe there

 moves a Wild One

 whose gestures alter

 earth's axis

 toward

 love.

 

In the immense

 darkness

 everything spins with

 joy.

 

Who is the tsunami survivor when everything they once owned is gone? How is a parent a parent when their child is gone? How is a woman a woman when her breasts have been destroyed by cancer? How is a daughter a daughter when her mother has died? When we write in a book, drop a stone in the water and share our joys and sorrows, these questions sit with us, as we seek to trust the natural cycles that point us towards love. We reject shallow theologies, continuing in our search for theologies that teach us “everything spins with joy even in immense darkness.” The winter solstice teaches us this joy. Christmas teaches us this joy. Christmas teaches us that it comes not in spite of darkness, but because of darkness. UU minister Earl Holt knows this when he writes, “The vain trappings of the season do disguise a great emptiness, but that emptiness waiting to be filled is the heart of Christmas and in that emptiness, that darkness, Christmas is waiting to be born.”

Christmas will come amidst the shootings and the cancer and the recovery and the relapse. Christmas will come as the earth turns towards more light. Pick up a thread of joy and a thread of sadness comes with it. This is as it should be. The life lived fully knows the joy that runs deeply underneath every sorrow and heartache. The life lived deeply listens to the winter stories told and re-told with ears to hear the ancient wisdom in the symbol of light shining in the darkness, and the darkness does not overcome it.
