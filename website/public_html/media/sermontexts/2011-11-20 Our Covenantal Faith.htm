Our Covenantal Faith

© by Lora Brandis

Preached November 14, 2010

South Valley Unitarian Universalist Society

November 20, 2011

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship

 

Love is the doctrine of this church,

The quest of truth is its sacrament,

And service is its prayer.

 

To dwell together in peace,

To seek knowledge in freedom,

To serve humanity in fellowship (human need),

To the end that all souls shall

grow in(to) harmony with the Divine –

Thus do we covenant with each other

(and with God.)

 #471, Singing the Living Tradition Arranged by L. Griswold Williams

 

I know this covenant by heart because I have been repeating it most Sundays for more than ten years. I have often reflected on the fact that we end with “thus do we covenant with each other” and didn’t know there was an option to covenant with each other and with God until I was more familiar with our hymnal Singing the Living Tradition. I don’t know why God isn’t mentioned in the covenant at First Unitarian Church of Dallas, but leaving those last three words out strengthens the covenant for me. I am not objecting to the word God. Growing in harmony with the Divine implies the transcendent, the vertical relationship. The accountability to something larger than ourselves.

It is the finality of covenanting with each other that strengthens this statement for me. When Unitarian Universalists join in community, we do not rest in our assurance of a covenant with God. We acknowledge the horizontal relationship as the most important; our covenant with each other sustains us as a religious community. We are more than a collection of individuals on our own spiritual journeys. The covenant is how we create what becomes the “more” in our gathering together.

There are many member churches of the Unitarian Universalist Association that do not affirm each Sunday that love is the doctrine of their church, the quest of truth its sacrament, etc. This independence is the foundation of our congregational polity. During the year I served as a ministerial intern, every time my husband and I repeated their covenant, we had to lock eyes to help each other through the end. Horizon UU, in Carrollton, TX is a church plant from the Dallas church. It has been there twenty-five years and has its own covenant and mission. Their covenant is the same as First Dallas through the line “to seek knowledge in freedom.” And then you can hear who they are as a community in the final lines of their covenant: “To serve humanity in harmony with the earth, Thus, do we covenant together.”

Conrad Wright, the former professor of American church history at Harvard Divinity School, wrote that in many of our churches “ancient covenants still serve their essential function: to make churches out of collections of individuals; to establish community” (Walking Together, p. 8). We can pick up the thread of history with the covenant used in the Dallas church and follow it back to New England to the Bible and further back to pre-biblical agreements. The Dallas church’s covenant is a variation of the covenant found as number 471 in Singing the Living Tradition. This covenant can also be found in a book entitled Antiphonal Readings for Free Worship, arranged by L. Griswold Williams and published in 1933. Conrad Wright gives us an example of an early Puritan covenant from 1629 Salem. The concept of covenant is embedded in the Cambridge Platform of 1648, which still influences the way we gather in community today.

The first covenants were political treaties in the ancient Near East dating as far back as the fourth century B.C.E. Consequences for breaking covenants were clearly stated in the covenants themselves. These ancient political covenants were kept in shrines and holy places because ancient people saw the gods as active in human history. These covenants may have been enshrined, but they were also political promises between groups of people to define territories and to maintain agreements. According to Reverend Burton Carley, minister of the First Unitarian Church of Memphis, “The Hebrew people took the political covenant that came before them and elevated it into another dimension. They used covenant to describe the divine / human relationship thus imparting to it a deep theological and moral significance.”

The first covenant in the Hebrew Scriptures is in the ninth chapter of Genesis in the story of Noah and the flood. The second covenant is also in Genesis in the story of Abraham and God’s covenant with the people of Israel. Carley points out that Unitarian theologian James Luther Adams described these covenants as “covenants of being.” What Adams meant, Carley tells us, “is that the covenant is not just between human beings but also with the very ground of reality itself.  …This reality that is a ‘creative, sustaining, commanding, judging, transforming Power,’ requires a mutuality that is ethical and sustained by a loving trust. It is a covenant with all living creatures that endures through every generation into the future” (James Luther Adams from The Essential James Luther Adams: Selected Essays and Addresses, quoted by Burton D. Carley in his paper “We Covenant” presented to The Prairie Group, November 12, 2007).

This larger covenant calls us out of our individual selves into community. This is the covenant that allows us to see our self in another and in that seeing to become transformed. As Unitarian Universalists we each get to define how we respond to this larger covenant, this “covenant of being.” When we sign the membership book, agree to our church’s covenant, maybe even recite it in church together, we are committing ourselves not to a smaller covenant, but to a covenant that evolves rather than endures.

One of the problems with our covenants, according to Conrad Wright, “is that we do not take them seriously enough. We do not remind ourselves that a covenant is an agreement made between parties, not a statement by an individual to be discarded or forgotten unilaterally” (Walking Together, p. 9). Our covenantal faith calls us to be in relationship, to walk together.

When I hear the simple words of the Salem covenant: “we…doe bynd our selves…to walke together,” I think of the famous quote attributed to the philosopher Albert Camus. “Don't walk behind me, I may not lead. Don't walk in front of me, I may not follow. Just walk beside me and be my friend.” I say attributed to Camus because he may or may not have said it. I hope he didn’t say it because I can’t imagine that a Nobel Laureate would have said something so vapid and superficial. Now before you tell me this is your favorite quote and you have it embroidered on a pillow at home, let me explain what I mean.

Walking together is often very difficult.

This is the time of year that we as Unitarian Universalists like to remind the rest of America how proud we are to have descended from the Pilgrims and their quest for religious freedom. It is true that we can trace the roots of our covenantal faith to the Protestant Reformation and how it influenced an English minister to lead his people in the development of a congregational covenant. This congregation and others like it who wanted to further remove Catholic elements from the Church of England became known as Puritans. We can trace the roots of our covenantal faith to these Puritans and those among them known as the Pilgrims. We can also trace the roots of our faith to the Mayflower Compact, a document written because walking together is often very difficult.

The Mayflower didn’t carry one happy band of Pilgrims covenanted to walk together as friends. Only half of the group came from one congregation who had escaped religious persecution in England, spent some time in Holland and from there headed to America. This was the group we call the Pilgrims, the others on the Mayflower the Pilgrims called the Strangers because they were not from their congregation. Trouble arose when the ship was unable to land at their intended destination, the two groups found themselves at odds with each other. The Mayflower Compact, a covenant, was designed by the leaders of the two groups to create a civic structure that recognized they needed to walk together in order to survive. In this covenant can be seen the beginnings of democracy in America.

It was the Puritans of Massachusetts Bay Colony, who came after the Pilgrims, who gave us the next historic covenant in America. The Cambridge Platform of 1648 summarized the elements of a congregation’s covenant as 1. a voluntary agreement, 2. a relationship with God, 3. public worship and 4. observance of the ordinances of baptism and communion. Today we understand covenant as a voluntary agreement to gather for worship and mutual help.

The legacy of the Cambridge Platform is not only in the covenant; it is also in how we organize ourselves as covenantal bodies. Here are some of the characteristics of how we gather as a religious body that come to us from the Cambridge Platform. The church is self-governing. The laity is the ultimate authority in the church. Membership is voluntary. Ministers are called out of the laity, with each church having the authority to ordain and call its own minister. Churches are distinct and equal, and are called into fellowship and cooperation with each other.

Just as each congregation is free to make its own covenant, each is also free to create its own mission. Covenant is the promise we make to each other in how we will be the gathered community. Mission is who the congregation will be in the larger community and in the world. I know First Unitarian of Dallas’ covenant by heart, but I had to look up the mission because it recently changed. Their old mission: “The First Unitarian Church of Dallas creates and nurtures a liberal religious community where all souls shall grow in harmony with the divine through worship, education, service, and fellowship” echoes language from the covenant. Their new mission, six words that fit nicely underneath the name of the church on everything from t-shirts to the web site, is: “reaching deeper to be the way.” Like our covenants, our missions continue to evolve.

The mission of this Fellowship just changed and many of you were here last week when I announced the change. The old mission: “to build and support a community committed to living Unitarian Universalist values.” The new mission: “to nurture spiritually courageous people who transform the world through justice and compassion.” We do not have an explicit covenant that we express liturgically in our worship service each week. As a member congregation of the Unitarian Universalist Association we do covenant to affirm and promote our seven principles. And we have a statement of purpose that implies a sort of covenant. We have just adopted a new mission; the next step in becoming who we are together because of our new ministry is to create an explicit covenant.

Many congregations adopt a covenant of right relations which goes beyond the covenant designed by L. Griswold Williams for the free church of the 1930s. These documents define how we create congregations as safe places to express our individual beliefs. Covenants help us draw boundaries between acceptable and unacceptable behavior. They are statements that can be included in worship or read before meetings. The Mayflower Compact, a covenant, recognized that the Pilgrims and the Strangers needed to walk together in order to survive. We no longer need covenants in order to survive the harshness of New England winters, but we do need covenants to recognize that walking together can often be very difficult. Walking together can test us.

Barbara Brown Taylor writes that “the world’s great religions have always required communities of people to make them work… these communities are the concrete places where the teachings of the religion are tested” (An Altar in the World, p. 93). Much has been written about the lack of a cohesive Unitarian Universalist theology because we do not have a binding creed. We do not ask you to sign a belief statement when you sign the membership book. We ask you to covenant with us, to walk with us in the spirit of love and with mutual respect. This is the teaching of our religion; you might even call it our theology – the covenant. This promise that we make to dwell together in peace, to be guided by love, to seek knowledge in freedom, to respect each other, to join in meaningful action, to use the democratic process, to affirm the dignity and worth of every human being…. This promise, this evolving covenant binds us one to the other and to that which calls us out of ourselves. Thus we are bound, to walk together. Thus do we covenant with each other.
