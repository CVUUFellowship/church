Transcending Mystery and Wonder

© by Lora Brandis

Preached December 2, 2012

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship

 

Unitarian Universalism draws from many sources. The first one we name is “Direct experience of that transcending mystery and wonder, affirmed in all cultures, which moves us to a renewal of the spirit and an openness to the forces which create and uphold life.” As I have walked towards this week’s theme I have walked with Mr. Henry David Thoreau through the woods near Walden Pond, into the mystery and wonder found in nature. To do this I first needed to find my copy of Walden, tucked away somewhere in the chaos of my library that lives between the church and my home office. Before I found my copy of Walden I was mysteriously compelled to clean out my magazine rack, which holds not only magazines needing to be recycled but also travel brochures and old coffee table books placed there shortly after I unpacked from my move.

In a mini-experience of mystery and wonder, I found In Wildness Is the Preservation of the World, a book of photographs by Eliot Porter specifically chosen to go with selected writings of Thoreau. I had no recollection of owning this book. It is one of those inherited treasures that I discover from time to time, something from my mother’s book shelves hastily thrown into a box when I went through her belongings.

Published in 1962 by the Sierra Club, In Wildness Is the Preservation of the World is a warning from fifty years ago that if we are to preserve our world “we need to witness our own limits transgressed, and some life pasturing freely where we never wander” (Walden). These are Thoreau’s words from Walden written in 1854. He also wrote: “At the same time that we are earnest to explore and learn all things, we require that all things be mysterious and unexplorable, that land and sea be infinitely wild, unsurveyed and unfathomed by us because unfathomable.”

In other words, there are places we can leave to mystery. There are moments filled with wonder that do not need to be explained away with overthinking.

The discovery of a book pairing ground breaking photography from the 1960s with wisdom from our transcendentalist past is not the great mystery I would like it to be. There is a scientific explanation for my bizarre urge to clean out my magazine rack on that particular day. The explanation can be found in the mammalian part of my brain known as the reticular activating system. The reticular activating system, or RAS, is responsible for many functions, but it caught the attention of cognitive psychologists and motivational coaches about thirty years ago because of attention. The RAS is believed to control sleep, wakefulness, and the ability to consciously focus attention on something. In addition, it acts as a filter so that the senses won’t be overloaded by stimuli.

I may have known that I owned a book with selections of Thoreau’s writings, but I didn’t need to carry this information with me all of the time. The RAS helped me to filter out unneeded thoughts when I “forgot” about this book that resided in my magazine rack. And then it functioned again to focus my attention on the magazine rack when I decided to take a walk this week with the words of Henry David Thoreau.

The reticular activating system is the science behind all of those cognitive psychologists, motivational coaches and New Age gurus who advise that we can become successful by focusing on success. The RAS helps us see what we want to see. The RAS helps us create our own reality. When we focus our thoughts on what we want, on what we want to achieve, all we will see will be related to our passions, values and beliefs.


If we focus our thoughts on what we don’t want, what we think is inevitable, on what we fear will happen; all we will see will be related to our fears, lack and disbelief. So much for mystery and wonder and my magical discovery of a book that offered me an experience of what I thought was the transcendent. I focused my thoughts on Walden and Henry David Thoreau. My reticular activating system did its job and pointed me towards the magazine rack. A scientific explanation prevailed. Mystery and wonder would have to wait for another day.

“What I know I could put into a pack as if it were bread and cheese, and carry it on one shoulder, important and honorable, but so small!” – writes the poet Mary Oliver. I happen to know about the reticular activating system. This knowledge, so small and honorable, reminds me to respect the rationalist strain in our Unitarian Universalist heritage. It also reminds me to respect the mystics in our tradition like Ralph Waldo Emerson who said, “Has the naturalist or chemist learned his craft, who has explored the gravity of atoms and the elective affinities, who has not yet discerned the deeper law whereof this is only a partial or approximate statement, namely, that like draws to like….(“Circles)? Emerson the mystic, explaining the action of the reticular activating system before scientists claimed it as truth. Emerson the philosopher, daring us all to speak from a “life passed through the fire of thought.”

As Unitarian Universalists our theological exploration begins with direct experience of the kind that Emerson had when he was discerning deeper laws, describing nature and clothing mystery and wonder with words that sometimes illuminate truth and, quite frankly, sometimes shroud mystery with more mystery. Thoreau is simpler. We walk with him into his “solitudes, where the problem of existence is simplified.” We want to walk with him into the natural world and meet his “grand, serene, immortal, infinitely encouraging, though invisible, companion” (Journal edition of 1906). We find comfort from his simple living that finds mystery and wonder at every turn.

Today is the first day of Advent on the Christian calendar. Hanukkah starts Saturday. This is the season of mystery and wonder. Rather than try to overthink the miraculous in a virgin birth or a one-day supply of lamp oil lasting for eight days, Unitarian Universalists come to this time each year with a yearning to make sense of the symbol of light in the darkest part of the year. We come to this time of year yearning for an experience of the transcendent – that which takes us out of our small selves into a sense of connection and awe, an experience of renewal, a feeling of openness to that which creates and uplifts. We come to this time of year seeking mystery and wonder with the hope that it will comfort us in the way Thoreau was comforted by his solitary woodland living.

On this day when there are only 23 shopping days left until Christmas, we can look to the symbols of the season for mystery and wonder. On this day, with Halloween, Thanksgiving and “Black Friday” behind us, (not to mention “Cyber Monday” and now “Giving Tuesday”) on this day we can come to church to catch our breath and look forward to our reward for surviving it all: Christmas Day. Here’s a warning: without mystery and wonder Christmas Day becomes just another excuse for conspicuous consumption.

This morning many Christian churches, indeed many Unitarian Universalist churches, are lighting the first candle in the advent ritual with these words from Isaiah, Chapter 60: “For darkness shall cover the earth, and thick darkness the peoples.” These words are recited as a reminder of the cycles of despair and hope that have marked human history.

The writer of these verses from Isaiah offered hope to the people of Israel who had just returned from exile in 539 BCE. The return to Jerusalem had not been as successful as the people had hoped; there was economic oppression, poverty and famine. And yet the writer offers light in the darkness, a light that ultimately could come from the nation of Israel itself.

Quoting from Isaiah and the Hebrew Scriptures to celebrate a Christian ritual often becomes problematic. Not everything in Isaiah points to the birth of Jesus as the Messiah, as was once assumed. Isaiah 7:14 is the most problematic of all when it comes to Jesus’ birth. Some of you probably already know this, but it bears repeating. Understanding what happened to Isaiah when it was translated from Hebrew into the Greek, is an important piece of our message to our family and friends at this time of year when we may find ourselves explaining who we are in an American religious landscape that is marred by a defensive “war on Christmas” and a primal urge to shop until we drop.

Isaiah 7:14 is an answer to a request for a prophecy. The prophecy is this: “the young woman is with child and shall bear a son, and shall name him Immanuel.” The Hebrew word for young woman used in this particular passage, can refer to a virgin, but there is actually another word in Hebrew that more specifically means virgin. Unfortunately when the original Hebrew was translated into Greek the word parthenos was used, which cannot be translated other than as the word virgin. In the translation of one small word, a doctrine can be created.

Today most scholars agree that Isaiah was not predicting a miraculous birth to take place seven hundred years after the prophet first spoke these words. Isaiah is, in some ways, stating the obvious: the birth of a child is always a sign for hope, a sign that the future is always contained in the present. Hope always moves us forward, even when it feels like all we can do is stand still and wait.

I’ve rushed too far ahead in the story, though, it’s not Christmas yet. We have only just arrived at the First Sunday in Advent. Advent is from Adventus, which is Latin for coming towards. Christmas is coming towards us at a pace we cannot control. Today we celebrate mystery and wonder. Today we walk into the darkness together, with humility, knowing that we don’t know everything. Knowing that there is something beyond knowing that keeps calling us forward.

Alone on the mountain trails and on the concrete sidewalks of life, even in a gloomy, misty day, when most would stay inside in the comfort of habit, we look to ourselves for this annual turning from darkness to light. We once again feel connected to the great mystery that transforms from old to new. We stare at the stars in wonder waiting for our own uncommon life to be born. What we value we are becoming. What we don’t know will teach us humility. What is beyond knowing will call us to our better selves. We come to our solitude with the companions of our tradition, fellow rational mystics all. Emerson, Thoreau, Fuller, the divine being sitting next to us in worship. We let go of the unessential and see things as they are, mysterious and wonderful. Would that we could walk every day out of the darkness of our ego selves into the light of our true selves, always waiting to be born. Wishing to forget the mundane pain of our everyday lives, we walk out into the solitude of mystery and wonder. We can find this stillness and solitude in nature. We can also find it in the quiet times, the moments at rest. Walking in the landscape here, past birds of paradise in full bloom and past the lights of the season. Walking past inflated snowmen, penguins and reindeer. Walking past Santa Claus standing next to manger scenes. We can even find mystery and wonder in the plastic, the commercial, the supposedly secular of the season.

We no longer experience mystery and wonder like our ancestors did. We move towards darkness this time each year knowing that the days will eventually lengthen, light will return. Even so, we can find mystery and wonder in the bits of wildness that remain: the falling leaves, the coyote-sighting on an early morning walk, the starkness of the night sky. There are places we can leave to mystery. There are moments filled with wonder that do not need to be explained away with overthinking. We need only look within to open to the mystery and wonder that is always waiting to be born.
