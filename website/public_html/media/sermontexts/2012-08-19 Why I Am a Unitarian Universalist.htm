Why I Am a Unitarian Universalist

©Rev. Lora Brandis

Preached August 19, 2012

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Congregation

 

While I was in seminary my mother developed the habit of bringing up the most recent sermon she had heard at her church in order to challenge my theology. Maybe it was because I called her every Sunday afternoon and the sermon was fresh in her mind. Although, I think she actually called me once in the middle of the week to tell me to listen to a sermon her Episcopalian priest delivered for Easter. Apparently he had confessed to not believing in the literal resurrection while he was in seminary; his sermon centered on how that belief had changed for him. My mother called to tell me to go to their web site and listen to the sermon and then she said, “Can you imagine being in seminary and not believing that the resurrection actually happened?” And then she giggled sheepishly.

Of course my mother knew that I attended seminary and I did not believe in the literal resurrection of Jesus Christ. At least I think she knew what I believed. She really struggled with this during the last few years of her life, which coincided with my time in seminary. I attempted to help her to understand exactly what I believe. I gave her papers I had written, suggested books for her to read, and taken her to a few worship services while she was visiting from Houston. Once (I think it was after I admitted to her I had never gone to the St. Dunstan’s web site and listened to the resurrection sermon) I told her that if she really understood what I believed she wouldn’t be worried about the state of my soul. Like the historical Universalists that are represented in the name of my chosen faith, I believe that God’s love is available to all, regardless of what religious belief they profess, so my soul (just like everyone else’s) is fine, thank you very much. I think my mother used to bring these issues up in our weekly conversations because she was actually worried that I may be headed for eternal damnation. Maybe this was because she had lived with cancer for eight years before she died; most of those years I was in seminary. Perhaps she was afraid she would not see me in heaven and wanted to do all she could before she died to make sure I will join her there someday.

The only sermon I listened to from my mother’s priest, the man who presided at her funeral, was preached on the Sunday in the Christian calendar calls the Feast of the Epiphany and the Baptism of our Lord Jesus Christ. I listened to the sermon because my mother was particularly taken by the story the priest opened with from “A Prairie Home Companion,” which seemed to disparage Unitarians. I did my usual inept job of explaining to my mother what I found unacceptable in the sacrament of baptism and why we practice child dedication instead. But, all she could say was that she was glad both her grandchildren had been baptized prior to my family joining the First Unitarian Church of Dallas. And so I listened to the sermon on baptism, curious as to how exactly Unitarians had been depicted. I have to admit I may have also listened in hopes of looking for more ammunition to better fight the war my mother was waging on our living tradition.

In Garrison Keillor’s weekly “News from Lake Wobegon” segment he told the story of one of the residents of Lake Wobegon who had moved to St. Paul and joined a Unitarian church. Her Lutheran sister visited for the “Affirmation of Personhood” ceremony for her niece and nephew and was left wanting more. I have never heard of an “Affirmation of Personhood” ceremony, and wonder if this was editorializing from Keillor or from my mother’s priest. I am assuming the target of the sermonizing priest is the child dedication ceremony that many of our churches celebrate. After establishing the “wrongness” of the Unitarian practice, the priest launched into the theme of his sermon: “the more” of Christian baptism. The priest identified “the more” as a sort of “holy watermark” that can be seen by God. Once baptized, the Christian has the name of Jesus on their side. Like Jesus at his baptism, the Christian is named beloved by God and in this naming the Christian is protected from evil. This is a direct quote from the sermon: “No harm can ever come to you again because of the power and victory of the love that Jesus shares with us in his name.” I find this remark to be completely astonishing.

I am appalled and angered that anyone in authority could suggest that a ritual has the power to protect anyone, much less a young child, from the “power of evil.” I am proud to be a part of a faith tradition that offers no such guarantees. I am also proud to be a part of a faith tradition that honors its Christian roots, just as I honor my own roots growing up in the Episcopalian Church. I am not Unitarian Universalist because I rejected my childhood faith based on the dogmatic interpretation of one priest. I am a Unitarian Universalist because our tradition of the free pulpit and the free pew allows me to interpret scripture, ritual and religion in an ongoing dialogue with my faith community.

I have come to learn that as Unitarian Universalists we welcome children into a church community acknowledging the fact that life sometimes brings harm and pain, both good and evil. We do not need a magical rite to protect us from harm; we need each other to find our way back to wholeness when harm has been done. We do also need something outside ourselves; we need the sort of grace that is available to all whether baptized or not. I am grateful each day for the opportunity to surrender to a faith that does not exclude someone from God’s love because they do not carry a certain “mark,” received in an exclusive ritual.

Why am I a Unitarian Universalist? The answer to this question changes as my faith matures and my journey takes me to new places. I first became a UU more than thirteen years ago so my children had a place to learn how to describe “transcending mystery and wonder” in their own words. While I was in seminary the answer to that question changed as I placed myself in a tug-of-war between my childhood faith and what I perceived as an overabundance of the rational in my chosen faith.

Frustrated with the endless reading in seminary I once complained to my minister that I wasn’t called to the ministry for the constant intellectual barrage but that “I just wanted to sit with people and hold their hand while they died.” I meant this in a larger sense, of course. I didn’t expect my entire ministry to be about sitting with people who were dying. I did think that ministry would be more about being with people than about being with mountains of books, however. It was with a sense of irony, then, that I read yet another required theological text the week I sat with my mother who was dying at her home in Houston.

Reason and Reverence by William R. Murry is one of the books I was required to read prior to my interview with the Ministerial Fellowship Committee on my way to becoming a fellowshipped minister. The book is Murry’s attempt to reconcile the humanist and the theist traditions in Unitarian Universalism. He integrates religious humanism with religious naturalism, which, in his words, “results in a greater spiritual depth and a language of reverence, both of which many find missing in traditional religious humanism” (from the introduction to the book).

I have been called a gentle theist, a deist and a panentheist by colleagues and professors who have sought to define my theology. If naming God as the ongoing act of creation itself identifies me as a theist, then so be it. You could also label me a sort of humanist if you want to; I start every theological inquiry with human experience not with a desire to discover a supernatural God who intervenes in human history. I do know this about myself: I do not want to give up on a language of reverence that points to a larger reality. Murry’s thesis intrigued me, but what comfort could it give me in the face of the stinging loss that I was experiencing?

Two days before my mother died, her Episcopalian priest and a member of their congregation brought communion to the house. I received communion with my mother and then participated in her anointing, a ritual similar to the Roman Catholic last rites. Although I disagree with most of the theology contained in these rituals, it was a deep honor to witness the comfort these rituals gave my mother.

Religious humanism rejects ritual and ceremony. It has also been rightly accused of relying on intellectual rationalism that seems cold and unfeeling. Murry’s humanistic religious naturalism acknowledges the role of emotions in determining how we live. Head and heart are balanced in his approach. Reverence is achieved not by worshipping something outside of ourselves, but by experiencing life in such a way as to become more fully human.

Watching my mother receive communion, be anointed with oil and repeat the Lord’s Prayer, I wondered what sort of ritual I would want when I died. And then the priest and his parishioner said good-bye to my mother. They told her they loved her and she told them she loved them in return. “That’s the sort of ritual I would want,” I thought.

Two days later my two sisters and I watched my mother die. She breathed us into this world. We were with her when she breathed herself out. “Ritual enough for me,” I thought at the time. Looking back on these events almost three years later, I begin to wonder if it is ritual enough for me. Now that I am a full-time Unitarian Universalist minister, I wonder if it is enough for us to be surrounded by those we love when we die. Or do we need some acknowledgement of our chosen faith, some acknowledgment of the comfort we get from our own religion?

It has been said that few will ask for the seven principles to be read while they are on their deathbed. If there is no proscribed ritual to be performed by an ordained elite, then what marks our final passages as Unitarian Universalists? Is it comfort enough to know we have lived well? Is it comfort enough to know that our religion, in some sense, becomes final as we are drawing our very last breath?

When the novelist D.H. Lawrence was subjected to the sermons of his mother’s pastor he wrote, “A person has no religion who has not slowly and painfully gathered one together, adding to it, shaping it; and one’s religion is never complete and final, it seems, but must always be undergoing modification.” Unitarian Universalist theologian Forrest Church, who defined religion as our human response to the dual reality of being alive and having to die, points out that Lawrence’s response to his mother’s pastor underlines our unique approach to religion. Rather than a “subscription to some fixed combination of doctrine and practice,” Unitarian Universalism offers a “gradual process of awakening to the depths and possibilities of life itself.”

When my parents remodeled their home after the last of us left the nest, they took over what used to be my bedroom on the second floor and turned it into a study. One wall was covered from about waist-level up with cork board so they could post maps with pins in them to indicate all of the places they had traveled. The cork board was also good for posting photos and certificates of accomplishment like my mother’s visit to Machu Picchu. A few weeks before my mother died, I stood in what was once my bedroom, staring at the wall of my mother’s travels. Posted next to the certificate indicating she saw the “Big Five” in Africa was another certificate, a proclamation:

“By this attestation be it known that Jeanne Brandis by virtue of fulfilling the biblical injunction has ascended to Jerusalem the holy city, capital of Israel, and is henceforth authorized to bear the title of Jerusalem pilgrim.” My eye was drawn to that particular certificate because I had just returned from my own Unitarian Universalist “pilgrimage” to Boston.

I went to Boston in my final year of seminary expecting some great illumination from this living tradition to brighten my path. The pilgrimage became about something else of course, because that’s what true pilgrimages do. We set out expecting to find all that is holy from a place and discover that trips are just trips, pilgrimages happen when we least expect them to. The more important pilgrimage may have come while I was standing in the room where I lived as a teenager, remembering what the room used to look like, remembering who I was when I lived in that room, remembering that nothing in this life is permanent. Standing in that room not knowing that the most important pilgrimage was about to happen in a few weeks: it would happen while I was reading a book on religious humanism with my mother dying in the next room.

“By this attestation be it known that Lora Brandis by virtue of fulfilling Life’s injunction has ascended to the holy city, capital of the soul, and is henceforth authorized to bear the title of pilgrim.”* But wait, there’s an asterisk on my certificate and some fine print to be read.

*May it also be known that the recipient of this certificate agrees that by bearing the title pilgrim that said recipient acknowledges that in no way is bearer done with all pilgrimages. In fact the holy city the pilgrim visited may be subject to moving, re-defining or disappearing altogether. As such, the bearer of this certificate agrees to hold harmless all parties involved in the pilgrimage, including the self and any or all parties previously identified as God.

I am a Unitarian Universalist because I am a pilgrim and Unitarian Universalists are a pilgrim people. It is my honor to journey with you.
