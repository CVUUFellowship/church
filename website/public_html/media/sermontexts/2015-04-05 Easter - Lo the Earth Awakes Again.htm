Lo, the Earth Awakes Again

Rev. Anne Felton Hines

April 5, 2015

 

Reflections on Passover

          “When the spirit struck us free we could scarcely believe it…” writes the activist priest Daniel Berrigan, in his re-write of Psalm 126. I imagine this was felt by the ancient Israelites as they found themselves free from the bondage of the Pharoah of Egypt. Their mouths must have been “filled with laughter,” and their “tongues with pure joy.”

          The story of the exodus from Egypt is re-told every year at Passover by Jews around the world, be they religious or secular. This year it began at sundown Friday, and will end at sundown April 11th.

          Each year, we are reminded of the terrible oppression that afflicted the ancient Israelites – how they were forced into slavery, and how even when Moses went to Pharoah and begged him to free the people, the Pharoah refused.

          And each year, we are reminded of the miracles their God provided for them – the ten plagues on the land of Egypt to try to convince the Pharoah to free the Israelites, the “passing over” the Israelites’ homes to save them from the final plague, and the parting of the Red Sea to let them escape the Pharoah’s army.

          In the Seder dinners I’ve attended in the past, the story doesn’t end with the liberation of the ancient Israelites. Instead, we are asked to remember others in more recent times who have faced oppression, and have struggled to liberate themselves.

          And we are invited to remember those individual men and women who, like Moses, answered the call to lead their people to freedom: Harriet Tubman, Susan B. Anthony, Martin Luther King, Jr., Rosa Parks, Cesar Chavez, and so many more who worked for freedom behind the scenes, in small, quiet ways, but without whom those more famous leaders could not have succeeded. It reminds us of the power that one individual can have, and invites us to consider ourselves as holding such possibilities.

          And finally, as we remember the story of Passover, and of all the subsequent liberation movements right up to our own time, let us not forget the role that religious faith has played. The Israelites were a community of faith; and it was that faith that allowed them to move forward in the face of great challenge and fear.

          It has been people of faith who have often challenged the purveyors of hate and fear through history. And still today we find individuals and congregations, in all faiths – including Unitarian Universalist – who take up the struggles for justice and liberation.

          Passover celebrates the courage of the ancient Israelites in their journey toward freedom. But it also celebrates the courage of freedom fighters throughout history, and to make their struggle our own.

Reflections on Easter

          For many the story of Easter is, like that of Passover, a story of liberation – only in this story, it’s not the liberation of a whole community of people from oppression, but the liberation of new life from the tomb of death. And as our Responsive Reading suggests, it is we who are invited by this story to roll away the stone from our soul, and move from the grief and sorrow of Good Friday, into the joy and possibilities of Easter.

          My second year of Seminary up in Berkeley was the hardest one for me. It wasn’t the academics that posed the challenge, but rather things going on in the world.

          It was the fall of 1978, and a month or so after classes began, an airplane crashed over San Diego, killing all on board; it was the same airline I often flew between Oakland and L.A.

That was followed a short time later by the mass suicide at Jonestown – a tragedy that still today causes a visceral reaction in me.

And less than ten days after that event, Harvey Milk and George Moscone were assassinated across the bay.

          And for some reason, I decided in the spring quarter to take a class on the Holocaust, which was – as you might imagine – the most depressing class I’ve ever experienced. I was wrapped in sadness, unable to remove from my head the images we were seeing in movies, and reading in the works of Elie Wiesel and others.

          The final blow was learning that my very best friend was now romantically involved with my ex-husband; I felt, rationally or not, completely betrayed.

          As the end of the school year approached, I found myself unable to see joy in much of anything; the laughter that ordinarily brought me such comfort eluded me; the hope I could so often find seemed a farce.

          Then one day my advisor, Ron Cook, sat me down in his office and said, “Hines,” – that’s what he always called me – “you need to find some way to move from Good Friday to Easter.” I’d never thought of myself as being stuck in a Good Friday frame of mind, but I realized in that moment that I had been choosing to remain inside the tomb of my grief, rather than allowing myself the strength and courage to move aside the stone that had been locking me in all those months.

          Joan Chittister, a writer and activist for peace and justice, suggests that, “Our struggles begin at those junctures of life where the past disappears and the future seems unclear or unacceptable; there is irreparable interruption;” and we may feel defeated and hopeless.

          Removing the stone that has blocked out the light means we must engage the struggle that has confronted us. We never heal our sorrow or despair by ignoring it’s power, or by rushing the process.

          But the purpose of engaging our struggle – whatever it happens to be – isn’t to remain there with it, but to allow it to teach us and transform us. “If we decide to endure to the end,” writes Chittister, “we come out of it changed by the doing of it. We dare the development of the Self.”

          The story of Good Friday and Easter is one of death and resurrection – not necessarily of the body, but rather the soul. And it assures us that no matter how bleak our world may appear, that courage and love will be our companions – sometimes in the form of people who love us, sometimes in the form of our inner wisdom, and sometimes in the form of whatever it is that we might call God. But that courage and love is always within our reach, rising like the first signs of Spring.

The Hope of Spring

          A happier memory of mine from Seminary (and there are many) is the adaptation several of us created one year of the ancient story of Demeter and her beautiful daughter, Persephone, and of course the wretched Hades, god of the Underworld! (I got to play the part of Persephone, which, as I recall, simply involved a lot of screaming!)

          I suspect that most of you are familiar with the story. Demeter and Persephone are living a life of bliss, when one day Hades captures Persephone and drags her off to the Underworld. Demeter is so distraught as she searches for her daughter, that she causes everything on earth to stop growing.

Finally the god Hermes descends into hell and convinces Hades to let Persephone return to earth. But Hades tricks Persephone into still having to return to him for a part of every year; hence the seasons of Winter – when Demeter’s grief prevents any growth, and Spring – when her joy at Persephone’s return causes the dormant seeds to burst open again with new life.

It is no coincidence that both Passover and Easter occur near the onset of Spring, for they both speak of new life, of new possibilities, of freedom and wholeness and transformation.

          And it is no surprise that so many Unitarian Universalist churches in America choose this time of year to honor one of our Unitarian “saints” – Norbert Capek, a Unitarian minister in Czechoslovakia who was an outspoken critic of Hitler; he was also a well-loved pastor to his people.

          The story I learned is that on his birthday one year, his parishioners surprised him by bringing flowers to adorn the Chancel – just as you have done this morning. He in turn surprised them by blessing the flowers, and then inviting everyone forward to take a flower home with them.

          In 1941 Rev. Capek was arrested by the Gestapo and thrown into Dachau, where he was tortured and, in 1942, put to death. But the story of his courage and his love of justice could never die. It is told year after year, as the Flower Communion he began is re-enacted in our churches in memory of him. He reminds us of the power of love, and of the hope that we hold out for the future every time we speak for justice, and act in faith.

          When we are wrestling with hopelessness over the state of our country or the world, we need the model of the Israelites and all the other justice-seekers since to give us courage.

          When we are wrestling with sorrow or despair, we need the image of the empty tomb to remind us that we can face our life struggles and in so doing, find new life.

Joan Chittister writes: “Proofs of eternal rebirth are everywhere: Spring comes every year. Dawn comes every morning. Love happens out of hate. Birth absorbs the pain of death….

          “We hope,” she writes, “because we have no reason not to hope. We sit by the window and wait for one more dawn, though there’s not an ounce of proof in the night’s black, black sky that it can come.”

          And yet we know in our bones that it will. May we always remember that. Amen.
