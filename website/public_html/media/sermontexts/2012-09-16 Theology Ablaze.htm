Theology Ablaze

©Rev. Lora Brandis

Preached September 16, 2012

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Congregation

 

We begin with a lump in our throats and a fire in our souls. We begin, not by closing our hearts and minds, but by opening all of the senses of our beings. We begin walking together, reflecting on words tossed into the fire of truth. We begin digging deeper this time, knowing there’s a fire just waiting for fuel.

We begin this church year together, united by a common theological exploration. Today while we are sifting through the ashes of words like theology and religion, our youngest children are singing along to “This Little Light of Mine” and reading along with the children’s book “Unitarian Universalism is a Really Long Name.” Our older children are reflecting on what it means to develop a theology as young religious Unitarian Universalists. All of our children are walking to their classes this morning down a hallway decorated with chalices ablaze with multi-colored flames. It takes more than a single flame to start our year together. It takes all of us, from the musicians to the volunteer teachers, from the ushers to the kitchen helpers, from our youngest babies playing in child space to our wisest elders listening through the headsets to our amplified worship experience. Ours is a religion that asks us to take personal responsibility for forming our own theology. Yet, we cannot do it alone. Our own light grows stronger in the company of others. We burn brighter in community. Our theology becomes more articulate when we gather together, giving us the practical tools to find the courage to cause our lives to flame.

Many of us, like the Rev. Dr. William Schulz, “call [our] feelings about theology ‘ambivalent,’ and [hopefully] not irredeemably hostile” (forward to Theology Ablaze). We begin with a lump in our throats and a fire in our souls. We begin with the word theology. Schulz reminds us that “theological questions … begin as, Robert Frost said poems do, with a lump in the throat, not an algebraic logarithm.”

The Rev. Tom Owen-Towle, the minister emeritus of the First Unitarian Universalist Church of San Diego, wrote the book Theology Ablaze last year in celebration of the 50th anniversary year of Unitarian Universalism. He also wrote it for a maturing faith, one that at age 50 could handle the challenge of naming a Unitarian Universalist theology, a religion that has moved beyond the necessity of consolidation into one that can embrace a depth theology. In his book he provides 26 theological themes defined through a Unitarian Universalist lens. Is there a unified Unitarian Universalist theology one might ask, or would it be better to say theologies? This is one of many questions we’ll ask together this year in worship, in religious education and even with a workshop taught by Rev. Owen-Towle in April. We will throw words into the fire, burning away the dross of accumulated misinterpretation and we will experience them anew.

First up is theology, first defined for us in 1963, two years after the merger, in a document titled The Free Church in a Changing World. The authors, a study commission “comprised of religious professionals, academics, writers and artists, nonprofit administrators, and others…define[s] ‘theology as a critical and creative intellectual attempt to express, clarify, defend, reconstruct religion’…adding that ‘religion is related to theology as practice is to theory…and we should never mistake theology for religion, or assume that the concept of theology can adequately substitute for the breadth and depth of religion.’” (Theology Ablaze, p.6) Theology is formed in a dark night of the soul; religion is what has been pieced together over time by scholars, professional religious and lay people alike each adding to the rich tapestry of the human response to the duality of being born and having to die.

In 2005 the definition of theology was updated by a new commission, this time working for four years to create a report called Engaging our Theological Diversity.

While the root words of the term theology refer to “reason or discourse about God,” these questions assume a much broader understanding of its meaning. In modern usage, the definition of theology is understood to include the full range of religious and philosophical beliefs (not just theistic ones) and our human understanding of the meaning and purpose of life and of Ultimate Reality.

 

In this most recent definition one can almost hear our maturity as a faith blazing forth. We acknowledge the root of the term in God language and celebrate the postmodern tendency to re-imagine and re-invent language. A mature faith is like a mature human being: no longer needing to define one’s identity by what one has rejected from a parent, the mature adult embraces what is best from its parents and bases its identity on lived experience rather than inherited traits. As a mature religious movement, Unitarian Universalism can use the word theology acknowledging its roots in God language while embracing the freedom that liberal religion offers to reinterpret past truths once rigidly defined.

This maturity was acknowledged by the past president of the UUA in his well-known “language of reverence” sermon when he said,

I believe that Unitarian Universalism is growing up. Growing out of a cranky and contentious adolescence into a more confident maturity. A maturity in which we can not only claim our Good News, the values we have found in this free faith, but also begin to offer that Good News to the world outside these beautiful sanctuary walls. There is a new willingness on our part to come in from the margins. (Quoted in Embracing our Theological Diversity, p. 4)

 

As we come in from the margins and embrace our theological diversity we are learning a common language. This is what the book Theology Ablaze offers us and this is why we have chosen it for our theme this year. In a world where people are dying because of words and images used to attack religious beliefs, we can summon the courage to throw old, dangerous words into the fire and see what new meanings emerge.

Words like gratitude, beauty, interdependence, peace and nonviolence are easy enough. There are harder, more dangerous words. We have all heard the joke about why UUs do not sing out in church – they are busy reading ahead in the hymnal to see if they agree with the text. Words like covenant, sacred, evil and God are dangerous words for many Unitarian Universalists. And they can be dangerous words for fundamentalists on either end of the scale.

This is the premise of another book we’ll study together this year by Gary Eberle. Dangerous Words: Talking about God in an Age of Fundamentalism invites us to “move beyond religions (plural) and understand how the religious impulse, shared by all our species, finds its rich expression in the many vocabularies we use to name our gods. The greatness of God transcends all names. Most traditions affirm this, so now we must decide either to find a common language and speak with each other or to continue our war of words and live in a post-Babel world of confusion” (p. xvii). If you just heard a very theistic interpretation of the word god in Gary Eberle’s words then you are probably not alone. Together we’ll throw that word into the fire twice, once with a sermon from me and then again with Tom Owen-Towle who will not only offer a workshop April 13th but will preach April 14th. Justine and I have purposefully designed our thematic ministry to wrestle longer with words that are our most worthy opponents. We will sit by the fire with words like God, covenant, Sabbath, death, sacred, love, grace and even gratitude more than once. Words like God are theologically divisive. Words like gratitude seem easier and that’s why they are so often taken for granted. Living with gratitude challenges us to let go of perfection and in Rumi’s words to “be grateful for whoever comes.”  “A joy, a depression, a meanness, some momentary awareness comes [A]s an unexpected visitor. Welcome and entertain them all!” A theology ablaze is a deep theology. A deep theology knows to be grateful for whoever or whatever comes.

A deep theology takes us back to that corner in Manhattan described by singer / songwriter Ani DiFranco in the song “fuel.” Digging deeper “beneath the bedrock of the mystery” and “beneath everything i can think of to think about… there's a fire that's just waiting for fuel.” The fire is the divine spark in each of us. The fuel is our vocabulary of faith.

Faith is on the list and so are embodiment, suffering, silence, prayer, justice, salvation, joy, mystery and wonder, self-care, forgiveness and reconciliation, hospitality, hope and evolution. All of these words constitute the Unitarian Universalist vocabulary of faith, words that define our theology. All of these words lift us out of the impulse to numb ourselves to a cross-marketed, information-saturated world. All of these words take us deeper into the religious impulse, shared by our entire species. It is important to remember that this is not just a conversation, this is a spiritual discipline. In the forward to Jeanne Harrison Nieuwejaar’s Fluent in Faith William Sinkford writes that his call to a “language of reverence” “was a beginning, not an end point. We are people of the word, it is true,” he writes. “But religious life is practiced, not just spoken.” We are all invited into a “’Practice of Reverence,’ not merely a conversation about religious language” (Fluent in Faith, location 107).

What does this practice look like? It looks like what we often name as the marks of our religious community: participating in worship, attending congregational meetings and exercising our right to vote, cultivating generosity, contributing time and talent, and becoming intentional about our own spiritual growth. This practice, this spiritual practice, also looks like taking a religious education class, attending community forum and participating in a social justice outreach event.

It is interesting to note that the word spiritual is not on Tom Owen-Towle’s list. It does show up in one of my planned sermon titles and is implied by most of the words. You can hear it kindling the theology of gratitude, Sabbath, grace, embodiment, sacred, silence, love and prayer. It is our third principle: Acceptance of one another and encouragement to spiritual growth in our congregations. Tom Owen-Towle doesn’t put the word spiritual on the list of our core theological themes because it is not a theme; it is how we are called to be when we engage our theological diversity. In fact, the word spiritual shows up on almost every page of the 2005 document Engaging Our Theological Diversity. Spirituality always points us to something larger than ourselves. If theology is to religion as theory is to practice, then spirituality is what moves us beyond theology as conversation into theology as transformation. Spirituality takes us deeper “beneath the bedrock of mystery.” Spirituality takes us through the fires of our lives, causing us to burn like the burning bush from the Hebrew Scriptures. Burning with a theology ablaze, we are more fully alive and, like the burning bush, we are not consumed.

We begin with a lump in our throats and a fire in our souls. We begin, not by closing our hearts and minds, but by opening all of the senses of our beings. We begin walking together, reflecting on words tossed into the fire of truth. We begin digging deeper this time, knowing there’s a fire just waiting for fuel. The fire is the divine spark in each of us. The fuel is our vocabulary of faith.

Eckhart Tolle, the author of The Power of Now, employs a striking image for those of us seeking to find the courage to cause our lives to flame with a theology ablaze with meaning and depth. “When a log that has only just started to burn is placed next to one that is burning fiercely,” he writes “and after a while they are separated again, the first log will be burning with much greater intensity. After all it is the same fire. To be such a fire is one of the functions of a spiritual teacher,” he concludes. (p. 34). To be such a fire is one of the functions of our gathered community. We are each sitting next to a divine fire burning with great intensity. Together we burn brighter. Together we are ablaze.
