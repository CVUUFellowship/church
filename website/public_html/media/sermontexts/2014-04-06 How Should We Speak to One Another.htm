How Should We Speak to One Another?

© Rev. Lora Brandis

Preached at Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship
April 6, 2014

 

The Sufi poet Rumi wrote, "Out beyond ideas of wrongdoing and right-doing, there is a field. I’ll meet you there. When the soul lies down in that grass, the world is too full to talk about. Ideas, language, even the phrase each other doesn’t make any sense." How should we speak to one another? We should speak to each other as if there is a field that exists that is beyond right and wrong. We should speak to each other as if language can carry us to a place where separation ceases and even the phrase each other doesn’t make any sense. We should speak to one another as if there is nothing to win and there is much to gain.

“How Should We Speak to One Another” is the eighth step from Karen Armstrong’s Twelve Steps to a Compassionate Life. It is a question rather than a statement. It is a query rather than a rule. Our Unitarian Universalist faith is guided by a set of ethical principles sustained by wisdom found in a variety of sources that religious people throughout the ages have turned to for practical steps in order to live with integrity. As members of this Fellowship we covenant to speak honestly and directly to someone with whom we disagree or have a concern. We assume good intentions and use compassionate language in our exchanges with each other. Sometimes we fall short, knowing that we can be called back into covenant to continue to learn how we should and can speak to one another.

Should – that’s a word that will stop a conversation.  “Should” is our controlling partner in the “good enough” dance, demanding we should be grateful to be alive, we should be compassionate, we should be a better person, among other “shoulds” we do to ourselves and others. There is no one right way to speak to one another. The eighth step to a compassionate life is a question.  “Live the questions now,” the poet Rilke reminds us. We are always living our way into the answers. How should we speak to one another? In a church, shouldn’t we at least be nice to each other?

That was the naïve assumption I made as an intern minister. Since that time I have learned that it is better to be kind than to be nice. Nice implies a smile painted over a seething emotion waiting just below the surface. Kind implies awareness – a smile learned from deep acknowledgment of what serves the whole.

While I was an intern minister I attempted to attend as many committee meetings as possible, learning not only how the congregation ministered to each other and to the world at large, but also how the congregation functioned as an emotional system. One evening I attended a committee meeting that was a potluck at someone’s home. We gathered and ate for about the first thirty minutes, holding our plates in our hands mingling with each other until we were called to sit and begin the meeting. I had met all but one of the committee members in attendance prior to the meeting. Several of the committee members were fairly new (within the last year) to the church. Two of those were not new to Unitarian Universalism. All were not new to church life, or to attending some sort of a religious community. So what followed may not have surprised them as much as it did me.

There was discussion about a particular project that was not supported by the board and the director of religious education as much as the project chair had hoped it would be. It was the benign sort of complaining that we have all engaged in from time to time while being the messy and imperfect beloved community that we are. And then the conversation took a turn that I had not anticipated. It was dominated by two people who decided to use part of the agenda to bad- mouth someone in the congregation. One even joked about her negative opinion about the woman in question and made mock catfight noises. She laughed and concluded that the woman had much less musical talent than she, even though she was sure that that woman thought otherwise. I sat in utter astonishment, with my mouth hanging open probably, unable to call the conversation back into covenant. How should we speak to one another? I thought we covenanted with one another to affirm the worth and dignity of every person and to affirm and promote the interdependent web of existence. I missed the part where we covenant to hijack a committee agenda to gossip about the people we don’t like.

The opposite of niceness is not meanness. Niceness fosters a politeness on Sunday mornings that gives way to true feelings on Saturday evenings. Unless we acknowledge the anxiety that lurks behind niceness and the pain that is hidden behind gossip, we will continue to make the same naïve assumptions about religious institutions that people have been making for centuries. Instead of James Luther Adams’ proclamation that “church is a place where we get to practice what it means to be human” we will declare that church is a place where we get to stay in our old habits and be nice to each other because it’s too hard to grow and change. The opposite of niceness is awareness.

Family systems expert Edwin Friedman put it this way:

Actually religious institutions are the worst offenders at encouraging immaturity and irresponsibility. In church after church, some member is passively-aggressively holding the whole system hostage, and no one wants to fire him or force her to leave because it wouldn’t be “the Christian thing to do.” It has nothing to do with Christianity. Synagogues also tolerate abusers because it wouldn’t be the Christian thing to do.

 

How should we speak to one another? How should I have spoken that night at the meeting so many years ago? At the time I was naïve enough to think that if the senior minister had been present that perhaps the discussion wouldn’t have turned so “gossipy” and negative. I was just an intern; I didn’t have any authority. I couldn’t imagine that some of the comments that were made would have been made in front of the senior minister. Now that I am a minister I know this isn’t true. We are human. We can forgive easily the petty sins of our ill-spoken moments. I have forgiven the two people who turned the conversation so negative that night. I watched them both serve the congregation with drive and passion. I also know how one credits the church with saving her daughter’s life. I have forgiven them. I have not let myself off the hook so easily. When do I not use right speech? When do I complain instead of take responsibility? When do I allow my petty feelings to get in the way of constructive work?

How should we speak to one another? Composer Stephen Sondheim advises: “Careful the things you say. Children will listen.  Careful the spell you cast. Not just on children. Careful the tale you tell. That is the spell.”

Karen Armstrong writes that, “In our highly contentious world, we need to develop a twenty-first-century form of Socrates’ compassionate discourse” (p. 135). Buddhist Joseph Goldstein says that “Although there is great elaboration of right speech in the texts, it all condenses into two general principles: Is it true? Is it useful?” Driven by a preoccupation with what disconnects some of us from our compassionate nature and what allows some people to stay connected to their compassionate nature, psychologist Marshall Rosenberg created Nonviolent Communication (NVC). NVC is based on the simple principle that “When we focus on clarifying what is being observed, felt, and needed rather than on diagnosing and judging, we discover the depth of our own compassion” (location 364).

It wasn’t that the toxic speech I experienced at the meeting was damaging for the community as a whole – it was, but that’s only partially the point – it was that the negative speech reflected an inner woundedness that kept the speakers from wholeness. This is the concern of the church. This is the pastoral concern of the minister: what pain are you hiding behind that nice smile of yours? What need isn’t being met?

Marshall Rosenberg identifies the toxic speech I witnessed that evening as life-alienating communication. “Our attention is focused on classifying, analyzing, and determining levels of wrongness rather than on what we and others need and are not getting,” he writes. Maybe if I had observed what was going on rather than sitting in stunned silence judging the behavior, I could have reminded the group of the congregation’s covenant to create a welcoming space for all to speak their truth respectfully. I could have reminded us all that when we enter into the covenantal community, we enter into a willingness to be held accountable whether we are meeting in a social setting or inside the church walls.

Whether we practice a twenty-first-century form of Socrates’ compassionate discourse, the Buddha’s right speech or Nonviolent Communication we can all learn to stop in mid-sentence, if necessary, and listen to the tales we are telling and how we are telling them. Marshall Rosenberg also writes that “the more people are trained to think in terms of moralistic judgments that imply wrongness and badness, the more they are being trained to look outside themselves – to outside authorities – for the definition of what constitutes right, wrong, good, and bad” (location 663).

There is a story from Genesis that tells of a time when the whole earth was of one language and of one speech. All of the people gathered together and said, “Let us build a city and a tower that reaches into the heavens.” Afraid that they might be scattered throughout the land and someday be forgotten, they wanted to make a name for themselves. Their god came down from the heavens to see the city and tower that the mortals had built.

Their god said, “Look they are one people and they all have one language. They have begun to build a tower all of the way into the heavens. They will be able to do whatever it is they imagine they can do.” Maybe their god was afraid of their arrogance or maybe their god didn’t realize how powerful his creation could be. No matter the reason, god decided to come down from heaven and confound the people’s language so that they could no longer understand one another’s speech. God scattered the people throughout the earth and they stopped building the magnificent city with the tower that reached outward towards the heavens. “Therefore it was called Babel, because there the Lord confused the language of all the earth; and from there the Lord scattered them abroad over the face of all the earth” (Genesis 11:1-9). Like the Garden of Eden story, the tower of Babel is a folktale of human pride and folly. How dare the creation try to be like the creator? How arrogant! “How in need of punishment the foolish and prideful human is,” the judging parent god proclaims.

There is another way to understand this folktale. What if the “sin” at the tower of Babel was not pride? What if humankind’s ongoing mistake is to build an external monument to an internal truth? “The more people are trained to think in terms of moralistic judgments that imply wrongness and badness, the more they are being trained to look outside themselves – to outside authorities – for the definition of what constitutes right, wrong, good, and bad.” Unitarian Universalists have a long history of rejecting external monuments, of rejecting the idea of worshipping the finger-pointing symbol instead of the eternal truth to which the symbol points. We are no longer accountable to the old punishing gods; we choose now to be accountable to each other.

How should we speak to one another?  We should (and can) speak to each other as if there is a field that exists that is beyond right and wrong. We should (and can) speak to each other claiming our feelings and recognizing our needs. We can speak to each other consciously, clearly, directly and in covenant. With compassion and gratitude, we seek not to win the argument but to gain wholeness for ourselves and the entire human family.
