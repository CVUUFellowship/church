Becoming Whole

© By Rev. Lora Brandis

Preached June 2, 2013

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship

 

Evangelist Rob Bell likes to tell the story about an art show that his mega-church held and the paintings and sculptures that people brought in to put on display. There was one piece that drew a lot of attention. It had a beautiful quote from Gandhi included in its composition. Many found the piece compelling, but one observer was prompted to attach a handwritten note that read, “Reality check – he’s in hell.” “Gandhi’s in hell? Really?” asks Bell. “He is, and someone knows this for sure?” This question sent Bell on a journey that got him in trouble with conservative evangelicals, put him on the cover of Time Magazine as one of the world’s most 100 influential people, and ultimately led to his departure from the 11,000 member congregation he founded.

In his book Love Wins: A Book About Heaven, Hell And The Fate Of Every Person Who Ever Lived, Pastor Bell had the audacity to proclaim: “At the center of the Christian tradition since the first church have been a number who insist that history is not tragic, hell is not forever, and love, in the end, wins and all will be reconciled to God.”

Carlton Pearson was once the pastor of one of the largest churches in Tulsa, Oklahoma. In the 1990s the average attendance was more than 6,000 each Sunday. You can hear Pearson’s brand of Universalist theology in the titles of two of his books: The Gospel of Inclusion: Reaching Beyond Religious Fundamentalism to the True Love of God and God Is Not a Christian, Nor a Jew, Muslim, Hindu…. Pearson was declared a heretic by his peers in the Joint College of African-American Pentecostal Bishops and his church closed. The few followers that were left eventually followed Pearson to All Souls Unitarian Church in Tulsa. After he left Tulsa to pastor a New Thought congregation in Chicago, those who had heard the gospel of universal reconciliation stayed on as members of All Souls.

Most recently Pope Francis said, "The Lord has redeemed all of us, all of us, with the Blood of Christ: all of us, not just Catholics. Everyone! ‘Father, the atheists?’ Even the atheists. Everyone!" We must meet one another doing good. ‘But I don’t believe, Father, I am an atheist!’ But do good: we will meet one another there.” Several days later a Vatican spokesman clarified the Catholic Church’s position by reassuring everyone that atheists are still going to hell. Belief in God is still needed to get into heaven, but there is hope. According to the Vatican spokesman, “every man or woman, whatever their situation, can be saved. Even non-Christians can respond to this saving action of the Spirit. No person is excluded from salvation simply because of so-called original sin.” (both quotes from www.huffingtonpost.com) So-called original sin….

Most conversations about salvation begin with sin. Unitarian Universalists have a bad reputation when it comes to sin. We see the world through rose-colored glasses, we are too “Pollyannaish,” TOO hopeful. Long ago our religious ancestors rejected the doctrine of original sin, embraced various forms of Christian universalism and when the principles and purposes of our consolidated movement were crafted we affirmed the inherent worth and dignity of every person. This is one of our most difficult principles from which to live. When we take our rose-colored glasses off and look around at the suffering in our world caused by those supposedly worthy and dignified human beings, we too wonder if there should be a hell reserved for our world’s worst offenders. Love wins is a hard message to sell.

Universalism came to this continent from England just as our country’s revolution was brewing. It comes into our heritage through a broken line of early Christian Church Fathers such as Clement of Alexandria and Origen. It is a broken line because many of these teachings were considered heretical; many of these teachings were lost in the fires of inquisition and intolerance. It is also a broken line because it comes out of a story that starts with an apple, a fall from grace and a picture of totally deprived humanity that can only be reconciled to God by an act of God. The doctrine of Universal Salvation gets complicated. This is why the simple message “Love Wins” makes so many people uncomfortable.

The simple message “Love Wins” also makes a lot of people comfortable – comforting them in their struggle for self-worth while they listen to a story about how sinners and unbelievers are punished for all eternity. The story used to be worse; it actually included an alternate ending that not only said that some would get to heaven but this alternate theology also said it didn’t matter what you believed or even how you behaved. Some were predestined; some were elected by God. Some were in and some were out and only God knew who.

By the late 1700s there were at least seventeen Universalist ministers preaching the good news in America that all would be saved, not could be, but would be. This variation of Universal Salvation, one that Rob Bell seems to promote in his book Love Wins, doesn’t mean that all are going to heaven immediately upon death. There is a strain in Christian Universalism that accounts for some divine punishment, and that God’s reconciling love means that punishment doesn’t last forever. The Universalism we inherit from our religious forbears comes through the broken line of early Christian fathers by way of an American Universalism that was a grass roots movement of people reading the Bible for themselves and seeing in it a redemptive love made possible by the life and death of Jesus.

Unitarian Universalism now finds itself as a maturing faith at a time when, according to the Pew Forum on Religion and Public Life, 74% of Americans still believe in heaven but only 59% believe in hell. Do we just roll our eyes when the Vatican corrects the Pope’s “salvation by good works” theology? Do we applaud Rob Bell when he proclaims “Love Wins,” saying welcome to the club we’ve been waiting for you for two hundred years?! Can we do more than just invite survivors of hell-steeped evangelism into our Sunday morning worship? Well, of course we can.

We can when we open our doors wider than our fear of intimacy and wider than our own stubborn need to be right. When we declare that our spiritual but not dogmatic way of doing religion is the only way then we are just as guilty as those who say the only way to heaven is through the one, true church. When we honor our own theological heritage as Christian Universalists we can celebrate any movement in institutionalized religion towards more inclusiveness. We can be cautiously optimistic.

We also need to recognize that even as the Catholic Church shakes off some of the shackles of medieval theology and tries to respond to the modern world with a more inclusive message, Church leaders still stumble along the path of exclusivity. They still define their religion by drawing a circle around themselves, defining “non-believers” as outsiders. The Pope’s radical-sounding statement that “we must meet one another doing good,” even the atheists, isn’t that radical when you understand that he is still clinging to a story that requires the redeeming blood of a crucified savior for access to salvation.

There is a different story told about sin and salvation, one told by Christians since the beginning. Human beings do sin, not because of so-called original sin, which is rooted in rebellion against God. Human beings sin because we are human. It is a story told by many world religions: life is suffering.

This is Buddhism’s First Noble Truth - duhkha. The Sanskrit word duhkha is usually translated as suffering and can also be interpreted as stress, anxiety or dissatisfaction. It is much more than any of these, however. If we trace the etymology of the word, it leads us to a metaphor of the hub of a wheel not properly centered, so the wheel cannot properly function. Duhkha, then, is the recognition that the way we live is off-centered. We are not at ease in our humanness; we are dis-eased. This is similar to a broad Christian understanding of sin.

We are not at ease; we are not functioning the way we should. We accumulate this dis-ease during a lifetime of responding to pain inflicted by our parents, acts of physical and emotional violence and broken relationships of all sorts. We keep ourselves from our own healing by our very woundedness. We keep ourselves from hearing healing in other people’s stories because we cling to the way things are. People suffer because they are limited by their images of the world, by the stories they tell themselves. This is how we sin. We sin when we think happiness comes when we get what we want, when circumstances change from what they are now, when those we blame get “it”, when we get the right job, spouse, car…“fill in the blank.” We sin and we are in need of salvation.

A Unitarian Universalist doctrine of sin includes the personal brokenness of the human condition. It is also includes collective damage: war, poverty, environmental devastation, racial and gender oppression – it’s a long list. Unitarian Universalists name the process of becoming whole as salvation. Salvation, for most of us, is healing from the brokenness of the human condition. Salvation is how poet and UU minister Lynn Ungar describes it in her poem by the same name:

Do you believe me when I say

you are neither salvaged nor saved,

but salved, anointed by gentle hands

where you are most tender?

Haven’t you seen

the way snow curls down

like a fresh sheet, how it

covers everything, makes everything

beautiful, without exception?

Salvation is our mission statement: to nurture spiritually courageous people who transform the world through justice and compassion. Eckhart Tolle states that “true salvation is a state of freedom – from fear, from suffering, from a perceived state of lack and insufficiency and therefore from all wanting, needing, grasping, and clinging. It is freedom from compulsive thinking, from negativity, and above all from past and future as a psychological need” (The Power of Now, p. 122) “There is no only way to salvation, but there is only one point of access” (Tolle). That point of access is now. Salvation doesn’t come at the end of a life well-lived with acts of good works and / or by believing a certain way. Salvation comes in this life now. We make heaven here on earth and hell, if we choose that instead. And evangelist Rob Bell agrees. In fact, it’s in the Bible.

According to Bell (and a lot of other scholars), Jesus wasn’t telling people how to get to heaven. He was teaching people how to live now. In Matthew 19:16-22 the story is told of a rich young man who asks Jesus how to have eternal life. Jesus doesn’t answer with, “here is how you get to heaven.” He says, “If you wish to enter into life…” The story ends with the young, rich man leaving in disappointment. He refuses to sell all he has to enter into the “kingdom of heaven” or what Jesus has previously named as entering into life. “Heaven for Jesus wasn’t just ‘someday’,” Bell writes; “it was a present reality. Jesus blurs the lines, inviting the rich man, and us, into the merging of heaven and earth, the future and present, here and now.”

The rich young man isn’t able to enter fully into life because his possessions get in the way. For us it may be our clinging to the way things are, our refusal to write a new story, our compulsive thinking or our constant negativity that keep us from entering fully into life, that keep us from salvation, from becoming whole. Our definition of universal salvation has changed from a nineteenth century, mostly Christian, one. When we declare ourselves to be Unitarian Universalists we proclaim that all human beings are capable of redemption in this life now, of being saved right now, all are capable of becoming whole now. Every person. Even you. Yes you.
