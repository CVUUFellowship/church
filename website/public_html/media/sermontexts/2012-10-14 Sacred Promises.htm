Sacred Promises

©Rev. Lora Brandis

Preached October 14, 2012

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Congregation

 

In an online course I took with Unitarian Universalist historian and Minister Rev. Mark W. Harris we had weekly assignments to write on subjects ranging from the centrality of covenant in our faith to the practice and ethics of ministry. One week we were given a choice. In the week exploring rites of passage we were asked to submit a brief paper on our theology of death, explaining what we, as ministers, were trying to do in a memorial service. OR we could write about our theology of marriage. I chose death.

When David Wood won the right in last year’s annual service auction to assign the subject matter of one of my sermons, he did not give me the choice between death and marriage. He wanted me to preach about a theology of marriage from the perspective of our liberal faith. I still would prefer to preach about death, but a promise is a promise and a promise made in the context of our community is a promise worth keeping.

The institution of marriage continues to evolve. This is the subject of E.J. Graff’s book What Is Marriage For? published by Beacon Press in 1999 and again in 2004. E.J. is a journalist and writer who weaves her personal story in and out of the social history of marriage as an institution. She married her partner Madeline in a friend’s living room in the traditional month of June. She writes that

It was nearly a delirium: by accident we’d spilled into something sacred. …To our utter surprise, the ceremony did bring us closer, pulling an invisible cloak around us that has warmed us during difficult times. We’d thought ourselves as committed as any couple could be: how else could we have exposed ourselves to the world’s ridicule? But now even the most subtle traces of doubt dissolve instantly, chased away by the memory of that day when we made our declarations so publicly, placing our love in the hands of God and everyone we knew. (p. 246-247)

 

A theology of marriage for liberal religion most definitely includes same-sex marriage. This may be a “no-brainer” for most of us here, having traced our own religious roots as dissenters from the seven sacraments of the Catholic Church, which included marriage, through the Protestant Reformation’s two sacraments, which no longer included marriage, to contemporary liberal religion which recognizes rites of passage but no longer contains proscribed sacraments. In tracing the history of marriage from legal contract in order to preserve property to an act made holy by the church, E.J. Graff celebrates the gift in her own life that married priests after the Reformation began to preach: “marriage, that difficult and daily attempt to treat each other well, could be a spiritual act that pleased God.”

Yes, as Unitarian Universalists we affirm the rights of same-sex couples to marry. We work for it, carry signs for it, wear yellow t-shirts and stand on the side of love for it. And that is where, for many of us, our theology of marriage ends. When we answer the question, “What Is Marriage For?,” we think in terms of what makes us happy, what personally fulfills us and what serves our own needs as evolving human beings. We avoid thinking in terms of marriage as a spiritual act, practice or discipline. We know that many marriages end in divorce, the condemnation of which belongs to more conservative religions than ours. As a minister I know that many marriages end in divorce, including my own first marriage. This is why I’m more comfortable describing my theology of death than my theology of marriage. When a person dies, those left behind form a community of grief that can find comfort in each other’s presence. When a marriage dies, those left behind are often asked to take sides. Children are emotionally, if not also physically, abandoned. Hearts are broken. The remnants of community after a marriage ends in divorce are often harder to heal, harder to put back together than communities mourning a death.

   We are promise-making, promise-breaking, promise-renewing creatures, according to theologian Martin Buber. The covenant of marriage is a promise made in community. When that promise is broken, the community is challenged to respond in a way that reflects the values of that particular community. The response may be stronger in a religious community clearly defined by shared beliefs. In a liberal religious community such as ours, one defined by shared values rather than beliefs, the response becomes more complicated. My theology of marriage includes how a community can respond when promises are broken. My theology of marriage is also shaped, surprisingly, by how a non-denominational evangelical Christian community in Dallas used what is known as the “Matthew 18 Process” as their response to one couple whose promises to each other fell apart and became a very public witness to the ethics of community.

Several years ago, while I was serving as director of development for a substance abuse treatment center for economically disadvantaged women, I was approached by a member of a growing church called Watermark Community Church. The church was, and still is, headed up by a Joel Osteen-like charismatic preacher by the name of Todd Wagner. Pastor Wagner encouraged each member of his growing flock to follow their passion to create outreach into the Dallas community. Once a member created a project, the church would get on board with volunteer and monetary support. I was approached by a hair stylist from one of Dallas’ most elite hair salons. Paul found out about our agency when he was helping to assemble “goodie bags” for our annual fundraiser that attracted the well-coiffed along with the well-heeled of Dallas. He wanted to do something other than give away bottles of shampoo to wealthy Dallasites paying high ticket prices to hear a national speaker at a luncheon. He wanted to do something tangible for the lost and the hurting.

For the next five years I worked closely with Paul to create an annual event known as “Glow:  A Day of Beauty & Encouragement for Women.” During those five years we became friends. He also became my hair stylist. I even invited him to my ordination. He was unable to come, which quite honestly may have changed our relationship if he had attended. For five years we had a somewhat unspoken agreement. We never unpacked our theologies beyond recognizing the common ground we shared in our care for the “least of these.” And we never mentioned the lawsuit that had erupted when a former member sued his church for breach of confidentiality.

The first year I worked with Paul and Watermark Community Church they were outgrowing their rental space in a local high school auditorium. They had just purchased expensive property along the LBJ Freeway including two buildings that had been the headquarters of a restaurant chain. The property included adjacent vacant land where they would later build two worship spaces for what would become their more than 6,000 member congregation. The first year of our event temporary sinks to wash hair were installed in what was once a kitchen for the building, and another floor was transformed into a health fair.  Our clients had the opportunity to have their hair cut, colored and styled, and to receive manicures, facials, massages and make-up. Paul assembled a host of volunteers including an entire beauty school, local hair stylists and of course, volunteers from the church. The second year the event was held on a floor of one of the newly renovated buildings that had been specifically designed with the event in mind: plumbing was included for temporary hair salon sinks and extra electrical capacity was added for multiple blow dryers and curling irons. Pastor Todd Wagner put the wealth and the commitment of the community behind his word: if you were a member of the church and had a project that made a difference in the Dallas community, the church would be your partner.

Watermark Community Church commits to being your partner in other ways as well. Their vision is to call all people to be fully devoted followers of Christ. They believe in the inerrancy of the Bible, the Trinity, Jesus as the only way to salvation and that Christ will come again. They also value commitment, authenticity and accountability in their church members. This is not my interpretation of who they are; this is who they say they are on their web site.

In 2006 a member went to the pastor for guidance after she discovered her husband was having an affair. At first the pastor counseled that they reconcile. When this failed, the pastor allegedly shamed the man from the pulpit and, again allegedly, threatened to send letters to people that both the man and his girlfriend knew in the wider Christian community. The pastor was following a common practice among evangelical Christians, a process based on Matthew 18:15-18:

If another member of the church sins against you, go and point out the fault when the two of you are alone. If the member listens to you, you have regained that one. But if you are not listened to, take one or two others along with you, so that every word may be confirmed by the evidence of two or three witnesses. If the member refuses to listen to them, tell it to the church; and if the offender refuses to listen even to the church, let such a one be to you as a Gentile and a tax-collector. (Truly I tell you, whatever you bind on earth will be bound in heaven, and whatever you loose on earth will be loosed in heaven.)

 

I read this passage to you within our context of the study of covenant. Last week we read our Covenant of Good Relations aloud to each other affirming such promises as:

·         Speak honestly and directly to the person(s) with whom I have a concern, using thoughtful, compassionate language.

·         Listen deeply to, and actively seek resolution with those concerned before engaging the Conflict Resolution Committee, Committee on Ministry, the Council or the Board of Trustees in conflict mediation.

·         Expect divergent beliefs and opinions and persist in respectful dialogue—especially in times of uncertainty—remaining open to new understandings while doing my part to sustain this Covenant.

 

There are some similarities to these two ways of being in religious community. There are major differences as well. We do not practice a rules-based ethic that calls for us to cast out a member as if they were a “Gentile or a tax-collector.” Rather, ours is a values-based ethic that encourages “a free and responsible search for truth and meaning.”

The danger is that we fall into a sort of anything goes attitude when it comes to marriage or other commitments we fail to take seriously in our antiauthoritarian rejection of what we perceive as rigid, rule-based religions. Marriage and family therapist William J. Doherty, writing in the UU World magazine, invites us to consider a theology of marriage that challenges our propensity towards Jonathan Swift’s “anythingarianism.” Doherty writes,

I come to this conversation as a big fan of marriage, both same-sex and straight, and with a background as a Catholic seminarian, a Unitarian Universalist for twenty-seven years, a university professor, and a practicing marriage and family therapist. With this history, I feel I have gone through a kind of whiplash on marriage during my adult life. As a Catholic, I knew marriage was a permanent covenant and a sacrament. As a new UU and liberal professional, I decided that marriage was not so special--just a personal lifestyle that should not be subjected to so much social pressure about entering and exiting.

 

Now after thirty-three years of marriage to the same woman and a career working with couples in distress, I see lifelong marriage as a countercultural act in a throwaway society. Without ignoring the shadow side of marriage and the pain of divorces that cannot be avoided, we religious liberals can support marriage and shape its future according to liberal ideals. (UU World, 2005)

 

As I consider my evolving theology of marriage, I look to the liberal ideals that I can include. One is an acknowledgment that affirming a respect for the interdependent web of all existence of which we are a part means that a lot of people get hurt when a divorce occurs. The interdependent web includes children, friends, co-workers and church congregations. Justice, equity and compassion in human relations means that we redefine old patriarchal models of marriage. It also means that when a marriage dissolves we work hard to make sure that our compassion for all those affected does not dissolve at the same time as the marriage does. When we “place our love in the hands of God and everyone we know,” as E.J. and Madeline did more than 20 years ago, we open ourselves to the possibility that transcending mystery and wonder can be found in marriage. We create what poet Robert Bly calls “The Third Body”: “A man and a woman sit near each other… They obey a third body that they share in common. They have promised to love that body.” As their religious community we promise to love that body too.

There is an epilogue to the story of how a non-denominational, evangelical church shaped my theology of marriage. For five years I worked closely with many of the elders of the church. All of the elders are men; it is in their doctrinal statement that only men will serve in that role. This is consistent with their Bible-centered faith. My last year of involvement with the church I grew bold and mentioned that I was attending seminary. One of the elders asked me why, what did I plan to do with a Master of Divinity degree. I replied that I was going to be a minister. The conversation abruptly ended.

Several months later I saw this same elder at an event for the recovery community. Not wanting to open up the previous conversation, I deliberately avoided him. He, however, made a point of joining me at my table and asking if we could talk after the event was over. What happened then was a sort of confession. He wanted me to know that he and his wife had been told to leave Watermark Community Church; that they had disagreed with the use of “The Matthew 18 Process.” He felt I needed to know this because I was becoming a minister. He wanted me to know how churches can behave sometimes. He felt compelled to warn me. I think he also wanted me just to listen to his story. He needed a pastoral presence to bear witness to his pain.

A liberal theology of marriage acknowledges the pain we can cause each other in community when we break our promises to each other. A liberal theology of marriage also acknowledges that the covenant made between two people in marriage is a sacred promise, one that is held in trust by a Love that dwells within us, among us and beyond us, always renewing and transforming us as we become more fully human.
