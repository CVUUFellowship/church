Asking for Forgiveness

© Rev. Lora Brandis

Preached October 9, 2011

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship

 

Jews have just marked the Days of Awe, the Day of Remembrance and Judgment and the Day of Atonement. These are the days, the High Holy Days, which set them apart as the people called Israel. Yom Kippur, ending at sunset yesterday, marks the end of Judaism’s High Holy Days. One of the rituals included in this ancient tradition is for the leader and the congregation to say together at the beginning of the evening service three times “May all the people of Israel be forgiven, including all the strangers who live in their midst, for all the people are in fault.” This seems to be a story told about a people only, one tribe, but the individual’s conduct over the past year affects the entire community. Who the community is, how the community defines itself, is based on the behavior of the individual. Even those who do not identify as part of the people, the tribe, are held responsible for the spiritual health of the community.

On the Day of Atonement Jews fast and pray all day. There is a public remission of vows, vows thoughtlessly made in the previous year and reckless promises they may make in the year to come. A prayer is sung, the formula recited: we transgress and are forgiven. This public recitation of individual sins requires that the community takes responsibility for what happens in it. There is an acknowledgment that the community is of one body and all are responsible.

“May all the people of Israel be forgiven, including all the strangers who live in their midst, for all the people are in fault.” “If even one of us remained silent when a single voice would have made a difference” (Robert Eller-Isaacs), then all of us are responsible. “May all the people of Israel be forgiven, including all the strangers who live in their midst, for all the people are in fault.” If one of us was paralyzed by fear when trust could have moved us forward, we are all responsible. “May all the people of Israel be forgiven, including all the strangers who live in their midst, for all the people are in fault.” If just one of us struck out in damaging anger, we are all responsible. The individual asks for forgiveness and the community that stands with them is made up of individuals asking for forgiveness. What affects one of us, affects us all. Asking for forgiveness, we take responsibility for ourselves and for the community we co-create.

The holiest day of the Jewish year Jews come before their God and hope for forgiveness. This is part of their identity as Jews. Forgiveness is also part of the identity of Christians. Christians, borrowing from their Jewish heritage, are also asked to repent before they can expect forgiveness. Christians carry the idea of forgiveness as central to their identity even further in what one scholar sees as a misreading of Jesus’ teaching to “turn the other cheek.” Eva Fleischner wrote:

I believe Christians – and non-Christians in their wake – have misread, and continue to misread, these texts, interpreting Jesus’ teaching to mean that we are to forgive anyone and everyone, whatever the wrong done to anyone. The element that is lost sight of is that Jesus challenges me to forgive evil done to me (in itself quite enough of a challenge!). Nowhere does he tell us to forgive the wrong done to another. Yet, the widespread impression persists among Christians that, to be truly Christian, we must forgive, plain and simple, no matter who has been sinned against. (Essay in The Sunflower: On the Possibilities and Limits of Forgiveness, pp. 140-141)

 

We’ve all seen them: those pitiable fellow humans standing in front of a camera on the evening news just minutes after losing a loved one to unspeakable violence. They proclaim from the depths of their despair that they are a Christian and because of that they will forgive the perpetrator of the heinous crime. And we do not believe them. We do not believe them because we think if we were the ones standing in their place in that moment of shock and utter disbelief forgiveness is not what we would offer. We could hardly offer anything to anyone, much less forgiveness to the one responsible for our anguish.

Five years ago this month we watched the despair and anguish of the Amish community of Nickel Mines in Lancaster County, Pennsylvania. A gunman walked into a one-room schoolhouse, opening fire on 10 little girls, killing five. The quiet landscape of Amish country was invaded first by an act of violence and then the media came. National and international media filled the quiet countryside, snapping pictures of people who object on religious grounds to their photos being taken. The morning of the girls' funerals, cameramen lined the streets as the horse and buggy procession passed. We watched a community in shock, mourning their loss. We watched a solemn community reach out to the family of the gunman, some of them even attending his funeral. The community’s quick move to forgiveness and reconciliation became the topic of the evening news as we all watched and wondered how we would have responded if our child’s life had been so brutally cut short.

There were no interviews given proclaiming forgiveness as the correct response. No one stood in front of a camera in shock and despair declaring that their identity as a people demanded forgiveness. Although we did not hear pitiable, intrusive interviews, we watched. We watched how they responded. We saw the familiar black and white of their simple existence against the backdrop of a way of life they seemed exempt from: the violence broadcast daily, victims lives insensitively exposed. We saw forgiveness in the actions of the Amish community and we believed them. The Amish did not offer a cheap, easy forgiveness to pardon a dead man for his unpardonable act of violence. If he had lived, they would have expected the man to be punished.  Because he did not live – he killed himself after killing five of their own – they attended his funeral and gave his family some of the money that had poured into their community after the tragedy.

The Amish did not offer cheap, easy forgiveness because they knew that forgiveness is a journey that never ends. “When recently asked about what he had learned over the last five years, one of the Amish fathers said, ‘I have learned that healing is possible. And that it is not yet finished’” (L. Gregory Jones, “Living forgiveness: Lessons on the fifth anniversary of the Amish schoolhouse shootings”).

This is a community’s response, a rising up as one body to forgive what has damaged the whole – an innate knowing that has been forged in the practice of being community. The tribal response of a community set apart by their simple way of life.

Judith Toy did not have a community of forgiveness to turn to when her family was murdered (source: http://theforgivenessproject.com/stories/). And she didn’t offer forgiveness immediately. She did not stand before the cameras on the nightly news and offer forgiveness to the 19-year-old neighbor who brutally murdered her sister-in-law and her two teenaged nephews. It wasn’t until five years later, through her newfound practice of daily mindfulness in the Zen Buddhist tradition, that Judith spontaneously forgave.

After the murders Judith turned to meditation to help ease her grief and confusion. One day, nearly five years after the murders, she sat down to write a poem. She was reminded of these words by her teacher, Thich Nhat Hanh:

I am the frog, swimming happily

 in the clear waters of my pond,

 and I am the grass-snake who,

 approaching in silence,

 feeds itself on the frog.

 I am the child in Uganda, all skin and bones,

 my legs as thin as bamboo sticks,

 and I am the arms merchant, selling deadly

 weapons to Uganda.

As she wrote her own words down, she knew the hard truth of her teacher’s poem. She knew the “I am” of “the 12 year old girl, refugee on a small boat,

 who throws herself into the ocean

 after being raped by a sea pirate,

 and [the] I am [of] the pirate, my heart not yet capable

 of seeing and loving.

That was the day I stopped thinking of Charles as the beast and began thinking of him as a boy in whom something had gone awfully wrong, [writes Judith]. The pain of resentment vanished! Breathing out, I saw in me the seeds of murder, seeds which have never been watered and so they have withered. Still they are there. I saw within me not only the victims, but also the perpetrator. Sadly, before I was able to tell him so, Charles took a laundry bag and hung himself in a prison cell. I mourned his passing. Twelve years after the murders, his mother and I cried together over our mutual loss.

 

This solitary act of forgiveness arises not from tribal, set apart community. This solitary, spontaneous act of forgiveness arises out of the knowledge of our connection to the whole of humanity. It is a knowledge that comes out of the willingness to see the seeds of murder in our own hearts.

Most of us will not have to be confronted with the choice of forgiving a loved one’s murderer, but we may be asked to forgive an act that causes us heartbreaking pain. We may not be asked, but we may choose to forgive anyway. Many survivors of childhood sexual abuse choose to forgive their perpetrators, knowing that this act of forgiveness frees them and subsequent generations from the cycle of abuse. Survivors of the suicide of a loved one may eventually decide to forgive the one who has abruptly left them behind, knowing that their own healing resides in a relationship that does not end with death.

These are all large acts of forgiveness that we can return to again and again on ritual days of atonement, on anniversaries and in the quiet of our own spiritual practices. It is the smaller, everyday acts of forgiveness that remind us that the word fault does not mean blame and that we are each responsible for the spiritual health of the community.

The story of the prince and the rhinoceros is a folktale about forgiveness told at the level a child can understand. The sort of forgiveness called for is the smaller, everyday act of forgiveness needed after harsh words. The kind of forgiveness a parent needs after yelling at a child too severely. The kind of forgiveness a partner needs after lashing out in anger and frustration. The kind of forgiveness a co-worker needs after criticizing another’s efforts. There is a happy ending to the story of the prince and the rhinoceros. They lived forever after — in friendship and great joy. Never again did an unkind word pass between them because the prince asked for forgiveness and the rhinoceros granted it.

I think there’s something missing in this simple story. Maybe they really did live forever after in friendship and great joy, but even after the rhinoceros forgave the prince, the prince would have to forgive himself. Even after the child forgives the parent, the parent will want to forgive themself. Even after the partner is forgiven in love, in self-love they will forgive again. This is the truth that the Amish now know five years later. They responded with forgiveness immediately to the gunman and his family. This forgiveness sent them on a journey where they discovered that healing is possible. The journey is not over. What started with reaching out to others will end in a journey to the self. What the Amish of Nickel Mines will discover, may have already discovered is that all acts of forgiveness lead back to the self.

“May all the people of Israel be forgiven, including all the strangers who live in their midst, for all the people are in fault.” For even we have had times when we have ignored the needs of others. “May all the people of Israel be forgiven, including all the strangers who live in their midst, for all the people are in fault.” Even we have broken our covenant and refused to see the worth of another. “May all the people of Israel be forgiven, including all the strangers who live in their midst, for all the people are in fault.” Yes, even we have thought we were separate and superior. May we all be forgiven. May it be so. Amen.
