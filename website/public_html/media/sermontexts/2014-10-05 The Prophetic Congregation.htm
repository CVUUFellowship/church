The Prophetic Congregation

©Rev. Lora Brandis

Preached October 5, 2014

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Congregation

 

I recently worshipped with a group called HeartSpace Spiritual Center in Dallas while visiting family. A friend invited me to worship there. The same friend, coincidentally, introduced me to Unitarian Universalism 17 years ago. HeartSpace is a member community of the Centers for Spiritual Living, an organization formerly known as the Institute of Religious Science which was founded by Ernest Holmes in 1927. Holmes was greatly influenced by Emerson, so worshipping with HeartSpace was a lot like visiting with cousins.

My husband Frank and I met my friend there thirty minutes before worship began, choosing to start our worship experience with a group meditation. It was a good choice - a reminder that just twenty minutes of meditation a day makes a difference in our lives. The worship experience itself was filled with soul-stirring singing accompanied by a jazz trio that included an old friend of Frank's who he's known since high school.

The worship experience was very informal. About thirty people gathered in a small storefront space, which is rented out for events by the wine bar next door. The minister's young daughter ran back and forth from her mother to a caregiver during the service. The group was quite diverse, the sort of multicultural gathering UUs aspire to. The experience was heartfelt and authentic. HeartSpace Spiritual Center embodies its name.

The sermon that day was "stump-the-minister." We were invited to write a question prior to the service and submit it for her response. The questions ranged from "how can I deepen my prayer life" to "how do I know I exist?" (For those of you considering suggesting that I provide a "stump-the-minister" sermon someday, please refrain from asking that second question.) The minister handled all of the questions with grace and wisdom, including inviting input from the lead musician and the worship associate for their responses to the questions. As to the question about existence, she quoted Descartes’s famous line "I think therefore I am" and quoted something from Emerson to further answer the question.

"I studied Emerson, and this was like drinking water to me." - Ernest Holmes (1887-1960). Read the statement of belief that Holmes wrote in 1927 or the updated statement at heartspacedallas.org and you hear Emerson's influence: "We believe that heaven and hell are not physical places to which we are sent by God, but describe states of mind. God is unconditional love, and would not create life to destroy or condemn it. When we tap into the energy of Abundance and Peace, we are in heaven. When we perceive separation from God, we experience hell." That's our cousin, a religious descendant of Emerson, expressing a belief about God.

Many of us would agree with that statement and yet we choose Unitarian Universalism. Why is that, I wonder? Of course, many of us agree with that statement in principle but would remove the theistic language.

As we explore this year's theme "People of the Books": Towards a Unitarian Universalist Identity we are asking ourselves many times over: “Why do we continue to choose Unitarian Universalism?” One possible answer is that we are Unitarian Universalists in order to be a part of a prophetic congregation. A prophetic congregation chooses to bless the world with acts of social justice. We choose Unitarian Universalism because we feel strongly that we have a responsibility to work with however we define the holy to create heaven here and now.

The Rev. Meg Riley writes, “Many Unitarian Universalists would abandon the idea of prophetic entirely. I have heard UUs speak instead of congregations that are bold, or visionary, or relevant, or vital, or justice-seeking. I like all those words, but I like prophetic more, because of its relationship to time and possibility; it draws from a place deep in our past and casts our vision around the corner into an unknown future” (A People so Bold, loc 229).

Our second source – “words and deeds of prophetic women and men which challenge us to confront powers and structures of evil with justice, compassion, and the transforming power of love” – invites us to embody a theology of interdependence. No longer satisfied with a faith tradition that merely defines our individual response to the holy, many choose Unitarian Universalism to join in community as the prophetic church. We choose this faith in order to cast our vision around the corner together into an unknown future that we help create.

Emerson defined the poet-prophet as an individual who creates the future with words, opening each generation up to a new interpretation of reality. It would be inaccurate to associate Emerson with the prophetic church, however.  Early on in the debate about slavery Emerson is quoted as saying, "Does he not do more to abolish slavery who works all day steadily in his garden than he who goes to an abolition meeting and makes a speech?"

Emerson’s statement, similar in ways to Gandhi’s “Be the change that you wish to see in the world,” is the emphasis on the individual which still permeates American culture, Unitarian Universalism and our cousins at the Centers for Spiritual Living. Eventually, Emerson joined the abolitionist movement but was criticized in his own time for coming to the movement somewhat late. He has also been criticized by historians who see in his thought an emphasis on personal spirituality that ignores a connection to the wider community. Emerson’s gift to Unitarianism in the nineteenth century was to provide a living language designed to awaken the individual’s inner life. It is a gift that was not limited to our religion, but that extended to the whole of American culture. We have to turn to another theologian, in another century, to take the individual further into community and into a deeper sense of what it means to be the prophetic church.

Twentieth century Unitarian theologian James Luther Adams wrote, “The prophetic liberal church is the church in which all members share the common responsibility to attempt to foresee the consequences of human behavior (both individual and institutional), with the intention of making history in place of merely being pushed around by it” (The Essential James Luther Adams: Selected Essays and Addresses, 112). Adams critiqued nineteenth century Unitarianism for its avoidance of a theology of evil and its inability to adequately name sin. The notion of the continued progress of humanity needs to be tempered with what Adams identified as the “tragic factor of history.” We must be careful also about our tendency to idolize the individual. “Be the change you want to see in the world” can lead to a bunch of individuals expressing their individual opinions about the change they each want to see. Without community and working out effective social change in conversation with partners and allies, our social justice efforts fall flat. Yes, “be the change you want to see in the world,” but also rise up together as one and be the change that we hope to see.

Rob Keithan writes, “In U.S. society, and perhaps within Unitarian Universalism especially, our pursuit of transformation tends to overemphasize one of two extremes. Either we concentrate on personal change without much regard for larger social factors, or we focus on changing society without paying sufficient heed to our individual lives and needs” (A People so Bold, loc 2428). Because ours is a religion of deeds not creeds, we have a tendency to overemphasize doing over being. It is covenant that can call us into balance as a prophetic congregation that recognizes the need for reflection and action. It is covenant that gives us the theological grounding to find the balance between the two extremes heard here in words by Mark Belletini:

When the machetes of injustice

tear apart the fabric of the nation and the world,

I speak up.

When the sunset resembles purple and orange vines

entwined like lovers,

I sigh in silence.

 

Unitarian Universalists speak up and sigh in silence. This is not an easy faith to have. We reflect and then we act. We peer around the corner together into an unknown future.

We are more than a collection of individuals on our own spiritual journeys. The covenant is how we create what becomes the “more” in our gathering together. Unlike the Centers for Spiritual Living, we do not have a statement of belief. We have a covenant.

The first covenants were political treaties in the ancient Near East dating as far back as the fourth century B.C.E. Consequences for breaking covenants were clearly stated in the covenants themselves. These ancient political covenants were kept in shrines and holy places because ancient people saw the gods as active in human history. These covenants may have been enshrined, but they were also political promises between groups of people to define territories and to maintain agreements. According to Reverend Burton Carley, minister of the First Unitarian Church of Memphis, “The Hebrew people took the political covenant that came before them and elevated it into another dimension. They used covenant to describe the divine / human relationship thus imparting to it a deep theological and moral significance.”

We can trace the roots of our covenantal faith to the Protestant Reformation and how it influenced one English minister to lead his people in the development of a congregational covenant. This congregation and others like it, who wanted to further remove Catholic elements from the Church of England, became known as Puritans. We can trace the roots of our covenantal faith to these Puritans and those among them known as the Pilgrims. We can also trace the roots of our covenant to the Mayflower Compact, a document written because walking together, being in covenant with each other, is often very difficult.

The Mayflower didn’t carry one happy band of Pilgrims covenanted to walk together as friends. Only half of the group came from one congregation who had escaped religious persecution in England, spent some time in Holland and from there headed to America. This was the group we call the Pilgrims, the others on the Mayflower were people the Pilgrims called the Strangers because they were not from their congregation. Trouble arose when the ship was unable to land at their intended destination and the two groups found themselves at odds with each other. The Mayflower Compact, a covenant, was designed by the leaders of the two groups to create a civic structure that recognized they needed to walk together in order to survive. In this covenant can be seen the beginnings of democracy in America.

It was the Puritans of Massachusetts Bay Colony, who came after the Pilgrims, who gave us the next historic covenant in America. The Cambridge Platform of 1648 summarized the elements of a congregation’s covenant and it continues to be the foundation of our covenantal faith today.

Much has been written about the lack of a cohesive Unitarian Universalist theology because we do not have a binding creed or set of beliefs. We do not ask you to sign a belief statement when you sign the membership book. We ask you to covenant with us, to walk with us in the spirit of love and with mutual respect. This is the teaching of our religion; you might even call it our theology – the covenant. This promise that we make to dwell together in peace, to be guided by love, to seek knowledge in freedom, to respect each other, to join in meaningful action, to use the democratic process, to affirm the dignity and worth of every human being…. This promise, this evolving covenant binds us one to the other and to that which calls us out of ourselves. Our covenantal theology is essentially a theology of interdependence that calls us to be the prophetic church in a broken and hurting world.

It probably really isn’t that much of a coincidence that the same friend who introduced me to Unitarian Universalism invited me to experience worship with her at the HeartSpace Spiritual Center. She and I have always been spiritual seekers drawn by the words and deeds of prophet-poets like Emerson, Thoreau and yes, Ernest Holmes. I have cast my lot with Unitarian Universalism, not just because it is important to understand the role of the prophetic church in “making history in place of merely being pushed around by it.” I am a Unitarian Universalist because ours is fundamentally a theology of interdependence. Ours is a religion that both nourishes our souls and calls us to bless the world.
