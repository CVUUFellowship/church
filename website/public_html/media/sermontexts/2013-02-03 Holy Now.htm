Holy Now

© By Rev. Lora Brandis

Preached February 3, 2013

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship

 

I went to the water for an experience of “the mind-stretching, jaw-dropping, in-your-face wonder of the universe itself” that religious naturalist Chet Raymo identifies in a metaphor he created in response to fundamentalist atheism. In his inverted cliché, he has the “bath water” stand for all of creation, an object worthy of our awe, thanksgiving and praise. The baby represents how religion has concretized its symbols, turned its gods into humans and created a non-existent supernatural world. Raymo comments that atheists have thrown the bath water out with the baby. Keep the bath water, but by all means throw out the baby Raymo tells us. Throw out the baby, the “memes without substance,” the cultural baggage religions have accumulated. Accumulated, because of our inability to be comfortable with a natural world that cannot be fully understood or explained. Accumulated, because we refuse to rest in mystery.

The word meme was coined by the evolutionary biologist Richard Dawkins to explain how ideas are spread throughout a culture. Memes are carried through writing, speech, ritual and repetition. They can be as inconsequential as a catch-phrase and as insightful as a philosophical breakthrough. Hoping to reconnect to a way of being religious that had once helped me to toss out the “baby,” the memes without substance, I literally and metaphorically went to the water. “After all,” Raymo writes, “water, as much as anything in our environment, is an adequate symbol for the creative agency that forges atoms in the hot interiors of stars, weds oxygen to hydrogen, and wets the Earth with the stuff of life and consciousness – an agency worthy of attention, reverence, thanksgiving, praise” (When God is Gone, Everything is Holy).

I could have stayed home with the less tepid water of the Pacific Ocean, but I would have conducted my search alone. I needed 450 colleagues and all of Unitarian Universalism with which to wrestle. Knowing that a solitary search for the holy would only get me so far, I flew across the continent to the biannual conference the UU Ministers Association names as the CENTER Institute for Excellence in Ministry. I went to the Gulf Coast waters in Florida to reconnect to my call, to learn from colleagues and to deepen my Unitarian Universalist identity.

Many months ago, I signed up for the workshop I would take for three of the four days I was there. I was excited to choose from titles like: “Ordained Ministry for A New Millennium: Theologies and Practices of Re-newal” and “Dancing on the Edge of Meaning: Religious Leadership for a Multi-Racial, Multicultural and Theologically Diverse World.” The preaching workshop with the Rev. Dr. James Forbes, named by TIME magazine as one of the top preachers in the U.S., filled up quickly so the terrifying prospect of preaching in front of a renowned liberal protestant preacher plus many of my colleagues thankfully fell of off my list of possibilities early on.

As much as I love Rebecca Parker, the president of our Starr King School for the Ministry, I couldn’t quite get excited about her workshop on ordained ministry for a new millennium. After reading the description:  “The Puritan inheritance of an apocalyptic and eschatological imagination has informed our current understandings of prophetic, pastoral, and counter-oppressive ministry, but these understandings might be further evolved in light of contemporary contexts. Similarly, the historical relationships between UU identities and the roles of ordained ministers have shaped our theologies of sacramental and ritual practice—and yet, how might these theologies shift now, especially in the context of multi-religious engagement? How might a post-modern, relational understanding human interchange inform an evolving ecclesiology in an age of social networking and internet technologies?” and finding out there was homework to be completed before departure, I signed up for the “Awakening Bhakti Workshop” which promised to help me express and share the deepest caverns of my soul and ultimately find the eternal spring of love.

Knowing well the joke about Unitarian Universalists arriving at the gates of heaven where there are two signs - one pointing to heaven and the other to a discussion about heaven – I was hoping I had not signed up for a discussion about chanting the ecstatic songs of ancient India. My soul was thirsty for the refreshing waters of reverence, thanksgiving and praise. My soul was yearning to sing.

While 400 of our colleagues were wrestling with ideas like religion and the American radical tradition, theme-based ministry and member integration, and practical ways to use ministerial power and authority, thirty of us were on the top floor of the conference center chanting the names of god. As we chanted we looked to our right to the Gulf of Mexico and to our left also – the Gulf. Surrounded by light bouncing off the water, we sang in call and response to Jai Uttal and a young Hare Krishna drummer by the name of Dharma Devi Das. In between ecstatic shouts of Sita Ram and Om Nama Shivaya, Jai told us the stories from the epic Ramayana, the story of Rama (an avatar of the Hindu preserver-God Vishnu), whose wife Sita is abducted by the king of Sri Lanka, the demon Ravana. Jai also told us his personal journey from New York Jew to Bhakti Yoga practitioner.

Jai told us how, at the age of 19, he left New York City for Northern California to study with the classical Indian musician Ali Akbar Khan. Jai then began regular pilgrimages to India, living among the Bauls, the wandering street musicians of Bengal, and singing with the kirtan wallahs in the temples of his guru. Singing traditional Indian kirtan (sacred chants) deepened Jai’s connection to the divine and gave him a sense of joy. Like many of his generation, (Jai first travelled to India in 1971) Jai found that his spiritual path led East.

We listened, we chanted and we rested. One day after lunch Jai remarked that we all looked quite tired. He told us to lie down on the floor and he would sing to us. Like kindergartners taking an afternoon nap, we sprawled out on the meeting room floor, not meditating, not chanting, in a floating state of not-doing our souls drinking in the waters of ancient Sanskrit sung for thousands of years as an act of devotion to the divine.

When I was 19 years old I had a profound meditation experience when followers of the Siddha Yoga lineage visited my college campus. The next week a friend and I got lost driving to the meditation center. We never tried going there again. Years later after meditating with other techniques and schools, I was reintroduced to Siddha Yoga by a friend who thought the practice of chanting, meditating and listening to the teachings of Gurumayi Chidvilasananda would be good for me. I was going through a divorce, co-parenting my two young children and had recently returned to working full-time. I was ripe for a change in my spiritual practice.

I attended regularly for a few years, even travelling to the ashram in upstate New York to meet the guru. I attended workshops and intensives. I chanted the impossibly long Guru Gita. I was even learning to play the harmonium so that I could help lead the chants at the Dallas center. My longing for the holy kept leading me to new insights and self-discoveries.

If you have read the book or have seen the movie, you may know that Elizabeth Gilbert’s guru in Eat, Pray Love is Gurumayi. You may also know that Siddha Yoga is rife with controversy, similar to other power-hungry Gurus that have come out of the complex religious landscape in India; the Siddha Yoga lineage is not as divine as it purports to be. As soon as I started hearing the rumors about Baba Muktananda, Gurumayi’s guru, including allegations of sexual abuse, I stopped attending. Muktananda had been dead for years, but it didn’t matter to me. I couldn’t be a part of something that purported a devotion to divine love publicly, but privately kept secret power struggles and abuse. I left and continued my search for the holy in less obvious places.

I became a Unitarian Universalist. My meditation practice is influenced by the many schools I have practiced with, but mostly now it is a Buddhist practice utilizing techniques I have learned sitting with two Sangha and reading many books by Western Buddhist teachers. I have given up on enlightenment, which is something I have in common with Jai Uttal. He joyfully mentioned this in passing while he gently guided us each in leading our own sung prayer.

Jai has given up on enlightenment, but not on singing the names of god. It connects him to the millions of chanters who for thousands of years have trusted the ancient power of the mantra. Jai brings kirtan all over the world, including Israel, where he regained his Jewish identity in a way that now makes sense to him almost 40 years later. You can hear it in his son’s name: Ezra Gopal. Ezra, the priest of the Hebrew Scriptures who reintroduced the Torah in Jerusalem after the Babylonia Exile. Gopal, the infant form of Lord Krishna.

This is the world we live in: an aging hippie born as a Jew chooses Bhakti Yoga as his spiritual path. Chet Raymo, a scientist born as a Catholic, becomes a religious naturalist. This is the Great Awakening the Rev. Dr. James Forbes preached to us in Florida: it is an Interfaith Great Awakening this time and Unitarian Universalists are ready.

We know that everything is holy now. We know that in a post-Enlightenment, scientific world there are no dualisms. There are no dualisms of sacred/profane, body/soul, natural/supernatural, matter/spirit, no heaven/hell.  The scientific story of creation teaches us that the universe is a unity – “an interacting, evolving, and genetically-related community of beings bound together inseparably in space and time,” in Raymo’s words (location 954).

If everything is holy now, does that mean the sacred loses its meaning of being separate, set apart? If everything is holy now, is nothing holy? Has the sacred been dragged down to the level of the profane?

On our last day in Florida, a colleague who had also chanted the names of god, sat with me for a last few minutes in the sun. She had also been a one-time member of Siddha Yoga and we were comparing notes. Another colleague walked by us carrying a shell, taking it to hotel staff with the hopes of finding out what it was, to find out how to name it.

A visit to the shores of lullabies,

So far from here, so very far away,

A floor of sand, it doesn't matter where,

And overhead a water-ceiling sways;

A shell is summoned to materialize—

The holy life, a spiral, hushed and pure,

Complete unto itself—a spiral shell

Is summoned from a substratum of wonder:

….

With everything in play, and all in play,

And all is well now, hush now, close your eyes— (Gjertrud Schnackenberg)

Chet Raymo concludes that when God is gone, everything is holy. This is similar to theologian Paul Tillich’s statement that when all of the concrete Gods are gone, the divine remains. When everything is holy we are not dragged down to the commonplace, to a place where everything is the same and nothing is special. When everything is holy we are lifted up into the unity of creation, into the wonder of the cosmos at play. All is well. Everything is holy now.
