Students of Compassion

© Rev. Lora Brandis

Preached at the Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship

September 15, 2013

 

Twelve years ago a group of Islamic terrorists attacked the United States killing almost 3,000 people and causing the collapse of the World Trade Center towers in New York City. It was the end of an age of innocence for most Americans who, up until that time, watched an angry world turned in on itself from the safety of a television broadcast. Terrorist attacks were something that happened to other people.

The attack twelve years ago was also an important entry for the United States into the conversation about religion and violence. In the aftermath of September 11th, hate crimes against Muslims and South Asians increased in this country. Muslim Americans came forward to educate us all that the acts of a handful of religious extremists do not speak for an entire faith group.

As Karen Armstrong states, she has lost count of how many times she has “been informed categorically that religion has been the cause of all the major wars in history. In fact, the causes of conflict are usually greed, envy, and ambition,” she says, “but in an effort to sanitize them, these self-serving emotions have often been cloaked in religious rhetoric” (Twelve Steps to a Compassionate Life, p. 4).

Even as the military option against Syria seems to be off the table for now, it is still important to understand the religious rhetoric that cloaks the conflict there. It can be argued that the conflict in Syria is an inevitable re-balancing of power along ethnic and religious lines. Most Syrians are Sunni Arabs, but the country is run by members of a minority sect known as Alawites who are ethnic Arab and follow a smaller branch of Islam. The borders in Syria are leftover from European colonialism, which forced religious and ethnic groups into a cauldron of potential conflict. Human greed, envy, ambition and distrust of the other are attached to tribalism and sectarianism. It may look like religion is the “bad guy” in Syria, but when we start to unravel the self-serving emotions, including our own country’s self-serving emotions, the case to convict religion becomes a bit more complicated. Even in Egypt, where both sides are now invoking that the “right” interpretation of religion is on their side, it can be concluded that political power is more at stake than religious loyalty.

It isn’t just religion as the cloak of war that has many blaming religion for the bulk of what is wrong with our world. It is also “abstruse doctrinal definitions” and downright refusal to accept modern science that has many naming religion as the “bad guy.” In Texas, a measles outbreak was traced to a right-wing megachurch whose pastor had preached against vaccines. Once the source of the outbreak was located, it should be noted, the pastor cooperated with Texas Health Department authorities, even offering to host vaccine clinics at her church.

This sounds like something that could only happen in Texas, right? The Netherlands has been experiencing a measles outbreak since May. The outbreak is expected to get worse because it is rapidly spreading among unvaccinated orthodox Protestant children. It is easy to blame all of religion as a superstitious, violence-promoting, backward-thinking institution that halts humanity’s progress. It is more challenging to unearth the compassionate voice of religion that has always been there. Yet, this is the task that religious scholar Karen Armstrong sets out to accomplish with The Charter for Compassion and her Twelve Steps to a Compassionate Life. It is the task that we have taken on this year as a people of faith.

The first step is “Learn About Compassion.” We start this task as students. We begin this year with pencils sharpened and minds open wide to learn. We are asked to learn “about the mythos that introduces us to the deeper meaning of compassion” (p. 27).

It is developmentally appropriate to read a story to our children that implies that if countries would only practice the Golden Rule there wouldn’t be any wars. As adult students of compassion we are asked to move beyond a simplistic application of the Golden Rule. We are asked to consider the complexity of living compassionately in a world that makes religion the “bad guy” and personal opinion an idol.

We are asked to consider Thich Nhat Hahn’s “Recommendation,” a poem he wrote for his fellow Buddhist workers in Vietnam on how to die nonviolently, without hatred. “The only thing worthy of you is compassion,” he wrote, “invincible, limitless, unconditional. Hatred will never let you face the beast in man.” Love becomes eternal when we face this beast with compassion. Loving our enemy as they kill us, is an embodiment of the Golden Rule that most of us, thankfully, will never have to face.

What is the mythos that introduces us to the deeper meaning of compassion? What are the stories that pull us down into the depths of life’s questions, past logic, past debate? What are the timeless, universal truths that can teach us how to respond compassionately? As we go through the twelve steps together this year we’ll examine some of the traditional myths – the stories – that will take each of us on our own hero’s journey into living compassionately. We do not need to ignore the tribalism and the violence in both the Jewish and Christian scriptures. We can learn that there is a long tradition in both Judaism and Christianity to read those scriptures through an interpretive lens of history, experience and allegory leading us to a deeper meaning of compassion. When we learn about the mythos that introduces us to the deeper meaning of compassion here, we turn to all the world’s religions and cultures, knowing that what forms a culture’s collective knowledge and memory can also take us deeper into the truth of compassionate living.

Karen Armstrong wants us to begin this journey learning about the religious and philosophical traditions that emerged during the Axial Age. She writes in the book that “From about 900 to 200 BCE, during what the German philosopher Karl Jaspers called the ‘Axial Age,’ there occurred a religious revolution that proved pivotal to the spiritual development of humanity” (p. 31). Hinduism, Buddhism, Jainism, Confucianism, Daoism, monotheism in the Middle East and philosophical rationalism in Greece all developed during this time. Armstrong’s thesis is that we have never surpassed the insights of the Axial Age. It is time for us to turn back towards the teachings of that time; it is time to turn back to the teachings of compassion.

The sages and the teachers from this time period honored their own experience of the transcendent, but didn’t necessarily give it a supernatural explanation. They also honored the transcendent experience of the student, urging everyone to test their teachings based on their own personal experience. Siddhartha Gautama (The Buddha) said this, “Believe nothing just because a so-called wise person said it. Believe nothing just because a belief is generally held. Believe nothing just because it is said in ancient books. Believe nothing just because it is said to be of divine origin. Believe nothing just because someone else believes it. Believe only what you yourself test and judge to be true.”

Before the Axial Age, religion was about ritual and animal sacrifice. After the Axial Age, personal transformation that translated into ethical action became central. Each tradition developed its own version of the Golden Rule:

  • Hinduism: Mahabharata 5:15:17: Do naught unto others which would cause you pain if done to you.
  • Buddhism: Udana-Varga 5:18: Hurt not others in ways that you yourself would find hurtful.
  • Jainism:  "Man should journey through life treating his fellow creatures as he would like to be treated."— Sutrakritanga 1.11.33
  • Confucianism: "Try your best to treat others as you would wish to be treated yourself, and you will find that this is the shortest way to benevolence."
  • Daoism: "Regard your neighbor's gain as your own gain, and your neighbor's loss as your own loss."
  • Judaism: "Love your neighbor as yourself; bear a grudge against no man. Whatever you hate, do to no man." — Leviticus 19:18
  • “We should conduct ourselves toward others as we would have them act toward us.” — Aristotle

 

And so we should, “conduct ourselves toward others as we would have them act toward us,” and yet we don’t. We are addicted to an egotism embedded in the old brain. We are limited by instincts that served our human and pre-human ancestors well. These drives – identified by neuroscientists as the “Four Fs”: feeding, fighting, fleeing, and…reproduction – keep us trapped in the “I.” The teachings of the Axial Age, radical in the development of humankind, invite us to disappear the “I” into a compassionate relationship with the other.

The good news is that along with the development of our instincts, the brain has been developing more benevolent and altruistic emotions. As science continues to apprehend the workings of our brain including the reptilian brain, the limbic system, the Neocortex and the differences between our right and left brains, we can begin to understand that empathy is possible and compassion is as much a part of our brain development as the development of an ego focused simply on surviving. Religion is the source of some wars and it is also the source of the Golden Rule, the criterion of true faith.

Even in its most simplistic application, applying the Golden Rule to the situation in Syria makes bombing Syria out of the question as a religious response. We condemn the use of chemical weapons.  Do naught unto others which would cause you pain if done to you, the Hindu version of the Golden Rule tells us.

The Syrians have clearly violated the Golden Rule. Responding with compassion, how is it that we would bomb and kill the Syrians in response to their own killing? It is like punishing a child who has hit a playmate or a sibling by hitting the offending child. Hurt not others in ways that you yourself would find hurtful.

In the Jewish tradition, during the High Holy Days which have just ended, Jews are called to ask God and themselves to move from the throne of Punitive Justice to the throne of Compassionate Repair. In recent remarks Pope Francis stated that, “Never has the use of violence brought peace in its wake. War begets war, violence begets violence.” The Rev. Peter Morales, President of the Unitarian Universalist Association said this in response to the situation in Syria: “We mourn the loss of life by all kinds of weapons. And we assert that the U.S. government needs to exhaust all non-violent methods to bring about an end to this conflict before resorting to military intervention.” This is religion at its best: responding from a deep tradition that embraces the principle of compassion. These are the compassionate voices of religion, voices that have been speaking for thousands of years. Just yesterday American and Russian diplomats reached an agreement that seems to have postponed a violent response to Syria for now. It would be naïve to conclude that these religious voices of compassion halted the use of force; it is more apparent that a diplomatic solution became easier to reach after a threat of a violent response.

The first step of the Armstrong’s book Twelve Steps to a Compassionate Life is the most academically dense. She advises that we read through the whole book first and then return to it a second time to practice the steps. Learning about compassion is not just an academic exercise; it is a spiritual one and first we must prepare ourselves with expanding our knowledge.

It will take time to unlearn our own judgments about religion as the “bad guy,” as the cause of all the major wars in history. It will take time to unlearn the damage done to ourselves and those we love by abstruse doctrinal definitions. It will take time to learn that love can become eternal when we practice compassion in a world that seems determined to self-destruct. This is the practice: when you find yourself sitting across the table from someone telling you their story – a story so far outside your own life experience – allow yourself to walk a holy spiral that pulls you down into the depths of your being. This is the practice: listen deeply to the story of the “other.” Listen deeply to this opportunity to learn compassion, knowing that the only thing worthy of you is compassion.
