Occupy Church

©Lora Brandis

Preached November 6, 2011

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship

 

On September 17th men and women of all races, backgrounds, political and religious beliefs, began to organize in nonviolent protest. They declared themselves to be the 99% with the goal of ending the greed and corruption of the wealthiest 1% of America. OccupyWallStreet had begun.

Members of our Fellowship met October 12th to organize Occupy Thousand Oaks. About 40 of us, including non-UU participants, gathered Saturday October 15th at the corner of Lynn and Hillcrest roads. It was also the same day that people all over the world gathered in more than 1,000 cities to support the Occupy movement.  We gathered again the next Saturday and will continue to gather Saturdays from 11am to 1pm and Fridays from 4:30 to 6:30pm.

UUA President Peter Morales issued a statement October 13th declaring, "Unitarian Universalism embodies a long tradition of working for economic justice and workers' rights. Today is another opportunity for us to live our faith, and the Occupy protests are a first step on the road to repairing our country.” On October 17th I signed The Unitarian Universalist Service Committee’s “Open Letter of Support for the Occupy Movement” “as an affirmation of my hope for fair and compassionate economic reforms, and as a renewal of my commitment to help make it so.” An Iraq war veteran was injured Oct. 25th in Oakland when police clashed with “occupiers” who were protesting the police dismantling of an encampment outside of the city hall. Occupy London protestors have set up camp in front of the historic Saint Paul’s Cathedral and the Anglican Church is poised to seek legal action to have them removed. On October 27th, The Rev. Dr. Giles Fraser, the canon chancellor of St Paul's Cathedral, resigned from his position at the Cathedral over the concern that evicting protesters would lead to "violence in the name of the Church".

This litany of dates and events demonstrates how new this movement is and how quickly this movement is growing. The responses from the president of the UUA and the Unitarian Universalist Service Committee indicate that we are to live our faith in response to this new movement – this new movement that is barely two months old and changing every day. How do we respond to a leaderless, unstructured event? How do we respond to a movement that is creating itself with each action it develops, each action constructed out of a direct democracy that honors the expression of each voice? How do we – a creedless, living faith tradition bound by no dogma, led by the democratic process in our own autonomous congregations, respond?

The second time I participated in Occupy Thousand Oaks, I stood next to a woman I had just met. As we were exchanging names and niceties a woman in a car stopped at the light started yelling at us. Before I knew it I was drawn into a shouting exchange that included the woman in the car yelling at us that it was all “Obama’s fault” and the woman next to me accusing her of being a racist. I shouted at them both: “This is not about blame. This is about coming together for the good of all.” The woman in the car probably didn’t hear me; she rolled her window up and drove off as the light changed. The woman beside me did hear me, laughing my response off as appropriate for a minister. I laughed with her at first until I realized that my response is appropriate for all people of faith, not just for their ministers.

Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr. published Where Do We Go From Here:  Chaos or Community? in 1967, shortly before he was assassinated in Memphis in 1968. The book is King’s prescription for what comes next after the first wave of extreme change that was the result of his call for equality. What King hoped would be next was a radical solution to the end of poverty; the end of the book is King’s argument for each person’s right to a living wage. More than forty years ago Dr. King wrote:

The stability of the large world house which is ours will involve a revolution of values to accompany the scientific and freedom revolutions engulfing the earth. We must rapidly begin the shift from a “thing”-oriented society to a “person”-oriented society. When machines and computers, profit motives and property rights are considered more important than people, the giant triplets of racism, materialism and militarism are incapable of being conquered. A civilization can flounder as readily in the face of moral and spiritual bankruptcy as it can through financial bankruptcy (p. 216).

 

In assessing where the civil rights movement was in 1967 King observed that there had been a shift from an emphasis on personal dignity to an emphasis on programs: programs that changed the basic system of social and economic control. In this shift there was a potential for the movement to go beyond race to embrace economic justice for all. King warned that after the first wave of extreme change, Americans would cling to the status quo for fear of a more profound and lasting change. No longer clinging to the status quo, we are awake now and occupying Wall Street.

On October 29th an early snow threatened to shut down OccupyWallStreet. New York City mayor Michael Bloomberg, using a "health and safety inspection” as his justification, instructed NYPD and FDNY to seize the fuel and generators used by demonstrators to power their media center and kitchen. The demonstrators, used to responding to an ever-changing environment, asked for help from a cycling advocacy group in Boston. The advocacy group mapped the energy needs of the park and determined that it would take 11 stationary bicycles pedaled continuously to replace the generators and fuel seized by authorities. (Source: http://www.lefthudson.com/2011/10/occupywallstreet-outsmarts-bloomberg.html) Adapting to a change in circumstances, the mutual decision-making power of the group uncovered an innovative solution.  

On October 27 economist and activist David Korten presented at our Community Forum here at this Fellowship and expressed his concern that the Occupy movement does not yet have a unifying story. There is a declaration, with a laundry list of wrongs ranging from illegal foreclosure processes to weapons of mass destruction. There is also a unique community. This global community is being created from lessons learned during the Arab Spring and the 15th of May Movement in Spain. This new way of creating community embraces Collective Thinking. Collective Thinking takes time and is different from the right/wrong, confrontational, you win/I lose type of decision making many of us are used to. The objective of Collective Thinking is creative and constructive. Replacing confiscated generators with safer bicycle generated electricity is an idea that arose from Collective Thinking. In the old way of thinking, call it Confrontational Thinking, we rate ideas and then vote on the best one. Someone loses or, at best, has to compromise what they thought was the best solution. In Collective Thinking a new idea comes from actively listening; a new idea that no one had thought of beforehand, a transformational idea arises independently from the wisdom of the whole.

An important component of the Occupy movement is the General Assembly. The General Assembly asks all present to participate and decisions are made by consensus. A way of communicating has been handed from General Assembly to General Assembly across the movement. The first time members of this congregation participated in Occupy Thousand Oaks here is what we learned: 1. Agreement is shown with upraised, open hands moving from side to side, 2. Mild disagreement is shown by lowering the hands and moving them from side to side, 3. Strong disagreement, based on moral and ethical reasoning, is shown by arms folded across the chest, 3. Needing clarification, or an answer to a question, is signaled by raising a hand and moving it above the head and 4. The “mic check” is used when participants in the back of the group can’t hear what is being said. The group acts as a human microphone, repeating what has been said until the entire group is brought into the discussion. The “mic check” has also been used to peacefully disrupt public meetings like the one recently held by Chicago’s Union League Club with keynote speaker Wisconsin Governor Scott Walker. Walker was surprised by cries of “Mic Check” from members of Stand Up Chicago and the Chicago Teacher’s Union who interrupted the meeting with messages from the 99%.

These signs and signals vary from group to group across the world as this new language is shared. Each time a new Occupy group gathers, common signals are agreed upon and a sub-group of this global language is formed. Each time a new person joins, signals and signs are shared. A new, constructive, respectful way of communicating is expected. Blame, prejudice and ideology have no place in Collective Thinking. The group associates freely; if you do not agree with a decision that is made then you are free to decline participation in a particular action.

On October 28, the filmmaker Michael Moore, addressing Occupy Oakland said, "We are all leaders. And we are all followers. We are all doing this together. The media and the power establishment is having a hard time figuring this out. So, be patient with them. They are used to just a few people showing up with a few signs and then they go away and have a meeting in the basement of the Unitarian Church. God bless the Unitarians, by the way."

On November 2, more than 7,000 protestors in Oakland joined in a general strike that shut down the shipping port. Supporters in New York, Philadelphia, Los Angeles and other cities staged demonstrations in support of the Oakland movement, which called for the general strike in response to the earlier incident when the Iraq veteran had been injured.

The general strike was considered a success with thousands of people participating in an action of civil disobedience, until violence erupted late during the night. Differing reports portray the perpetrators of the violence as angry protestors who broke into an empty building that had once housed a program that aided the homeless. The program had been dismantled due to budget cuts. Others report that the violence came from masked intruders, who broke off from the crowd. Those who occupied the building intend to re-start the homeless program. It is not clear who started the violence, but it is clear that the majority of protestors in cities worldwide are calling for peaceful demonstrations. (Source: http://www.huffingtonpost.com/2011/11/03/occupy-oakland-violence-_n_1073325.html#s451892)

The new, unifying story of the Occupy movement is being written with each passing day. Unfortunately, the new story is being told at the same time the old story refuses to die. The violence that occurred late Wednesday and early Thursday is the old story. The story we tell about power fighting power with confrontation, ending with winners and losers. The new, unifying story of the Occupy movement is struggling to be written with new words: the words that come from Collective Thinking and peaceful resistance. The old American story is going away - the one that goes: “I pulled myself up by my bootstraps, why can’t you?” The story that tells of an America conquered by rugged individualism; the story that values things over people; the story where the “giant triplets of racism, materialism and militarism” always win.

The new unifying story of OccupyWallStreet is emerging out of the experience of the protestors. It is a story about shared power, power with rather than power over. It is a story arising from within a grass roots community organizing effort, rather than a story imposed from outside by a hierarchy led by a privileged class. It is the story of interdependence. It’s a new story and it is also a very ancient one. It is a story a lot like the story of Indra’s Magnificent Jeweled Net.

The story of Indra’s Net comes to us from the Hindu and Buddhist traditions. Indra’s infinite jeweled net holds dazzling jewels at each crossing point on the net. The jewels infinitely reflect one another. This is the story of the interdependent web of all existence. We are each a jewel in this story. As we look into the shining individual next to us we see ourselves. We are independent at the same time we are interdependent. This story is often told to remind us that what one person does in the interdependent web of all existence affects us all.

There is another element of the story, which may go missing to the Westerner who hears it. The story is also a reminder to look beyond the surface of reality. While we are proclaiming we are the 99%, we cannot assume that appearances tell the whole story. We cannot assume that the woman yelling from her car at protestors, blaming our president is a racist. We cannot assume that someone who stands on the corner with us as the 99% votes the same way we do or prays to the same God, or even prays at all. As the new, unifying story of the Occupy movement arises, it asks us to go beyond blame, beyond appearances, beyond power over. This new, unifying story invites us into partnership, into constructing reality here and now and into a new way of being that allows creative solutions to arise out of the wisdom of the whole.
