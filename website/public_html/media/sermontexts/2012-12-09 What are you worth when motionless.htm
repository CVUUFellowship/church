What are you worth when motionless?

© by Lora Brandis

Preached December 9, 2012

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship

 

Teachers often teach what they need to learn and preachers often preach what they need to hear. This week’s theme of Sabbath literally brought me to my knees, but not in prayer. Friday morning, in my haste to eat breakfast before a morning meeting, I fell on my knees. Racing about in my office (apparently it is possible to gather enough speed in my small office to do some damage) I tripped over my computer’s power cord and came crashing down on my knees. I didn’t even try to break my fall with my hands – they were full. Rather than dropping the hard boiled eggs, the blueberries, the almonds and the empty coffee cup I was carrying, I took the full force of the fall with my knees. Now every time I climb into bed, get down on my hands and knees to play with the cat, or search for something on the bottom bookshelf I have a painful reminder to slow down.

United Church of Christ Minister Donna Schaper writes, “Although the reality of Sabbath as a day of rest has been lost in our busy society, the sense of Sabbath, as spiritual leisure, is still very much needed in our time-starved world” (Sabbath Sense, p. 14). I remember Sunday as Sabbath. Attendance at church was always followed by the Sunday afternoon meal where we sat together as a family, marveling at my mother’s culinary skills. I remember the end of Blue laws when we could finally stop at our favorite neighborhood store on the way home from church for a sweet treat or a forgotten item from the weekly shopping. When we moved to Texas from Illinois I marveled at the curious site of those items not for sale on Sundays. The grocery stores would be open, but certain items were roped off, beyond reach of Sunday shoppers. The blue laws that remain on the books in Texas stipulate that car dealerships must remain closed on either Saturday or Sunday, at the option of the dealer. Any alcohol (beer and wine only) cannot be bought on Sundays before noon. Liquor stores are closed on Sundays. People should be in church on Sunday mornings. At the least, they should not be drinking.

Blue laws are not just about Sundays. In Massachusetts and Connecticut blue laws dating to the Puritans of the 17th century still prohibit most retail stores, including grocery stores, from opening on Thanksgiving and Christmas. Sabbath is not just for us to keep, but it is also a way of allowing all workers access to expansive time. It may be convenient to start our holiday shopping Thanksgiving evening, but it also promotes a cultural norm that values doing over being.

“Type-A” Unitarian Universalists will want to take up the cause and immediately start protesting the abuse of workers forced by low wages to work on holidays. This is not the point. The point is to stay home, create Sabbath rituals of our own and then perhaps tomorrow take up the task of crafting a letter to the editor proclaiming the need for a livable wage.

We can stay home. We can keep the Sabbath. We can learn to do nothing.

We can learn how to do nothing from wikiHow, “a collaborative effort to create the world’s most helpful how-to guides.” wikiHow is like Wikipedia in that anyone can write or edit an entry. It took 123 authors working through 482 revisions to create a list of eight “somethings” to teach us “how to do nothing,” raising the question “is it really possible to do nothing?” The first how-to on the list sets the tone: “1. Schedule quiet time. Whether it's an hour, a day, a week, a month, or year of doing nothing, cancel all of your appointments for that block of time” (http://www.wikihow.com/Do-Nothing). I have never met anyone capable of doing nothing for a whole year. Even ministers, who can take up to seven months of sabbatical leave after seven years of service, are doing something during their sabbatical time. The expectation is that the minister will use sabbatical time for study, education, writing, meditation, and other forms of professional and religious growth.


We are not wired to “do nothing.” Even the 123 authors of the list of eight acknowledge that doing nothing can create anxiety. They issue a warning at the end of their list, cautioning the reader that they may feel nervous, sad, or restless when first attempting to do nothing. Relax, they tell us. The ultimate goal of doing nothing is so that we can become more productive and creative, ultimately enhancing our ability to do more.

In the long run, learning how to do nothing does not take us deeper into Sabbath. Tom Owen-Towle, the author of Theology Ablaze gives us a better question than “is it really possible to do nothing?” He says that the consummate theological query comes from Antoine de Saint Exupery who asks, “What are you worth when motionless?” I am one of those “type-A” Unitarian Universalists who finds this question very difficult to answer. Rarely motionless, I would rather come crashing down on my knees than drop all it is I am holding in my arms.

Observant Jews look to the Sabbath as separate from ordinary time. By late Friday afternoon those who keep the Sabbath are preparing themselves by bathing, putting on their Sabbath garments and getting ready for the special meal. Sabbath begins at sunset and ends when three stars have appeared Saturday night. During the meal, particular Sabbath foods are served. During the Sabbath there is time for reading and studying the Torah, synagogue worship and rest. Sabbath is marked with ritual handed down over time; it is one way Jews learn and reaffirm their identity as Jews. Sabbath ends with the havdalah (translated as separation), a ritual that indicates the end of holy time and the beginning of ordinary time.

Donna Schaper writes that Sabbath also means to separate. “Sabbath is the separation of time into different parts. Sabbath is neither rest nor labor but the separation of the two. Sabbath is the pause between them,” she writes (p. 20-21).  Not completely motionless during Sabbath, the action we do take is to separate ourselves from the sameness of our days during ordinary time.

During ordinary time we are driven by a Protestant work ethic distorted by a sense that we might not be “good enough.” If we just work hard enough perhaps we will be good enough.

We equate salary with worth. You can see it in the current debates over the distribution of wealth in this country. The idea that somehow a highly paid CEO works harder than his minimum-wage earning employee to reach the top of his profession distorts the idea that hard work equals success, equals wealth, equals happiness.

Very few of us work a 40-hour week anymore. We complain about it as if it were a badge of honor. It proves after all that we are worth something – look how hard we work! We are our accomplishments. We fear that we may not be worth anything when motionless, so we keep moving, keep achieving, proclaiming “no rest for the weary” and celebrate our test scores, our grades, our salaries and our possessions as if those were the only measures that mattered. We forget that our accomplishments are only temporary and they are not who we are.

Who we are when motionless is pure potential. We need Sabbath time to separate who we think we should be from who we are becoming. This is not to say that work can never make us happy. Work as vocation can call us to our authentic selves. Without Sabbath we may not hear that call. Without Sabbath rituals to remind us of our true selves we can get lost in an ordinary time that exhausts and overwhelms.

We do not have to wait until the end of the week for Sabbath time. We can set aside just a few minutes each day to pause, to become more mindful, to stop rushing, to stop multi-tasking. We can set down all that we hold in our arms and wait.

We can also recognize the rituals we already have in place that provide us a sense of Sabbath. These may be as simple as taking a morning break and enjoying a cup of tea in a special cup or going out to eat once a week with our family. Sabbath can be found when we take the time to notice how we “take” our time, how we spend our time, how we give away our time. Sabbath reminds us of the value of time. It also reminds us that we have value outside of time.

My body is adjusting to a different way of experiencing Sabbath. When my children were young I returned to church, joining a small Presbyterian church to sing in the six-member choir. Sunday was a day of rest and ritual, from the early morning choir rehearsal to the conversations at coffee hour. Entire weekends were set aside from ordinary time. As a stay-at-home Mom this was the time to hand the kids off to their Dad, to run errands, to attend soccer games. Sunday was slower than Saturday and was reserved for the great American rituals of watching football, creating the family evening meal and the long afternoon nap.

Sabbath changed for a short time after our divorce. I left the small Presbyterian church feeling that I wasn’t receiving the support I needed as I walked through the end of my marriage. It was as if who I was becoming no longer fit within the walls of that Sunday space. I took up a new Sabbath practice. Every other Sunday when I had custody of my girls we went to the local waterpark, which naturally was empty because most everyone else was at church. The water held me in motionless exhaustion while my children splashed and played around me. As we discovered who we now were as a family, the summer ritual of water, sun and play shaped a new identity. The Sabbath ritual gave us the pause between the rest and labor of a shifting ordinary time. I was no longer a stay-at-home Mom. My girls now lived in two different homes. We needed ritualized time to in Schaper’s words, “ritualize the chaos of modern life.”

Eventually we went back to church, First Unitarian Church of Dallas, where our Sabbath ritual included choir, religious education, worship, volunteering for countless pancake breakfasts and the deepening of the commitment to our newly chosen faith. Sunday afternoons were left unplanned as Frank and I learned how best to blend our families and share Sabbath-time with our former spouses and extended families. Now that I am a minister Sunday is no longer my Sabbath. My body is still adjusting to Monday as my Sabbath. My mind is still adjusting to Monday as Sabbath.

In attempting to heed the Hebrew scripture’s commandment to remember the Sabbath and to keep it holy (Exodus 20:8), I have discovered that is not enough to take a break from emails and phone calls and to-do lists. I must also take a break from thinking about emails, phone calls and to-do lists.

I could listen to the 123 authors of “How to do nothing” and turn off my phone and computer. I do attempt to practice other suggestions from their list including sitting quietly on the beach and meditating. I have discovered that it is my constant focus, my constant thinking about the to-dos, the musts and the shoulds of ordinary time that prohibits me from remembering the Sabbath and keeping it holy. This is the most difficult Sabbath-taking I have encountered. I long to be still, to separate my time into different parts, to pause between rest and labor, but I don’t know how.

Perhaps I could get some help from the seven authors of “How to Have Fun on Sabbath” posted on wikiHow. The eight-steps for this how-to include: nature walks, napping, going to the dog park, playing bible board games, reading a good book, taking pictures, inviting friends over, and writing or reading a wikiHow article. There are some good suggestions on this list. Going to the dog park could be awkward. I’m not sure if my cat would enjoy it. I might draw attention to myself if I went alone, without a dog, so I might have to adjust this suggestion to my unique situation. Playing bible board games – I might also have to adjust this suggestion to my unique situation. Traditionally the Sabbath is to be spent reading and studying religious and inspirational texts, but my ordinary time is filled with this study. My Sabbath might include a good novel.

The reality of Sabbath as a day of rest has been lost in our busy society. We are so afraid that who we are when motionless is not good enough that we have forgotten how to slow down. As spiritual sojourners here for such a brief time we can learn to set down all we hold in our hands and in our minds. We can be drawn to our knees in rest and contemplation. We can withdraw into the holy time of Sabbath and emerge more fully alive into an ordinary time filled with meaning and with purpose.
