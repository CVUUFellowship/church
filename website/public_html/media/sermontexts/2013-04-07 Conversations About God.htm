“Conversations About God”

© By Rev. Lora Brandis

Preached April 7, 2013

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship

 

I have a small plastic cross with the imprint “God Is Love.” I imagine I got it when I was eight or nine years old. It glows in the dark. Or at least it used to. I have kept it all these years on my home altar alongside other keepsakes. These pieces of my heart include the note my husband wrote with the first flowers he sent me, a note from our girls celebrating our engagement officially blending our two families, four photos of Indian gurus, two other cross icons, a yin yang symbol, a stone with the word happiness painted on it and a card from a Catholic bookstore with a prayer to consecrate the family. I’m not taking any chances; I want to include everything I can in my naming of the holy. Like Nancy Shaffer I want everyone to feel included in my prayers, but I don’t mean those who would pray with me. I mean I want to be inclusive in my naming. I want to make sure that the unnamable, the unfathomable who may or may not be listening, is mentioned in my prayers and represented on my altar. I want it all on my altar to the Life living in and through me, as Shaffer invokes in her poem “That Which Holds All”: “Spirit, Holy One, Mystery, God, Spirit of Life, Spirit of Love, Ancient Holy One, Mystery We Will Not Ever Fully Know, Gracious God, Spirit of this Earth, God of Sarah, Gaia, Thou….” (“That Which Holds All” by Nancy Shaffer).

“God Is Love” – a simple phrase for a child to comprehend. A simple phrase even for a child to begin to question. A child’s first gods are her parents. By the time she reaches eight or nine, the age I may have been when I received my plastic cross, the child begins to suspect her gods are not so perfect. God may not be love all of the time. But in the stories, in the myths handed from one generation to the next we begin to make sense of our life, even at a very childish and literal level. “God is Love” written in small block letters on a cross is simple at first, a hopeful beacon for a child wanting god to be love all of the time. The word God starts to get a little bit more complicated as the child grows and learns all of the details of the story of Love hanging on a cross.

In Dangerous Words: Talking about God in an Age of Fundamentalism Gary Eberle notes that, “In polytheistic pagan northern Europe, there were many gods and goddesses, each with his or her proper name. From conversion on, however, the ancient word God would be reserved only for the Christian God described in the Bible, a God who allowed for no other god to be named God.” This is the God most of us reject when we say we do not believe. This is the God I put away when I stored my plastic cross in a box with other souvenirs from my childhood, the plastic dinosaur from the 1964 World’s Fair, a replica of a Native American drum from the Adirondack Mountains, my first Barbie Doll. So much plastic! Plastic, like god, has multiple meanings. Plastic also means malleable, pliable and flexible. How is it that this little plastic cross can really be plastic? Malleable, pliable and flexible? How can the word god continue to travel with me after being put away in a box with other childish things? How is it that I can become comfortable with a little plastic cross sitting in between a picture of Paramahansa Yogananda and a symbol of the Tao?

In the book What We Talk About When We Talk About God Rob Bell writes this: “I realize that when I use the word God in the title of this book there’s a good chance I’m stepping on all kinds of land mines. Is there a more volatile word loaded down with more history, assumptions, and expectations than that tired, old, relevant, electrically charged, provocative, fresh, antiquated yet ubiquitous as ever, familiar/unfamiliar word God? And that’s why I use it.”

Here’s why I use it: I want to be a part of the conversation. I want to be a part of the conversation represented by Rob Bell’s book and Gary Eberle’s book and the many other books that are wrestling with this word - The God Delusion, The Evolution of God, The History of God, Good without God and on and on. I want to be a part of the interfaith conversation, the political conversation, the human rights conversation and I want to bring my gods with me – especially into those conversations that use the word in ways with which I disagree. I use the word because it is plastic – malleable, pliable and flexible. When I use the word it carries all the meanings that it has held throughout my life. It also carries the potential of meaning so much more.

In this year of doing theology together, of discovering together what it is to kindle the fire deep in our souls and then talk about it with each other, we have encountered a lot of words along the way. We have explored a lot of theological themes together. Forgiveness and reconciliation, covenant, beauty, evil, embodiment, faithfulness, gratitude, mystery and wonder, Sabbath, joy, death, hospitality, peace and nonviolence, the sacred, love, grace, suffering, justice, hope and now God. If you are keeping track, and I hope you are, here is what we have left to cover: interdependence, prayer, self-care, silence, salvation, and finally, evolution. Last fall in our “Introduction to Theology” Ablaze class, a member of this congregation very wisely wondered what words on this list caused him to shut down, to stop the conversation, to stop listening and to stay in rejection.

Even the word love shuts us down sometimes. Even the word love becomes small, unattainable, turned in on itself. What is your word? Is it the word sacred, salvation, faith, hope, love? What words do you reject and why? What words cause you to stop the conversation, to leave the room, to stand still on your journey? Is it the word God?

Notice I did not ask you what you believe. I am asking you what religious words you use to open your heart, to connect with others, to cry out with anguish or to shout in praise. Notice that in the video we showed this morning of congregation members responding to the word God, we did not ask, “do you believe in God?” We asked, “what do you hear when you hear the word God?”

Some of you think of metaphor when you hear the word God. The realm of metaphor takes us deeper into a place where things aren’t quite as they seem and startling similarities jolt us into new awareness. God is like love. God is like a strong mother, a warm father. God is like an “entire ocean of compassion” (Shaffer). All religious language is metaphoric. It is not a matter of belief. It is a matter of movement. The best use of religious language takes us someplace new, towards a place we can never fully understand, never fully arrive. God is a journey word. It is more poetry than theology that word God. It is a word that can point beyond literal interpretation if we let it. It is a word that can retain the innocence of a child believing in love at the same time it holds the cynicism of an adult who has learned that the literal God is dead. Philosophers and theologians have worked this out: when the concrete gods are gone, the divine remains.

The divine remains in metaphor and all religious language is metaphoric. Gary Eberle says that “The conceptual map of religious language only works, however, if we know or can remember how to read it. And it is on this point that we seem stuck today. The scientific modernist looks at the religious conceptual map as an antiquated chart of a world that doesn’t exist. The strict fundamentalists, stuck in their literalism, confuse the map with the terrain it describes” (p. 174). Unitarian Universalists are stuck on this point too. In 2003 when William Sinkford, then the president of our denomination, preached a sermon calling for UUs to reclaim a vocabulary of reverence including the word God, it made the national news. Religious language doesn’t have to mean we use the word God, but it does mean that we somehow remember to read the conceptual map of religious language. It means that we continue to develop a vocabulary of faith that points beyond the map. It may mean that we venture beyond the uncharted areas of the conceptual map. We may need to go beyond that place where medieval cartographers proclaimed “here be dragons.” We may need to wrestle with the literal gods we have rejected in order to find gods beyond all naming.

Right now I do not have a home altar. The table I was using for it is still in Dallas with my husband Frank. It’s a handmade table we picked out together – a small plank of wood inserted into side supports carved with jaguar heads, green marbles are used for their eyes. We bought it in a shop that specializes in importing Mexican artesanía –folk art made by hand to be used as home décor. In the Olmec and Mayan traditions the jaguar represents the ability to move between the “real” world and the “spirit” world. The jaguar protects the shaman as he moves from the earth to the spirit realm. This jaguar table seemed an appropriate object for a home altar; another symbol that pointed beyond itself. Mayan gods depicted as jaguars are gods of the underworld, gods of the night, gods of fertility and strength. Jaguar gods have their own names; names they lost in the conquest.

Right now I do not have a home altar. I retrieved my plastic cross with the block letters “God is Love” from a drawer where it has been residing in a plastic bag along with love notes, four photos of Indian gurus and other symbols of the divine. It is more than forty years old and may not retain the ability to glow in the dark, although I spent most of the day Saturday testing it. I left it sitting under a lamp, hoping to revive its ability to put forth a bit of light in the dark. By the end of the day when I took it into a darkened room I think I could see the gleaming outline of a cross. Maybe it was just wishful thinking – the adult who I’ve become hoping that the literal message “God is Love” still shines light on my path. Call it what you will, “love” or “ground of being” or “Spirit of Life.” If I make love one of my gods I will be on my way to constructing a vocabulary of faith.

May all of our vocabularies of faith point beyond the literal. May we know in our loss of innocence that love can prevail, that there can be words of our own choosing that open a way for us that is beyond words. May we choose a path that takes us beyond rebellion into connection, beyond rejection into a deeper reverence for all of life. May we become one with the “entire ocean of compassion”, with “That Which Has Been Present Since Before the Beginning,” with the wind, the rain, with “that which holds all” (Shaffer). May we enter into this great conversation about God and may we be held.
