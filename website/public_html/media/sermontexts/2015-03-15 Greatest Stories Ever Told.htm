Greatest Stories Ever Told

The Reverend Anne Felton Hines

March 15, 2015 (CVUUF)

 

          A few summers ago, I drove across the United States and spent a few months of a sabbatical on Cape Cod, in a wonderful old house owned by my sister and brother-in-law. (Life is rough, but somebody’s gotta’ do it!)

In mid-December I began my return home; and probably because I was spending so much time alone in restaurants, gas stations, and hotels, I became more aware than usual of the overwhelming presence of Christmas every place I visited.

        Christmas trees were in lobbies of hotels. Wreathes and other greenery adorned the restaurants. And Christmas carols were non-stop everywhere – even as I pumped gas into my car at gas stations.

        And all of a sudden one day, as I sat in some restaurant somewhere in the southern United States, listening to yet another string of mindless Christmas songs, I thought to myself: Gee…I wonder how I would feel right now if I were Jewish? Or Muslim? Or Hindu?

        The commercialism of Christmas in the American culture has roiled me for most of my adult life – and still does. But that year, on my drive across our country, I became more aware and uncomfortable with the religious exclusivity of our American culture – especially during December.

It’s not that I hadn’t noticed it before; objections from conservative Christians to the so-called “war on Christmas” have been going on for a very long time. And there have been numerous battles with fundamentalist Christians over displaying crosses or the Ten Commandments on government property, allowing prayer in public schools, excluding the teaching of Evolution to children (or including so-called “Creationism” as an equally-valid concept), etc.; it’s been going on since I was a child – which means a very long time!

But that particular year at Christmas is when I – who was raised as an Episcopalian and in many ways is still tied to that tradition – gained a heightened sensitivity to what it might feel like to be a non-Christian in America. It was a humbling experience.

But as often happens, once I returned home and immersed myself in the Christmas traditions of my own family, I forgot all about my experiences of the previous few weeks; life went on as usual.

And then the following Christmas, the issue came to a head not far from home – in Santa Monica; you may remember hearing about it. Apparently since the 1950s, the city of Santa Monica has allowed a series of fourteen Nativity scenes to be displayed in the park during the month of December. A few years ago, a man described as “a local atheist,” Damon Vix, objected to the scenes because the park is public land – paid for by the residents of Santa Monica. Mr. Vix began protesting by erecting signs with more humanist messages – such as a quote by Thomas Jefferson: “Religions are all alike – founded upon fables and mythologies.” But he went further than that; he and (quote/unquote) “other atheists” applied for dozens of spaces in the park to set up their more inclusive displays.

 

And then – I guess because too much space was being filled up in the park with displays – the Santa Monica City Council held a lottery for spaces – and the atheists won the majority of them! This left only a few spaces for the people in charge of the Nativity scenes (which only tells me that God not only is in favor of religious inclusivity, but loves a good practical joke!).

Unfortunately, some folks didn’t understand the humor or the American values of religious freedom, and began vandalizing the atheists’ displays. So finally the City Council voted unanimously to ban all unattended private displays from city parks – essentially ending both the Nativity scenes and the non-Nativity ones.

What really caught my attention, however, in reading about this brouhaha, was the statement by one of the Nativity scene supporters, who said it’s a “sad day for freedom of…religion when a small group of people with an ideological ax to grind succeed in censoring the greatest story ever told.”

And I thought to myself: But this is exactly the point! While the story of the birth of Jesus – and of his entire life and death, actually – is a “great” story – but it’s only one great story; there are numerous others from other traditions throughout the year! And the government can’t decide which of these stories is “the greatest!” That’s what’s meant by “freedom of religion” in our Constitution.

My mother was a devout Episcopalian. She dragged all five of us kids to church every Sunday, and made us read the Gospel and Epistle for any Sunday we stayed home sick. She read us Bible stories when we were young, helped us say prayers at bedtime, and made sure we got confirmed in the church at the appropriate time.

But she never, ever would have suggested that Christianity was the only true faith. While I doubt she knew much about religions other than her own, I never heard her speak ill of someone else’s faith – other than perhaps when they used their faith to defend racism, homophobia or

violence. Indeed, I used to joke that she had only herself to blame for my becoming a Unitarian Universalist, because she was so open to my questions when I was young, and so convinced that the main role of religion was to spread kindness and do justice.

My mother didn’t know a lot about the festivals and stories of other religions; but I suspect she would have gotten a kick out of the Hindu “Festival of Colors,” which falls on the last full moon day of the lunar month of Phalgun – March 6th this year; and is marked by people throwing colored powder and liquids at each other! (Sounds a bit like Paintball, but not quite as messy, and without toy guns that look life-like!)

The festival is apparently especially raucous in India, where colored water is squirted on passersby, and people are dunked into muddy water. I read that people can get away with anything during Holi by simply saying, “Oh, don’t mind it; it’s Holi!”

 

One of the most treasured stories behind this festival is that of Lord Krishna and his bride, Radha. Long before they married, when they were still quite young, Krishna asked his mother why he had such dark skin, when Radha’s was so light. His mother jokingly suggested that Krishna smear some colored powder on Radha and change her skin to whatever color he’d prefer; and so he did! Thus the practice of throwing colored powder or water at people.

There is a calmer element to Holi, which my mother would have also liked. It is a time for family members to get together, exchange gifts, eat special foods and decorate their homes; sounds a lot like Christmas! It is considered to be one of the liveliest and most beloved festivals of the Hindu calendar; and the stories behind it – there are more than I have time to share this morning – are pretty great!

I imagine my mother would have appreciated the stories about Siddhartha Gautama, the prince born in Nepal in the 6th century BCE who later became known as the Buddha.

The young Siddhartha’s mother died just seven days after his birth. Legend has it that soon after his birth, some “holy men” announced that he would one day become either a political leader and unify India, or a great religious leader.

This upset his father – the King, who had wanted his son to follow in his footsteps, living a life of luxury and power. So he forbid Siddhartha to go outside the palace gardens, for fear that he would see the suffering in the world, and want to do something to fix it – like becoming a political or religious leader! Instead, he found a bride for Siddhartha by age 17, who became pregnant with their son soon after the wedding.

But that didn’t satisfy Siddhartha; he was restless to see what lay beyond the palace, and finally convinced his father to let him go out. Once out on the streets of Nepal – despite his father’s order that anyone old or disabled or poor be kept out of his son’s sight – the young man did see people in great stress and unhappiness; and he was never able to get these images out of his mind.

On his 29th birthday, Siddhartha left home, renouncing his royal life, exchanging his rich clothes for simple cloth, and becoming instead a wandering monk, seeking to find the cause of suffering and how to alleviate it.

One day, as you probably know, he settled himself beneath a Bodhi tree, vowing to himself that he would not move from there until he discovered the answers to his questions. And, according to some Buddhists, on the night of the full moon in the month of May, complete Enlightenment came to him, bringing him a sense of deep peace, an understanding of the true nature of all living beings, and an acceptance of the natural cycle of life and death. From then on he became known as the Buddha – the “Awakened One.”

Surely this is one of the “greatest stories” ever told!

There are two major festivals celebrated by Buddhists: In May is the most important one – Wesak or Buddha Day, marking both the Buddha’s birth and his Enlightenment; and on December 8th, Bodhi Day – celebrated by some as the day he found enlightenment.

The stories of the birth of Muhammad in the year 570 of the Common Era, share some interesting similarities with the birth of Jesus, which I’m sure my mother would have found quite intriguing!

Such as the story that tells us that when the prophet’s birth was near, Allah ordered the angels to open all the heavens and the doors of the skies – reminiscent of the angels “singing on high” the night of Jesus’ birth. There was even mention of a special star hovering in the sky the night he was born.

In addition, at the birth of the Muslim prophet, the mountains stood high with pride, the seas became wild, the sun shined brighter, and the devil was hung upside down in 70 chains! And more importantly, a long drought was ended by falling rain; the land turned green, and healthy crops were everywhere. Sounds like a pretty great story to me!

Many Muslims celebrate Mawlid – the commemoration of Muhammad’s birth – with street parades and by decorating their homes and mosques. They donate food and other goods to charities, or listen to their children recite poems about the prophet’s life. My mother would have liked that.

On April 5th, we’ll be reflecting on the stories of Passover and Easter. But there is another story important in Judaism that is one of my favorites: the spring festival of Purim, wherein the beautiful Queen Esther plots to save her people from the wicked Haman.

It takes place in ancient Persia, where the king has chosen Esther as his wife, not knowing that she is Jewish. At the same time, the king’s aide, Haman, becomes furious when Esther’s uncle, Mordecai, refuses one day to bow down to him; so he orders all the Jews of the kingdom to be killed.

Mordecai learns of this, and convinces Esther to let the king know she’s Jewish, and that Haman plans to kill her people; it is an incredibly courageous act on her part. When the king learns of Haman’s plans, he’s outraged, and orders that Haman be hanged – on the very gallows that Haman had built to use on Mordecai!

It is a story not only about the triumph of love and courage over evil, but also of trickery – of things suddenly turning upside down. So on Purim, people dress in outlandish costumes, and carry noise-makers so that whenever they hear Haman’s name they can drown it out.

It is by far one of the greatest and most fun stories and celebrations of all, and I’m sure that if my mother wasn’t familiar with Purim, she would have loved learning about it!

The story of the birth, life and death of Jesus is definitely a wonderful story, rich with wisdom and inspiration for us all. But it isn’t the only story that can carry meaning for us, nor is it the “greatest.” True…it is the story that most of us know and celebrate, at Christmas and, in a few weeks, at Easter. And I always look forward to both.

But I don’t want my country, or my state, or my City Council celebrating it for me, with my tax money – and I bet most of you don’t, either.

 

Because as Unitarian Universalists, we embrace the wisdom found in all religions – which is why you have hanging on the walls of your other building two framed paintings depicting the symbols of over two dozen religions from around the world.

As Unitarian Universalists, we honor the sacred traditions and rituals of all faiths; that’s why in a few weeks we will celebrate during our worship services the Spring holy days of both Passover and Easter.

And as Unitarian Universalists, we celebrate the many religious stories – including those of our own faith – told throughout the year; we know that all of them are truly some of “the greatest stories ever told.”

May we share this faith – our Good News of religious freedom and understanding – to all who might be feeling that there is no place for them. Here, may they truly know that “whoever (they) are, wanderer or worshipper,” they are welcome here.

Amen and Blessed Be.
