Spiritual Courage

© by Lora Brandis

Preached December 11, 2011

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship

 

Welcome to a church that calls itself a Fellowship. Welcome to a religion that calls itself a movement. Welcome all you spiritual but not religious people. You are welcome here.

And you are not alone. Anywhere between 10 and 33 percent of Americans call themselves SBNR, spiritual but not religious. It is their attempt to separate themselves from institutional religion, which after all doesn’t have such a great track record. It is their declaration of independence from an outside authority. SBNRs look to the findings of science and reject the creeds of the church. The spiritual but not religious folks find God in the sunsets, the sacred in walks on the beach, according to United Church of Christ pastor Lillian Daniel. “Like people who go to church don't see God in the sunset! Like we are these monastic little hermits who never leave the church building. How lucky we are to have these geniuses inform us that God is in nature. As if we don't hear that in the psalms, the creation stories and throughout our deep tradition,” she writes.

Rev. Daniel has stirred up quite a response with her sarcastic remarks. UU minister Kathy Schmitz commented that “Snarky, derisive behavior and eye-rolling may sell better, but it does not represent our best selves and it does not represent either community or religion at its best.” I agree with both my colleagues. Sarcasm divides and we have something to say to those identifying themselves as spiritual but not religious.

First of all, it is a twentieth century invention to define spiritual as something different from religious. As more people became disenchanted with the spirituality found in organized religion – supernatural gods they no longer believed in, empty rituals they no longer practiced and truth claims that couldn’t stand the test of science – they struck out on their own. They constructed personal spiritual practices that were separate from institutions. Spiritual became associated with private beliefs and practices; religious meant public. Even more so: spiritual was good and religious was bad. And so I say, welcome to a religion that helps you redefine words like God, spiritual and even religion. Welcome to all you spiritual but not religious folks out there. Welcome to a place that nurtures spiritually courageous people who transform the world through justice and compassion.

When the board of trustees and I sat with the results of the survey, the cottage conversations and the one-on-ones I have been having with you, we sat with our own hearts hoping to be open to a mission statement that came from the depths of who this community is. This community – a religious community – who when asked what spiritual and religious practices are important in your life this is how you responded: 76% responded that communing with nature is important to you. These will not add up to 100% because there is overlap; many of you have more than one spiritual practice. Sixty-five percent of you count service to others as a spiritual practice. Fifty-two percent marked creativity and artistic expression in the survey as a spiritual and religious practice. And 47% of you practice some sort of meditation.

Here is what you expect from a worship service: you want fellowship and friendship, inspiration and insight, intellectually challenging ideas and an uplifting emotional experience. Thirty-six percent of you want to recharge your spiritual batteries. But spiritual means different things to all of you. From nature to service you, like our spiritual but not religious friends, are free to explore that word. Unlike our spiritual but not religious friends, you have a cloud of witnesses to help you with that definition. We have the many sources upon which we draw to inform our spiritual journeys, define our terms and to create our living tradition.

Almost all religious traditions have a term for Spirit, the indwelling power in each of us that connects us to all that is. Practitioners of yoga call it Kundalini. The Japanese call it ki, the Chinese chi, and in Christianity it is known as the Holy Spirit. The Holy Spirit, the third person in the classic doctrine of the Trinity is that which sustains us in our daily lives. It is the sacred not as creation or as redemption; it is the sacred as connection. To experience this connection with all that is, we must first experience connection to ourselves. Perhaps this is why spirituality has become so personal. Yet to experience this connection to all that is, we must also step out of who we are and recognize the Spirit that is also in our neighbor.

Spirit is ruah, Hebrew for breath or wind. When translated to Greek it becomes pneuma. Spirit is what inspires us (breath, inspire, inspiration)… spirit is what inspires us to make meaning of our lives. Spirit, infused in religion, brings connection and inspiration into our communal worship experiences.

“The spirit,” writes poet Mary Oliver “likes to dress up like this: ten fingers, ten toes, shoulders, and all the rest.” She writes this in a poem she titles “Poem.” Of the thousands of poems Oliver has written during the last fifty years, she titles this one poem “Poem.” As if to say, “all poetry is metaphor and this one is a Metaphor with a capital M. This is a metaphor about a metaphor and it’s not the metaphor you expect it to be.” The spirit, not a real thing, is a metaphor for that which inspires and connects us to all that is. In Oliver’s “Poem” the spirit needs the metaphor of the body. We know that the body, “with [its] lime and appetite, [its] oceanic fluids” is the real “thing.” In “Poem,” Oliver reverses our expectations. It is the body that is the metaphor to the spirit, which is an “airy and shapeless thing.” The spirit needs the body and is lit up by the mornings, the awakenings of our understandings. And at night, in the dark nights of our souls, the spirit lights us up. Spirit…ruah…breath…inspiration, indwelling connection to all that is needs us as much as we need it.

Our mission is to nurture spiritually courageous people who transform the world through justice and compassion. It is not to nurture secularly courageous people or courageously spiritual people, for that matter. Quite specifically we acknowledge that the spiritual questions we ask about where the universe comes from, why we are here, or what happens when we die, require courage. When we live out of these questions without definitive answers and when we live out of these questions with a sense that our one life matters, we are called to spiritual courage.

We are called to the sort of spiritual courage that “Victim Number 1” must have been called to. Victim Number 1 is not the first apparent victim of former Penn State football coach Jerry Sandusky. But he is the first courageous young man to step forward and tell his truth about the alleged sexual abuse he suffered. Alleged, because one aspect of this truth will be determined in a court of law. Courageous, because by coming forward he started the investigation. Spiritually courageous, because his actions provide a space for his own healing. Spiritually courageous, because his speaking out may have saved other boys from becoming victims.

This is not the sort of spiritual courage we are called to every day. It is the sort of spiritual courage that can inspire us, however. Victim Number 1 is spiritually courageous. And in reading the accounts of other victims of the Penn State tragedy, there may be no other word for it but tragedy, we are reminded of our own pain, our own spiritual courage to heal and what we hope would be our own spiritual courage to speak up. We are reminded of Edmund Burke’s famous quote: “The only thing necessary for evil to triumph is for good men to do nothing.” Spiritually courageous people do something. Spiritually courageous people act. Spiritually courageous people encounter the sacred in a real human community.

Rev. Lillian Daniel’s full reflection on what it means to be spiritual but not religious appeared in the September issue of The Christian Century. It is less “snarky” than the shorter, blogged version that upset so many. In it she writes about a man who left the church he was attending after his marriage ended, feeling that the church belonged more to his former wife than it did to him. He started to sleep in on Sunday mornings and to take a run in the woods instead of attending church. He found God in the woods and realized he too, like so many, was now deeply spiritual but no longer religious.

Only about half of Unitarian Universalists attend Sunday services regularly (Michael Durall, The Almost Church Revitalized). Like the man who found God on his morning run in the woods, we often substitute communing with nature for attending church. Remember the statistic from our congregation’s survey? Seventy-six percent of this congregation responded that communing with nature is important. Spiritually courageous people attend church on a regular basis. I realize that I am preaching to the people who are attending church right now. Spiritually courageous people also tell others that they are missed when they do not attend church on a regular basis. Spiritually courageous people confront those who want to contribute something to the community, but who only show up occasionally. Spiritually courageous people inspire others to be fully present in the life of this congregation, this religious community. Spiritually courageous people know that the sacred can be found not only in the woods or in the sunset, but also in the lost job and the sick child.

The spiritual but not religious are welcome here, but they are asked to become spiritually courageous people who transform the world through justice and compassion. Here you will find comfort but you may not be comfortable. Here we will care for you but not take care of you until you are unable to care for yourself. Here we can acknowledge that Spirit dwells in each of us and when we join in religious communities sometimes we are challenged by behaviors that defy this deeply felt truth and beliefs that are different from our own.

As we travel together redefining words like God, spiritual and even religion we covenant with each other to look for the Spirit in real, human community. As spiritually courageous people we covenant to:

·         Know when we are serving our own ego and when we are serving the entire community

·         Agree to be accountable to the community when our behavior is called into question

·         Listen deeply to each other’s search for truth and meaning and to have our own search shaped by that deep listening

·         Communicate directly to someone we disagree with, taking complaints to the place they belong and refraining from gossip

·         Take responsibility for our own faith development, knowing that we are never finished

·         Attend Sunday services regularly

·         Participate in one program each year that deepens our faith

·         Participate in one outreach project each year

·         Reach the 5 to 10 percent giving level as soon as possible

·         Tell others about this church (The Almost Church Revitalized)

 

The last five on that list come from the book written by Michael Durall that inspired me and the board of trustees in our work to uncover the new mission statement. Spiritually courageous people do all of these things and more. Spiritually courageous people are welcome here. You are welcome here.
