The Art of Installation

©Lora Brandis

Preached November 13, 2011

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship

 

 It started more than a year ago. It started with carefully choosing a search committee, with completing a survey, with cottage conversations. It started with the ending of one ministry, knowing that beginnings are in endings and identity is defined through change.

The Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship started when six couples looking for a liberal religious oasis for themselves and a religious education home for their children gathered in December of 1961. We will celebrate our official 50th anniversary in February, which marks the moment the congregation joined the UUA as the Tri-Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship. Since that time, the Fellowship has grown from those six couples to more than 200 members. There have been seven part-time consulting ministers who have worked closely with lay leaders since 1967 to establish programs and provide worship. In 2000 the Fellowship called its first full-time settled minister, the Rev. Dr. Betty Stapleford. Today you will install your second full-time settled minister.

Whether you are a first-time visitor or a long-time member you can participate in this historic event. All are invited this afternoon to celebrate the birth of our new ministry. All are invited to experience how a covenantal faith calls us to the fullest expression of our deepest selves. Lorella Hess, one of the members of the ministerial search committee and the installation committee, pointed out this odd fact to me: I was ordained by my home congregation, the First Unitarian Church of Dallas on Sunday, February 13th. Just nine months later, on Sunday November 13th I am being installed as your newly settled minister. Please join us this afternoon for a celebration of my new life and our new life together: the birth of our new ministry.

Our journey towards this moment started with a search committee, a survey, cottage conversations and a process that’s a lot like online dating. I viewed your online profile. You viewed my online profile. We decided we liked what we saw and did what everyone does after a first date: we got to know each other better. We “exchanged packets” – pages and pages and pages of information designed to make us both want to continue our online romance. The online part of our romance concluded with an interview and then we decided to meet face-to-face. The whirlwind weekend we spent together went so well that we both decided to make a commitment. It was time to meet the rest of the family. After spending the week together in April, the family voted and I said yes. I have been living with you since August 15th and this afternoon we are getting married. Marriage and birth can be central to the human life cycle. They can also be appropriate imagery for the church life cycle.

Like marriage, the installation of a minister acknowledges a sacred covenant. It is a promise to be in relationship in a particular way. You will hear the echoes of marriage vows this afternoon as we make our promises to each other. Like birth, the calling of a new minister welcomes a new member into a church family.

The installation is an opportunity to re-commit to the mission of this Fellowship and the mission of Unitarian Universalism. It is also an opportunity to re-commit to something larger than both this congregation and our religious movement. It is an opportunity to reconnect to a greater sense of purpose, to “discover the community of our lives” as Parker Palmer puts it.

Knowing that beginnings are in endings and identity is defined through change, I thought today would be the perfect time to announce our new mission statement.

From the start, you made it clear that you wanted to embrace a new identity. In your congregational record, that online statement that made me want to go on our first date, this is what your ministerial search committee said about you: “While our diverse mix of people and interests is an attractive attribute, it also has produced a somewhat diffuse sense of mission and purpose. During our current period of transition, CVUUF has undertaken a profound review of who we are, how we do things, and what we want to be in the future. There is a clear desire in the congregation for a minister who will rally us around a strong sense of shared mission, vision, and values.” You said this in our start-up weekend with District Executive Ken Brown: “we want a greater sense of mission; we need a clear, inspiring mission; and we want a shared sense of mission.”

Just as you empowered your leaders on the search committee to find me, you empowered me along with the board of trustees to define our new mission. Before I announce it, let me first tell you how we discovered the new mission. Better said, let me tell you how we uncovered the new mission. You remember the survey and the cottage conversations that helped write the online identity that I fell in love with? Just a few weeks ago, the board and I uncovered our new mission there in your words.

We found the new mission in statements that you made about what this church means to you. We found the new mission arising naturally from stories of how this church has changed you. And we found the new mission in the stories you told us about why you came here in the first place, and why you stayed. Here are some of the things you told us:

“It’s a place where you’re encouraged to find yourself.”

“I came for RE, for a place where my child could be different and not suffer for it, could ask questions without fear. I didn’t expect it, but I found good friends for myself. There are good people here doing good work.”

“There is a tremendous community, a level of connection to people that I don’t find in other places and parts of my life. They are very caring people and I feel a part of it.”

“I like being opened up to alternative ways to think about things.”

“A place to bring children that is spiritual but without dogma.”

“I joined originally for more liberal values in a conservative community primarily for my kids. The truth is I have been the most involved one in my family.”

“Faith in Action is our commitment to be there when important things come up - like Prop 8 - and we line up with people to make a difference in the world.”

“Having a place for others to discover that they are not alone with their UU values and can actively participate and practice their values in this community.”

“I value the fellowship, members who basically stand on the same ground and look for a meaningful life.”

“I like the whole mix of seeing everyone else happily involved and finding meaning in their lives and making a difference in the lives of others.”

The board and I listened to what was said last year and what was captured by the survey. I listened to what you said to me in the one-on-one conversations we’ve been having. We read other Unitarian Universalist mission statements. We paid attention to our Association’s tagline: “Nurture your spirit. Help heal the world.” We also took to heart these words from UU author and church consultant Michael Durall: “…the language of a congregation’s mission or vision statement is vitally important if that congregation genuinely wishes to live out its call to ministry. A mission statement reveals the nature and character of that congregation, its place in the world, and what the future might hold” (The Almost Church Revitalized, p. 81).

Before I tell you our new mission, I’d like all of you to turn your order of service over and look at the old mission statement. Now get out a pen or a pencil, you may have to borrow one from your neighbor, and cross through the old mission statement. Write this down: The mission of the Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship is to “nurture spiritually courageous people who transform the world through justice and compassion.” That’s it. Twelve words. These 12 words will guide everything we do together from this day forward. These 12 words ask us to dream, not to wish. These 12 words ask us to take responsibility for ourselves and for the world we will leave our children. Take these 12 words home with you today, put them on your refrigerator door, pin them to a bulletin board. Look at them every day and start to live from this mission, our mission that calls us to our shared ministry.

Writer, teacher and activist Parker Palmer points out that [Frederick] Buechner’s  definition of vocation “starts with the self and moves toward the needs of the world: it begins, wisely, where vocation begins – not in what the world needs (which is everything), but in the nature of the human self, in what brings the self joy, the deep joy of knowing that we are here on earth to be the gifts that God created” (Let Your Life Speak, p. 16-17). Just as true vocation starts with the self and moves toward the needs of the world, our mission starts with the self – the spiritually courageous individual – and moves toward the needs of the world: justice and compassion.

Henry Hampton says that Unitarian Universalists don’t dream – they wish. I think this can be said about most people. Wishing comes from starting with what we think the world needs. Dreaming comes when we start with our own healing, our own joy and discover how our true, authentic self can create the world as we would really have it. When we start with what the world needs we can sometimes ignore our own needs, trying to fix the world from the “hole” in our own soul. When we do this there is a disconnection between our deep knowing and the work we do in the world. Finding true vocation, one’s personal mission, can be as easy as is like breathing in and breathing out. We breathe in: this is the discovery of the self. We breathe out; this is our effort to share what we have discovered with the world. Breathing in and breathing out, the body is whole. Breathing in we nurture our own spirit, breathing out we share our spirit with the world.

Our mission – to nurture spiritually courageous people who transform the world through justice and compassion – welcomes all who walk though these doors. Whether you are here for your children or here for yourself, or think you’re here for your children and then discover you are here for yourself, we welcome you to a spiritually courageous life. Whether you stand on the corner carrying a sign or stand in the kitchen cooking for the homeless, we welcome you to transform the world through justice and compassion. We welcome you who come through these doors looking for safe harbor during the storms of your life and invite you to ask with us “the question of selfhood and answer it as honestly as [you] can, no matter where it takes [you]” knowing, “Only as we do so can we discover the community of our lives” (Palmer).

The next time someone asks you what church you belong to, tell them you belong to a church that “nurtures spiritually courageous people who transform the world through justice and compassion.” Incorporate our mission into your life. Measure our success not in numbers, but by how well we travel from self to service, how well we understand that your vocation and our mission start with the self and move toward the needs of the world.
