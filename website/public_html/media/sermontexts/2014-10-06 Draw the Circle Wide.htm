Draw the Circle Wide

© Rev. Lora Brandis

Preached at the Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship

October 6, 2013

 

The circle is a widely-used religious symbol, one that implies the movement of time just as the planets move around the sun. The circle is often invoked as a symbol at weddings when the rings are exchanged: the ring as an unbroken circle, a symbol of unending love that will continue through the end of time. Circular temples, the whirling dervishes, the circling of the Arab pilgrims around the Ka’aba reflect the movement of the universe. A circle also symbolizes the continual development of creation. As an image of that which has no beginning and no end, the circle symbolizes eternity.

Circles also define. We say the “inner circle” knowing there can be an invisible line drawn to define an in-group and an out-group. There are the Confucian concentric circles of compassion, emanating out from the family to eventually encompass the entire community. There is the sacred circle created intentionally by the gathered community. Some religious ceremonies include the actual creation of a circle – the drawing of a magical circle – a protective measure to keep dangerous powers out and to hold love in.

Our task as human beings, according to Albert Einstein, is to free ourselves from the delusion of being outside of the circle of the entire universe. The delusion of separateness is a sort of prison. In Einstein’s words, “Our task must be to free ourselves from this prison by widening our circle of compassion, to embrace all living creatures and the whole of nature in its beauty." Our task is to draw the circle wide.

To accomplish this task we need the guidance of those who have gone before us, the sages and the prophets who have left the comfort of the world they once knew, embarking on the hero’s journey to the center of the self. Joseph Campbell said that “There is a certain typical hero sequence of actions which can be detected in stories from all over the world and from many periods of history.” The hero decides to leave home to seek answers to life’s big questions. Along the way the hero will experience loss, pain, rejection and danger. The archetypal mythic hero teaches us how to leave the comfort of home, to expand our circle of compassion and how to be the hero of our own life.

The story of the prodigal son is not usually told as a story of the archetypal mythic hero. It is usually told as a cautionary tale: don’t be a squanderer like the prodigal son. Sure your father will still love you, but really it’s bad to leave home and spend all of your inheritance on loose living. I really think the prodigal son has gotten a bad rap.

The prodigal son asks his father to give him now what he would someday inherit, which is not the same as his older brother would, by the way. In the time when this story was first told, the older son would have inherited the bulk of the estate. The most the youngest son could inherit would be one third. The Biblical text indicates that in response to his youngest son’s request, the father actually gave both sons their inheritance ahead of time which meant he no longer owned anything. The father would have to rely on his oldest son to live. This is an outrageous request, with an even more outrageous response from the father.

Maybe the father trusted his son or maybe he is the ideal, forgiving, father who loves without condition. This is the father who traditional Christianity wants us to name as God. In naming the father in this story God there were interpretations by the early church that have led some scholars to wonder if Jesus actually told the story. The early church liked to tell the story because they felt the younger son represented the church and the older son represented the synagogue; the father shows his preference for the younger son, just as God the father made a new covenant which replaced the old.

This interpretation from the early church is not only dangerous; it also ignores the context of the story. The parable of the prodigal is one of three parables told that defend Jesus’ habit of hanging out with “sinners:” the bad people, the marginalized, the people “not like us.” The people on the outside of the circle. Those people are the same sort that the youngest son hung out with when he went away. Most of us leave the nest, the dovecote, the comfort of home. Most of us have felt our “wings pass through all distance and fear in the course of their (our) wanderings,” in the words of the poet Rilke.

This is not always such a bad thing and I think Jesus knew this when he told the story, if he told the story because the scholars remind us we don’t really know. Ultimately it doesn’t matter who told the story because it is one of those timeless stories that reaches beyond fact into the realm of mythology, into a universal truth that becomes known through experience. We all must leave home and set out on a quest to be the founder of our own life. Again, in the words of the poet Rilke:

Dove that ventured outside, flying far

            from the dovecote:

Housed and protected again, one with

the day, the night,

knows what serenity is, for she has felt

            her wings

pass through all distance and fear

in the course of her wanderings.

The parable of the prodigal son appears in only one of the canonical gospels – the Gospel According to Luke. Luke’s gospel is known as the gospel of the poor. When the youngest son spent all of his money, he was poor. When the famine came he was hungry. So hungry that he was willing to eat what the pigs ate. So hungry he was willing to return to his father’s house as a servant. He spent his father’s inheritance alright. He gave up a life of comfort and security and learned what it is to experience loss. Like the Buddha who left his princely life, like Jesus who went off to the desert, like Muhammad who withdrew for a time apart, the prodigal son goes on a quest. This quest is usually judged as the over-indulgent wasteful living of a spoiled frat boy, but it is a quest none-the-less and the prodigal returns changed by where he has been.

I defend the prodigal son because I know that spending his father’s inheritance is not just about spending money. It’s also about losing the parts of his parents’ inheritance that are not his to keep. We inherit so much more from our parents than money; some of us, like the poor in Jesus’ stories, will never inherit any money from our parents. All of us do inherit thoughts and attitudes, ways of being and sometimes we even inherit a religion. The youngest son loses the part of his father’s inheritance that would have hardened his heart to the plight of the poor and the hungry. The youngest son wins back his own heart, the won-back heart of Rilke’s poem, because of all he was willing to give up. Part of what he gave up, he gave up when he came home. The prodigal returns willing to humble himself before his father. The prodigal returns no longer thinking only of himself and his self-preservation.

The end of the story of the prodigal is open-ended. The older son, the dutiful one, stands at the threshold deciding whether he will join the party or not. We do not know what kind of man the prodigal will become, now that he is no longer the “prodigal,” the recklessly extravagant one. The family is restored, but it will never be the same because what was once lost has now been found. Heavier by the weight of where he has been, the hero returns.

Thousands of would-be heroes leave their homes every day for the promise of a new life. Theirs is not the conventional pattern of the mythical hero which, according to Joseph Campbell, always includes departure, fulfillment and return. Departure comes when a migrant leaves seeking work to support a family. Departure also comes when a political refugee leaves seeking asylum.

There are countless reasons for leaving home including to become a migrant worker or to flee religious, political, and social persecution. Many people leave home because of the effects of climate change. There is a couple trying to stay in New Zealand because their country is disappearing under a rising ocean. The man and his wife are “from Kiribati, a remote chain of atolls in the Pacific halfway between Hawaii and Australia. It is one of the lowest lying nations on Earth and as such, it is one of the first nations that will likely disappear as ocean levels rise. Kiribati has a population of about 103,000 people, and is one of the poorest countries in the world.” (http://motherboard.vice.com/blog/a-man-in-new-zealand-is-seeking-asylum-from-climate-change) The 37-year-old man and his wife moved to New Zealand six years ago on work visas, and have had three kids while living there. New Zealand immigration authorities have already rejected the claim of this couple as climate change refugees.

The UN High Commission for Refugees defines a refugee as someone with a “well-founded fear of being persecuted for reasons of race, religion, nationality, membership of a particular social group or political opinion,” and “is outside the country of his nationality, and is unable to, or owing to such fear, is unwilling to avail himself of the protection of that country.” This definition does not include climate change refugees. The case of the couple from Kiribati could be precedent-setting. The UN estimates that as global temperatures continue to rise there could be as many as 330 million people displaced world-wide. Their definition of refugee will need to change.

The conventional pattern of the mythical hero is departure, fulfillment and return. The unconventional pattern of the everyday immigrant hero sometimes includes fulfillment. It also often includes enslavement. Undocumented immigrant workers are subject to abusive working conditions because of the threat of deportation. Industries that employ large numbers of immigrant workers, like poultry processing and meat packing often do not pay employees for all the time they work and expose workers to unsafe working conditions. An alarming statistic from the U.S. Department of Labor shows that immigrants from Mexico are killed on their jobs in the U.S. at a rate 75% higher than that of workers who were born here.

Departure, fulfillment and return. There are 11 million undocumented people living in our country. Even as our government has been shut down this week, the deportations have continued. Families live in constant fear, never knowing when ICE will come and take parents away leaving children in an already crowded foster care system. Often immigrant families are separated for decades without a legal path for unification. Undocumented young adults are often deported to foreign lands they left as infants, deported to places they do not know and to languages they do not speak. Are they heroes or criminals? All of us are heroes, according to Joseph Campbell. The hero’s adventure is the adventure of being alive. The hero’s reward, the treasure unearthed during this adventure, is compassion.

In dreams, the circle is a symbol of the Self and indicates the end of the process of individuation, of striving towards a psychic wholeness and self-realization – the hero’s journey ending with a return to home, a return to self. In religious symbolism the circle represents eternity, the ongoing act of creation. Our task as human beings, according to Albert Einstein, is to free ourselves from the delusion of being outside of the circle of the entire universe. The delusion of separateness is a sort of prison. In Einstein’s words, “Our task must be to free ourselves from this prison by widening our circle of compassion, to embrace all living creatures and the whole of nature in its beauty." Our task is to draw the circle wide.
