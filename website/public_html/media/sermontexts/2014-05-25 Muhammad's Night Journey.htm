Muhammad’s Night Journey

© Rev. Lora Brandis

Preached at Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship
May 25, 2014

 

Muhammad’s Night Journey reverses the typical pattern from Arabic poetry that depicts the poet embarking on a night journey and returning joyfully to his tribe. The Prophet Muhammad’s journey to Jerusalem and his Ascension to Heaven describe an archetypal truth rather than a factual truth. It is a journey beyond ego to reunification with the whole human family. It is a journey toward concern for everybody.

It is a story that, as a Christian raised in a culture dominated by stories from the Hebrew and Christian Bibles, I do not know well. It is a story that, as a Unitarian Universalist minister educated in a liberal Christian seminary, I do not draw upon to inform my own faith journey. I am familiar with the story of the Hijrah – the flight of Muhammad and his followers from Mecca to Medina to escape persecution. I am familiar with some of the stories from Muhammad’s life including how he was orphaned at a young age and raised at first by his grandfather and then by his uncle. I know that it is said that he encountered the Angel Gabriel in a cave and received the words of the Quran directly from God, that his wife Khadija – a strong and independent business woman – was the first person to convert and is known as the mother of Islam. And I know the headlines of Muslim extremists as terrorists.

I did not know that there is a story from the tradition of Muhammad’s Night Journey, a mystical experience of the Prophet that is referred to in the Quran and given more detail in the oral tradition about Muhammad’s life. In the journey, Muhammad travels to Jerusalem – the holy city of the Jews and Christians – where he leads the great prophets of the past in prayer. He then ascends through the seven heavens where he speaks to God, who gives Muhammad instructions to take back to the faithful regarding the details of prayer. This story is one of the most important underlying establishments of belief behind Islam. The founding of this new religion included a physical, psychological and emotional break from the tribal ego of the past. Muhammad’s Night Journey gives context to the Sufi mystics like Rumi who wrote:  “Not Christian or Jew or Muslim, not Hindu, Buddhist, sufi, or zen. Not any religion or cultural system. I belong to the beloved, have seen the two worlds as one and that one call to and know, first, last, outer, inner, only that breath breathing human being.” 

Cordoba was the capital city of Al-Andalus, a medieval Islamic state that included most of what is now Spain, Portugal and part of Southern France. Cordoba was the capital during a time known as “La Convivencia” or the co-existence, a time when Muslims, Christians and Jews lived in relative peace. With this rich history in mind, Imam Feisal proposed that a Islamic Community Center known as “Cordoba House” be built in lower Manhattan just two blocks away from the World Trade Center site. Imam Feisal dreamed that a center to promote interfaith dialogue could embody a shared history of co-existence and could also reflect the pluralistic promise of America.

Instead, Cordoba House stirred up controversy and prejudice rather than cooperation and co-existence. Part of the community center was to have been set aside for a large auditorium to accommodate Muslim prayer and so the project became known as the “Ground Zero Mosque.” Now, a few years after the media storm and public protests have faded, the project has been renamed Park51, a less evocative name that simply reflects the building’s address, and Imam Feisal has been removed as the chief spokesman and fund raiser. There are no longer plans to create a center to promote interfaith dialogue. The current project developer would like to someday tear down the existing building and create a museum of Islamic culture. Meanwhile, the space was opened to the public with an exhibit that featured 160 portraits of immigrant children living in New York. A small carpeted prayer room is located in the lower level.

Eboo Patel, founder and president of Interfaith Youth Core, writes that “The strangest part of the Cordoba House debate for me was the idea of sacred ground. The people opposed to Cordoba House insisted that the blocks around Ground Zero constituted a holy area…. I believe every inch of America is sacred, from sea to shining sea” (p. xxviii).

Driving to the high desert this past Thursday to meet with men held in Adelanto Detention Center I thought of Eboo Patel’s words. We were about to meet with four men who were being held by ICE, our government’s immigration and customs enforcement agency, to let them know they have friends in the outside world. Two of the four are seeking asylum. Both of the asylum-seekers are from African countries and both are Muslim.

We drove through the holy land of the high desert, noticing the beautiful sea of yucca in bloom along our way. Eboo Patel believes we make America holy “by who we welcome and by how we relate to each other.” He quotes the Qur’an: “We made you different nations and tribes that you may come to know one another.” Patel continues to believe in a vision of America as holy even though we build false oases in the desert to house those who would be welcomed. Patel’s Islam teaches him to have concern for everybody. Patel’s faith teaches him that pluralism is possible.

Eboo Patel points out that Harvard University scholar Diana Eck makes a key distinction between diversity and pluralism: “diversity is simply the fact of people from different backgrounds living in close quarters” he writes (p. 70). Pluralism is the intentional commitment to make diversity work. Patel’s Interfaith Youth Core was created to make diversity work by creating a society built on mutual respect for people’s religious identities, constructive relationships between people of different religions and “common action for the common good” (p. 71).

As Unitarian Universalists we pride ourselves on being the religion that affirms there are many paths to the holy, but when it comes to Islam few of us walk that particular path. We identify as UU Christians, UU Jews, UU Buddhists, UU Pagans, humanists and atheists. We have yoga practices and meditation practices and create rituals that follow the cycles of the seasons. Few of us identify as UU Muslims. Our religion has its roots in the Abrahamic religions - Christianity, Judaism and Islam – but I believe we know less about Islam than we do about the other two Abrahamic faiths. We know in our minds that all religions are to be respected, but in our hearts we still doubt.

It is commonsense to conclude that the more experience you have with people of other faiths, the more you are able to see the individual behind the belief. It is also commonsense to conclude that if you are educated about other religious traditions that the knowledge gained will help you to develop tolerance and even compassion for those whose practices are different from your own. It is not only experience and information that matter, however.

A recent Gallup Poll makes it clear that what type of experience you have and what type of information you know make the most difference for interfaith interactions. From Patel’s book Sacred Ground: “According to a recent Gallup Poll, only 2 percent of Americans say they have a great deal of knowledge about Buddhism, and 14 percent report feeling some prejudice towards Buddhists. Meanwhile, only 3 percent of Americans claim they have a great deal of knowledge about Islam, yet 43 percent claim some prejudice towards Muslims. (p. 78).

Even as liberal religionists we are subjected to the images of Islam as violent. We quote Rumi and invite liberal Muslims to share our pulpits, but we don’t know all the stories or have an exact definition for terms like “sharia,” “dawa,” and “taqiyya” so that we can counter anti-Islamic sentiment. In contrast, many of us understand Buddhist terms like metta, karma and bodhisattva. To be good interfaith partners, it is time for us to have encounters and knowledge that help us stand up to the 43 percent of Americans who claim some prejudice towards Muslims.

Dawa is a concept that promotes education about Islam. It is not, as purveyors of Islamophobia would have us believe, a concept that promotes Islamic domination. Taqiyya is a little-used practice that allows Muslims to hide their true motives if someone wishes them harm. It is not a core belief that requires Muslims to always lie and hide their true intentions.

“Sharia translates as ‘the path to a watering place.’” According to Patel, “In a religious context, the term refers to various abstract values like the importance of life, religion, and education as well as more practical matters dealing with prayer times and funeral rites” (p. 49). Sharia has counterparts in Catholic and Jewish teachings. For example, Catholics have their own way of applying what they consider to be moral law when it comes to life matters such as abortion and divorce. Jewish religious law, known as Halacha, is similar to sharia in that it governs how Jews believe and behave as Jews under Jewish religious law.

Sharia is not just about the high profile cases of women being killed for adultery, even if these horrifying acts are often the first image that comes to mind for many of us when we hear the term. The path to a watering place – imagery from a desert bound people who know what it is to thirst.

“The journey of love is a very long journey, but sometimes with a sigh you can cross the vast desert,” wrote the Muslim poet and philosopher Muhammad Iqbal. The two Muslim men we met with this week crossed more than vast deserts to present themselves at our country’s border, only to be immediately handcuffed and taken into custody.


A post-911 America is not kind to asylum-seekers, especially to Muslims. Understandably we are afraid; we are cautious. The Tsarnaev brothers, who are suspected of perpetrating the Boston Marathon bombings, were Chechen Muslims. Granted asylum, the older brother was a permanent US resident at the time of the bombings.  Dzhokhar, the younger brother, became a naturalized citizen just seven months prior to the bombings.

Out of our fear we are building false oases in the desert, creating diverse communities of immigrants from Congo, Ghana, El Salvador, Mexico, Honduras, Jamaica and a host of other countries. Diversity is simply the fact of people from different backgrounds living in close quarters, we are told. Pluralism is the intentional commitment to make diversity work.

If all of America is sacred ground, then the cramped quarters of an immigrant detention center is just as holy as the rest of our country. The promise of America waits in unexpected places, made holy by who we welcome and how we relate to each other. The promise of America waits for us to affirm and extend an invitation beyond ego to reunification with the whole human family.

One of the men I met with was from war-torn Congo. He told the story of his journey which, in his words, included much walking, walking, walking, and suffering, suffering, suffering. He walked across Africa, travelled to Europe and then on to Brazil, up through South and Central America on to Mexico where he presented himself at the border seeking asylum and he was immediately handcuffed and taken into custody.

Hoping to lighten his mood, I asked him if he’d been outside yet last Thursday when we visited and if he knew that it was raining.  While we were locked inside for our first visit it had started to rain and each time a visitor emerged into the waiting room there was a shared exclamation of “It’s raining!”

The man from Congo had not seen the rain that day, but he wanted me to know the story of Moses wandering in the desert with the people complaining about the lack of rain. Three times they sent Moses to God to ask for rain and still, no rain. The third time that Moses came back to the people from God a voice arose from the crowd saying, “It’s me. I am the one who has sinned. I am the one who is broken and made mistakes in my past.” The skies opened. It rained. The man was forgiven. The healing could begin.

Now, every time it rains I will know that someone somewhere has been forgiven. Someone somewhere is being healed and welcomed back into our human family.

We need rain. We need forgiveness. This journey beyond ego to reunification with the whole human family is a long one. This journey toward concern for everybody is a long one. “The journey of love is a very long journey, but sometimes with a sigh you can cross the vast desert.”
