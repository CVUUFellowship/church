A Gay Wedding, A Verdict, and A Get Out of Hell Free Card

© By Rev. Lora Brandis

Preached August 4, 2013

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship

 

Prior to vacation I announced three rules to all who were about to visit me this year in California: 1. No discussing religion; 2. No discussing politics and 3. No mention of my messy apartment. The third rule was the easiest to keep; we didn’t stay long at my messy apartment, leaving almost immediately to explore California. I didn’t put the first rule in place out of some need to honor the guidelines of polite society. My work is religion. I discuss religion pretty much every day. I was going on a vacation from religion, therefore rule number one. Rule number two was put in place for much the same reason. As a Unitarian Universalist my religious life is informed by seeking justice in this world, not hoping for it to arrive in some ethereal next world. I breathe in the issues of the day, hoping to breathe out peace for myself, peace for those I serve and peace for the broken world. I was going on a vacation from politics, too.

Every time I broke one of the rules, my sister was quick to point it out. I broke the first two rules immediately. As we set out on the road to Northern California, immigration came up as both a religious and political issue as we drove past the farm fields just yards from where I live. I broke the rules again when I tried to talk about my daughters, who were still reeling from their days spent protesting for women’s reproductive health at the capitol in Austin, where they both live.

We were sitting in a hotel room in San Francisco when the verdict in the George Zimmerman trial was announced on the nightly news. The rules I had failed to establish didn’t matter anymore; there is no vacation from being human. A sadness settled over us as we prepared to find a restaurant for dinner.


“The prosecution never really had a case,” one of us said. “George Zimmerman will have to live with the fact that he took another’s life for the rest of his life,” another of us said. “Just another casualty of our gun culture in America.” “Just another reminder of the legacy of slavery.” “Well, the verdict was decided. Our justice system has spoken.”

This conversation may have continued at dinner, despite my vacation rules. This conversation may have continued, except that it was a very uncomfortable one without any clear consensus. This conversation may have continued, except for the fact that we had no dinner reservations. “Forced” to sit at the chef’s table at a restaurant, we sat at a counter not conducive to having much of a conversation. Sitting, watching the restaurant’s meals being prepared, the four of us stared ahead at the kitchen staff and the issue of race in America stared back at us. The staff preparing our dinner were all people of color, mostly Hispanic, with one Black gentleman preparing the desserts.

Like librarian and book blogger Joy Weese Moll, I am uncomfortable joining in the cry of “I Am Trayvon Martin.” I understand the need for a call to solidarity, but I also wonder if it might be better for a White, middle-aged woman to examine what it means when I acknowledge that I am not Trayvon Martin. I like to think I am not George Zimmerman either, but then who exactly am I in this conversation about race in America and what should I say? Moll suggests that white women over the age of 50 lead another rallying cry for change with this alternative: "I Am Not Afraid of Trayvon Martin." (http://www.huffingtonpost.com/joy-weese-moll/i-am-not-afraid-of-trayvon-martin_b_3616863.html)

When I see a black man while I’m in my car, I will not reach for the automatic lock button. When I enter an elevator and there is a young black man as the only other occupant I will not clutch my purse tightly to my side. I will not assume there is danger where there is none.


I will take responsibility for the assumptions I make based on race and based on fear. I will not stand here in a hoodie and tell you that “I Am Trayvon Martin” or that I have the potential to become a George Zimmerman, but I will tell you that “I Am Not Afraid of Trayvon Martin,” hoping that this rallying cry will start to make the change needed in our conversation about race in this country.

Joy Weese Moll writes that, “The best antidote to fear is education. Read The Warmth of Other Suns by Isabel Wilkerson, [the story of the migration of almost six million black Americans who fled the South from 1915 to 1970] Why Are All the Black Kids Sitting Together in the Cafeteria by Beverly Daniel Tatum, and The New Jim Crow by Michelle Alexander [a book we have studied together as a congregation and as a faith movement].”

Beverly Daniel Tatum is a psychologist and president of Spelman College, the nation’s oldest institution of higher education for Black women. In her book, Tatum answers the question “Why Are All the Black Kids Sitting Together in the Cafeteria?” along with other questions about race and identity. And she helps this middle aged White woman better understand what it means to be White without carrying the guilt for an entire racial group. She explains that the human development task for Whites is to “develop a positive White identity based in reality, not on assumed superiority.” According to Tatum, I am to become more aware of my Whiteness, accept it and learn to feel good about it “not in the sense of a Klan member’s ‘White pride,’ but in the context of a commitment to a just society.” She goes on to write that in the classes  and workshops she has taught on race, “Most of the White people I talk to either have not thought about their race and so don’t feel anything, or have thought about it and felt guilt and shame. These feelings of guilt and shame are part of the hidden costs of racism” (p. 94).

So why are all the Black kids sitting together in the cafeteria? This is mostly a phenomenon that begins at adolescence, the time when most young people are beginning to define their identity based on the group to which they belong. At first all of our young children take in the messages from a culture that values Whiteness. As the child grows she begins to understand that her racial identity will have an impact on her future. Who better understands her encounters with the realities of racism than other adolescent peers experiencing similar realities? Black kids sit together in the cafeteria, just as White kids, Hispanic kids, other kids of self-identified groups because they need each other in response to a society that defines what normal means. And living into society’s definition of normal, Whiteness being the dominant cultural norm, is ultimately costly to us all.

But aren’t things getting better, you might ask? Aren’t we in a post-racial era now? Aren’t we standing on the right side of history now that we’ve elected a Black man to the presidency of the United States?  In his remarks after the verdict, President Barack Obama acknowledged that yes things are getting better, but we’re not in a post-racial society yet. When Trayvon Martin was first shot and killed the president remarked that Trayvon could have been his son. In his more recent remarks, he reflected that the boy who was killed that night by George Zimmerman could have been him 35 years ago. He reminded us that it is important to acknowledge that the African American community sees the events that happened in Florida through a set of “experiences and a history that doesn’t go away.” Then he said this, “There are very few African American men who haven't had the experience of walking across the street and hearing the locks click on the doors of cars. That happen[ed] to me -- at least before I was a senator.”


It is not an exaggeration to link this common experience to what happened the night that Trayvon Martin was killed. It is not exaggeration to see this case in the context of a justice system that is infused with the historic message that Whiteness is the norm to which everything is compared, that Whiteness is superior.

If we hadn’t been forced to sit at a counter facing our meal being prepared rather than facing each other the night the verdict was announced, I am not sure the conversation would have continued. It might have been pointed out that we were breaking my rules again, this time as a way of avoiding the discomfort with the conversation about race in America. In the end my rules were pointless, there is no vacation from religion or politics, there is no avoiding the realities of being human – no vacation from being human. Ours is a religion that does not offer an escape from life, but instead promises that we can be more fully alive when we face life as it is.

There is no “get out of hell free card,” even though I could have picked one up on the second half of my vacation.  On the second half of my vacation I attended San Diego’s Comic-Con International with my family and about 150,000 fans attending this annual explosion of the popular arts. It attracts fans of comic books, science fiction/fantasy books and science fiction film/television, horror, animation, anime, collectible card games, video games, webcomics, and independent artists who interpret all of the above. It also attracts an odd collection of street preachers, some holding signs like “God Destroys All Who Forsake Him” and signs condemning abortion, homosexuality and Islam (together, on one sign) as the sources of evil in this country. There are a variety of preachers from in your face, condemning you to hell to those quietly passing out “get out of hell free cards” with instructions on the back to believe in Jesus and the Bible as a means to escape the fiery pits.


Street preachers are a tradition at this decades-old event and in recent years counter-protesters have begun appearing next to these self-righteous evangelists trying to save people from hell. One woman held a hastily, handmade sign with the words “Episcopalians welcome nerds.” The atheists were there with professionally produced signs which read, “Religion the ultimate placebo effect” and “These people are going to hell for annoying you at Comic Con.”

Comic Con is meant to be a surreal experience, a culture unto itself. It is an experience that promotes instant intimacy while standing on line for hours with people you’ve never met before. United in a common love for Doctor Who, Sherlock, Firefly, Wolverine, Battle Star Gallactica and yes, even Snoopy, you bond for a short time with other humans at play. So to the young man holding the sign that said, “Don’t be comic conned, your life is not fiction, get real with Jesus,” I have this to say: “It’s my vacation and you’re breaking the rules!”

Here is my postscript on my postcard to you from my vacation. PS: My colleague Theresa Novak and her long-time partner Anne Spatola, while driving from Utah to California for their vacation, decided to get married. It just so happened that they were vacationing near where we were. On our way from Napa to San Francisco my husband Frank and I stopped off for a brief legal ceremony to fulfill Theresa’s Facebook request for a colleague in the area to marry them. I officiated, Frank was the witness and Theresa wrote a poem for the occasion, which she read from her I-phone. Frank was also the official event photographer. Anne and Theresa will have a formal wedding in January with their four adult children present, but wanted the legal protection that California now offers them as a married couple before that date.

That morning as we were getting ready (luckily Frank always travels with a suit), Frank remarked how good it felt to be on the right side of history. Being on the right side of history is a tricky business, I reminded him, because history is often written by the “winners.” There we were again, breaking the rules, talking about religion and politics – humans at play, making our way on the road towards something new.
