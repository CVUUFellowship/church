Stepping Out Into Empathy

© Rev. Lora Brandis

Preached at the Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship

December 8, 2013

Unitarian Universalist Community of Cambria

January 26, 2014

 

Last week children in our religious education classes stepped into other people’s shoes. Director of Religious Education Justine Fischer took a trip to the Goodwill Store and bought several pairs of shoes for the children to try on. This was an opportunity for our children to try out what it is to live from this Native American prayer, “Great Spirit, grant that I may not criticize my neighbor until I have walked a mile in his moccasins.”

Rather than literally walking a mile in the ill-fitting, adult-sized shoes, the children imagined who the person was that once owned those shoes, now donated to the Goodwill Store, now no longer needed by the person who had once worn them. Our children learned what Atticus Finch in the great American novel To Kill a Mockingbird tried to teach his children, "You never really understand a person until you consider things from his point of view - until you climb into his skin and walk around in it."

Sympathy, compassion and empathy are often used interchangeably. Sympathy is a general alignment with another’s feelings. Compassion connotes a deep sympathy with another’s sorrow, implying the desire to alleviate their suffering. “But what is empathy?”  Professor Roman Krznaric asks and then answers this way, “It’s the ability to step into the shoes of another person, aiming to understand their feelings and perspectives, and to use that understanding to guide our actions.” (http://greatergood.berkeley.edu/article/item/six_habits_of_highly_empathic_people1#)

We make assumptions when we see the shoes of another person – assumptions about gender, class, race, education, occupation, and many others. Krznaric outlines the six habits of highly empathic people and helps us step out of our assumptions into an empathy that we can continue to develop throughout our lives.

We need only practice the following six habits: 1. Cultivate curiosity about strangers, 2. Challenge prejudices and discover commonalities, 3. Try another person’s life, 4. Listen hard – and open up, 5. Inspire mass action and social change, and 6. Develop an ambitious imagination.

 I remember the first time my family encountered a transgender woman in church. I remember because my pre-teen daughter leaned over and whispered, “Do you think that’s a man?” A hard question to answer anywhere, but certainly made more difficult by the situation in which we found ourselves: the quiet of the Sanctuary before the service begins and the liberal church that supposedly teaches tolerance of difference. My daughter’s curiosity was genuine, her application of it made less compassionate by her innocent ignorance. Practicing the six habits of highly empathic people takes work.

Nina Here Nor There: My Journey Beyond Gender is Nick Krieger’s memoir of his journey from Nina to Nick, a journey that teaches all three of the concepts of sympathy, compassion and empathy in a way that makes us understand why these terms are not interchangeable. Beginning with our own curiosity, to enter the world beyond gender we must learn a new vocabulary. We learn words like “cisgender,” which is a label for individuals who have a match between the gender they were assigned at birth, their bodies, and their personal identity.

The world beyond gender is a world that few of us can understand, but Nick helps us navigate it with humor when he writes, “I envisioned the somber introductory music of a special with Barbara Walters, the voiceover of her inquisitive rasp: ‘Tonight, we have cisgender women – girls who wear dresses as children, dresses as teenagers, and dresses as adults – how do they do it?’” (p. 193).

We can imagine the shoes that go along with these dresses and begin to understand that Nick as Nina was never comfortable in the shoes that were assigned to her at birth…him at birth. Gender pronouns become confining in this new world. Curious about the stranger sitting next to us on the bus, in the pew, in the theatre, we can also be kind. We can refrain from using gender pronouns, or once our relationship develops past that of a stranger, we can ask. There is the gender neutral “ze,” which some prefer. We can ask.

Our Unitarian Universalist tradition teaches us habit number two with the words and deeds of prophetic women and men who have come before us. Habit number two - “Challenge prejudices and discover commonalities” is invoked over and over again throughout our history in our quest to bend the moral arc of the universe toward justice. More than 150 years ago Unitarian and Transcendentalist Margaret Fuller wrote, “Male and female represent the two sides of the great radical dualism. But, in fact, they are perpetually passing into one another. There is no wholly masculine man, no purely feminine woman.” These words continue to challenge our prejudices and preconceptions. Yet, when we translate these words into Nina’s world, into today’s world, we find ourselves confronted with the second part of this habit: discover commonalities.

Personally, I am more able to feel empathy for Nina’s mother. When Nina’s mother comes to visit for a week, she comes to help Nina recover from surgery. She is there to help Nina recover from “top surgery,” the removal of her breasts. By the last day of her visit to help her daughter recover from this life-altering surgery, Nina’s mother returns home from a walk in the famous Castro neighborhood in San Francisco. Sitting down in the living room she asks, “Is everyone in the Castro gay?”

“What makes you think people are gay?” I teased.

“I don’t know. The hand holding, the couples. I can’t be sure. But they are, right?”

“Yes, mom. I was just giving you a hard time.”

“I’ve never been anywhere like it,” she said. “It’s just different is all.” (p. 196)

 

This mother/daughter dialogue could happen anywhere, in almost any geographic location. It doesn’t need the Castro to make sense. The focus doesn’t have to be a daughter’s journey beyond gender. Most of our mothers have never been anywhere like the world’s we occupy. And at some point many of us will find ourselves sitting in a child’s world, thinking “this is different.” Finding commonalities in the gap between a mother’s experience and her daughter’s life, we can step out of ourselves and into empathy.

Nina is determined to share the history of her people with her mother – the secrets kept and the stories relinquished to the edges of the past. She is determined to teach her mother why so many lives have been made invisible. And when she tells her mother that she is going to go by a new name, she lets them both off the hook by waiting to reveal the new name until they are both ready to hear it said out loud. We weep with them both, stepping into the shoes that we most recognize. We weep with them both for the loss of Nina and for the vulnerability of Nick.

My own mother would make us take our shoes off whenever we entered her house. We would line our shoes up on a mat in the laundry room, honoring her request to keep her carpets clean by this habit that had nothing to do with our ethnicity or some time-honored custom. At some point my mother had opted for white carpet; we lined our shoes up dutifully before we entered the rest of the house because she didn’t want us to ruin her carpet.

I thought of this shoe line-up when I read Nick’s story. I pictured his mother slowly accepting the new shoes that would replace the ones she had thought would be there for years, lined up like so many obedient little soldiers.

Highly empathic people try on another person’s life. They expand their empathy by gaining direct experience of someone else’s life. When I think of my daughter whispering, “Do you think that’s a man,” I remember my own distance from the woman sitting a few pews over. In the years to come I would preach from that pulpit and look into this woman’s eyes as her transition became more evident. Try on her life? Am I capable of that? It is difficult enough to read the statistics from the National Transgender Day of Remembrance, held since 1999 each year in November to memorialize those who have been killed due to anti-transgender hatred or prejudice.


·         This past year 238 trans* people were murdered worldwide.

·         41 percent of trans* people in the United States have attempted suicide.

·         Most transgender deaths are unreported or lost due to misgendering.

·         The murders are violent, often involving body mutilation.

 

Listen hard and open up. We usually think of listening as something passive, as doing nothing. We want to help and fix, to do something. When we accompany someone on their journey we can contribute listening. UU minister Jacob Trapp wrote: “To listen to another person is to take seriously his otherness.” Our listening makes a difference when we are willing to be vulnerable. Being vulnerable means we sit with the discomfort of our own feelings, but do not respond. How many times have we each responded to someone’s tale of woe with our own tale of woe? Listening hard, we simply say “I’m sorry.” Opening up, we sit with someone right where they are.

Habit 5: Inspire mass action and social change. It has been several years since I looked out from the pulpit into the eyes of the woman we whispered about in church. I have looked out into the eyes of many a transgender person, some known and probably some unknown to me. Unitarian Universalist congregations extend a warm welcome to lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender, and queer individuals, families, and communities. We are committed to protecting the civil and legal rights of LGBTQ people and families across the country. Yet, many families have come to our congregations thinking we are a safe place only to discover that we are more comfortable with the LG and the B than we are with the T and the Q. Many have come to our congregations and heard the whispers. Practicing the six habits of highly empathic people takes work. Practicing the twelve steps to a compassionate life takes work.

Hoping to inspire mass action and social change, this past June at our annual General Assembly of UU congregations, we took over the Louisville Convention Center and changed the majority of the restrooms to gender neutral. Temporary signs were posted covering up the familiar blue signs that separate us into the usual binary definition of gender.  One morning I encountered two older women clutching each other’s hands, confused about which bathroom they should use.

It was an incomplete experiment at best; you could tell which bathroom was which just by glancing in quickly and determining if there were urinals and stalls or just stalls. I reassured the women that were hesitating outside what would have been the women’s restroom, had the UUs not been in town, and invited them to follow me. It’s easy to make fun of our clumsy attempt to inspire mass action and social change, but after a week of this social experiment many of us understood what it must be like to be gender non-conforming and the risk of the simple action of using a public restroom. The women I encountered that morning could understand that risk better. They had walked in someone else’s shoes.

Habit 6: Develop an ambitious imagination. Justine’s project with the children asked them to imagine the people who once wore shoes that were given away to the Goodwill Store. Some were worn out, some were out of style, and some were dyed garish colors. We imagine the stories behind the shoes and have empathy for those we think are living on the margins. Certainly the next people to wear the shoes are those possibly struggling with poverty; those who cannot afford a full-price shoe store. What if Justine had cut out photos of really expensive shoes? Or shoes that belong to people who hold different political views than we do? What do those look like, exactly? What do the shoes of an oil company executive look like? What do the shoes of a religious fundamentalist look like?

Towards the end of his journey beyond gender Nick Krieger discovered empathy. He acknowledges the huge step his mother took to be with him for his surgery and then he writes, “having my own needs fulfilled made me want to help those with fewer opportunities and greater challenges, the people who weren’t like us.”

The people who aren’t like us are those with fewer opportunities and greater challenges. The people who aren’t like us are also those who we count as our enemies. Stepping out into empathy we leave the confines of our own suffering and enter into the life of another. We begin to live from this prayer, “Great Spirit, grant that I may not criticize my neighbor until I have walked a mile in his moccasins.”
