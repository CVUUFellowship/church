Hate the Wars, but

Love the Warriors

Rev. Anne Felton Hines

May 24, 2015 (Memorial Day)

 

          I was at my credit union the other day, and the bank teller casually asked me if I had any plans for the Memorial Day weekend. I didn’t tell him that I’d be working for some of it; instead I simply said no, other than maybe going to a movie with a friend. And then I asked him the same question; and his answer was similar to mine: he might do some barbecuing with the family, he said, but no big plans.

          Of course, I’ve been invited to lots of places over the weekend: Kohl’s, Macy’s, Coldwater Creek online – you name it; they’ve assured me that I’ll find bargains like never before, all in honor of Memorial Day!

          And for anyone who cares about the rules of fashion, Memorial Day marks the beginning of the season when we women can wear white accessories; hallelujah! (Actually, I’ve purposely been wearing white accessories long before this weekend, just to annoy the fashion police!)

          Lots of people have taken off for camping or the beach, including my daughter and her family. And for those of us who just wish to stay at home, there are several marathons on TV to binge-watch: “Friday Night Lights,” “Keeping Up with the Kardasians,” and one called “Hoarding: Buried Alive” – which sounds so intriguing I might just have to tune in! And of course, there are lots of war movies.

          But there was a time when Memorial Day carried more weight than simply going out-of-town or taking a trip to the mall. It began as “Decoration Day” after the end of the Civil War, and people would visit the cemeteries and place flowers or flags on the graves of those killed in that war. It was a somber day of remembrance – a time to mourn the deaths of loved ones – and perhaps even strangers – and feel the costs of that war.

It wouldn’t be until after World War I that the holiday would evolve into one honoring all Americans who fought in a war. But when it began to lose its solemnity and become more like any other holiday for recreation and merchandizing, I cannot say.

Of course, there are celebrations this weekend remembering our military – parades, concerts, memorials at cemeteries in many communities around the country. But I’ve never taken them very seriously; they’ve felt too patriotic for me – too much of a celebration of war and a false patriotism, and not enough about the real costs of those wars, and our yearning for peace.

So I’ve pretty much avoided paying this holiday much attention. Most years in churches I’ve served, I’ve used it as an opportunity for the congregation to honor people in their lives who have died in the past year. Called “Building an Altar to Life,” people were invited to bring photos or mementos honoring their deceased loved ones, and place them on an “altar” created for the occasion; it was always moving, but had little to do with military deaths.

When I have preached the occasional Memorial Day sermon, it’s honored Conscientious Objectors, or celebrated peacemakers, or protested war; it has not been a sermon simply paying homage to the soldiers who have died or been severely wounded while fighting for our country; I have felt too conflicted about them. But that has changed for me, in large part because of the stories of some of my UU colleagues.

Rev. David Pyle, former assistant minister at the Ventura UU church, also served as a military chaplain to American soldiers in Bosnia/Herzegovina, El Salvador, and elsewhere.

He wrote that he began sensing the tragedy of war at an early age, as he stood one day with his father at the Vietnam War Memorial, and saw his father softly crying while tracing his fingers over the name of someone with whom he’d fought side-by-side in that war.

A few years later, Rev. Pyle would question why his grandfather was openly weeping while staring at an old abandoned bunker on a beach in Hawaii. His mother explained that his grandfather had lived in that bunker while serving as a sailor during World War II – had lived, fought, and watched friends die. All those years later, Rev. Pyle’s grandfather was still carrying those men in his heart.

My own family has been pretty untouched personally by war; I don’t think I’ve known anyone who was killed or even seriously wounded in one.

But reading some of David Pyle’s reflections of his experiences called to my mind moments when I was suddenly brought into the life of a soldier or their family, and was opened in a way I hadn’t expected to be.

Like the year that I attended a peace rally at the Arlington West exhibit on the beach below the Santa Monica Pier. It was the first time I’d visited this display of crosses in the sand, each one representing a soldier who had died in the Iraq or Afghanistan wars. People were wandering through the aisles, placing flowers on some of the crosses, as well as slips of paper with a soldier’s name, and personal messages of love or gratitude or peace.

          Participants in the rally were asked to carry a flag-draped cardboard box the size and shape of a casket through the streets of Santa Monica. Another woman and I chose one that had a photo of a young soldier leaning against it.

As we stood waiting for instructions, a man came over to us and introduced himself as a friend of the soldier in the photo.

We stood there chatting with him, when suddenly his cell phone rang, and it turned out to be the mother of “our” soldier! After the man spoke to her for a few minutes, he said, “Here; I’ll let you talk to a couple of the people who will be carrying Tommy’s casket through the streets of Santa Monica,” and proceeded to hand the phone to me! I hardly knew what to say. Finally I told her how honored I was to carry this casket representing her son; and I let her know I was a minister, and that I would be lighting a candle in her son’s name the next day at my church.

She was so very grateful, and speaking with her – hearing the sorrow and the gratitude in her voice – brought the depth of her experience alive for me.

I don’t remember how long it took the crowd at the rally to walk through downtown Santa Monica, carrying all these symbolic caskets on our shoulders. But as we did, I began to feel the burden on my shoulders of this young soldier; I could feel the heaviness of his coffin, and the grief of his death. I was reminded, as my colleague Barbara Pescan once wrote, that he and all these soldiers belong to me and to us all.

Another time, when members of my congregation and I had returned to Arlington West to help put away the display until the next weekend, I found myself standing beside a young Marine as we awaited our turn to carry a flag-draped cardboard “casket” to the storage area. I asked him, “How long ago were you in Iraq?” And he answered with great pride and excitement in his voice, “Oh, I haven’t been yet. I go next year!”

          I tell you, I felt as if I’d been stabbed in the heart. I wanted to grab him and plead with him not to go. I wanted to take him home with me and protect him. But I couldn’t; all I could say was, “Ahhh…I will think of you” – meaning, “I will pray for your safety, and that you will have to kill no one.” I have so often wondered what became of that young man; I can only hope that he’s back home where he belongs, and that he is well.

          I do not want to forget these two soldiers – one dead, and one hopefully still alive; nor do I want to forget their families – that mother’s voice, the children left behind – the children never even born. Yet too often I do forget.

“War is the only form of Hell I believe in,” writes David Pyle; “a Hell existing in this time, in this world; a Hell of our own making. It is one of the deepest and most destructive aspects of who we are as a human race,” he contends. And I think he should know, as he has witnessed the aftermath of war up close.

He has witnessed what it does to those who survive the war, but who carry deep wounds – either physical or emotional – because of the experience.

He has witnessed what it does to the children of war. In one of his Memorial Day sermons, Rev. Pyle wrote, “When I think of war, I think of children; of the cycles of violence – of learned helplessness and of power. I think of the lessons we teach our children – that what we teach them, implicitly and explicitly, becomes the reality of the world of tomorrow.”

And he has witnessed what war does to the families of those who fought – of those who died, and of those who returned wounded.

And because of all he has witnessed, Rev. Pyle tells us that we should be treating Memorial Day as a sacred day. “For military folks,” he writes, “it is a day for silent tears, for touching a wall, for remembering those who served alongside them in concrete bunkers, and walked through Hell with and for each other. He asks us to make this not a day of celebration, but a National Day of Remembering and of Mourning.

 “Place your arms around our soldiers,” he pleads, “as we weep our silent tears.”

But the true meaning of this holiday, writes Rev. Pyle, is to remember that the cost of war is almost always too high. “For those who believe in Peace, who hope for Peace, who pray for Peace, and who work for Peace…there should be no more sacred day in the calendar than Memorial Day – a day to remember the cost of war, and to never, never, never allow that cost to be forgotten.”

My friend and colleague Rev. Jan Christian, senior minister at the Ventura church, whose older brother was killed in Vietnam, suggests that one way to truly honor those who have served in our wars is to simply say “Welcome Home” upon their return, and to stand “ready to listen to their sorrows, knowing it might take years.”

But both of these colleagues tell us also that in remembrance of the women and men who walk through the Hell of war for us – out of love for them and for their families, and especially for the children everywhere – we must never stop praying and singing and working for peace, and the justice that walks hand-in-hand with that peace.  We, as Unitarian Universalists and as patriotic Americans, must do whatever we can to bring an end to war, and to ensure that those who have survived our wars, are given the support they need upon their return. As we now know, our country has fallen far short of such support.

Over 87,000 Americans have died in military combat in my lifetime; and of course, many thousands more have been severely wounded, both physically and psychically. All these men and women came from different backgrounds, different circumstances, carrying different hopes and dreams in their hearts. Most of them were probably young – in some instances, barely out of high school. Some left behind young children, and others were looking forward to starting a family. But all of them died before they should have; each of their lost lives needs to be remembered, mourned; they must not be forgotten.

Rev. Pyle has said that he hears the true meaning of Memorial Day in the “prophetic voices” of the soldiers themselves, imploring us, “Remember me.” So in the silence that follows, I invite you to call into the sacred space of this Sanctuary the names of any members of our armed services who you hold in your heart this weekend, be they living or deceased. May our compassion and gratitude embrace them now.

May these names be forever blessed, as they bless us. May their lives and their sacrifices be honored, always.
