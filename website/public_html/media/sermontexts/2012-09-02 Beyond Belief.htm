Beyond Belief

©Rev. Lora Brandis

Preached September 2, 2012

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Congregation

 

It finally happened: someone asked me if my church believes in heaven. I did my awkward best to answer. Some of us do, some of us don’t. It’s really not fair to ask what the church believes; we have to ask first what each person believes. We’re non-creedal and covenantal. It’s not about what we believe; it’s how we behave with each other that matters. After listening patiently as I fumbled along, my questioner finally said, “Well, my church believes in heaven and it matters how we behave with each other too.”

And then she described to me exactly what she loved about her church: how they took care of each other when one of them was sick, how they greeted each other every Sunday with a hug, how they divided up into smaller groups so they could get to know each other more intimately. Sound familiar? Maybe our beliefs don’t separate us from other religions as much as we think they do.

My questioner asked me about heaven. She didn’t ask me about what we believed about immigration as a moral issue or marriage equality. Just because we didn’t discuss either of those two issues, I cannot assume she and I would hold different beliefs when it comes to immigration reform or the right of same-sex couples to marry. I did not tell her about our Standing on the Side of Love Campaign, how the immigration reform proponents in Arizona think of us as the “love people.” I did not tell her that my church stood on the corner and held signs questioning the 1% and arguing against California’s Proposition 8. She had asked me if we believe in heaven. This belief is what mattered to her. This belief is what she thought might separate her church from mine. In the end, I listened to how she loves her church and hoped she could tell how much I love mine.

Last Saturday I met a man at Ventura County Pride who told me what he loves about his church. He belongs to a small Christian church with about 50 members. He was proud to share that they call him their “token” – the church’s only gay member. He is a cancer survivor with a miraculous story of leaving hospice to outlive all of his doctors’ predictions. When he left hospice, he was told he would be going to a rehab center to get used to the new hip that had replaced his cancer-ridden bone. Members of the church picked him up and took him not to a rehab center, but to a room in someone’s home that they had equipped for him so that members of the church could care for him around the clock. His church’s denomination is currently embroiled in a debate about ordaining gays and lesbians, so of course there is a wide range of beliefs in his church. What he loves about his church is how they move beyond belief to love the stranger, caring for him beyond belief, loving the stranger that he once was into wholeness.

Gail Geisenhainer was evangelized beyond belief into Unitarian Universalism. She now serves as the minister of the First Unitarian Universalist Congregation in Ann Arbor. I am told that she once served our nearby congregation in Canoga Park. I am also told that when she said, “All blank-ing churches are the same….” “They say they’re open—but they don’t want queer folk. To heck with church!” that her words were probably a bit stronger than she’ll admit in an article published in the UU World magazine.  She does, however, admit to carrying a rock-hard chip on her shoulder from every other religious experience she had ever had. Ever.

The gray-haired ladies of the Maine UU church had a surprise for Gail. They offered her coffee. They loved her beyond belief into wholeness. She stayed for coffee and for Unitarian Universalism. Isn’t this a great example of Unitarian Universalists being the “love people?” Aren’t we proud of how we are so welcoming, how what we believe really does matter? And how what we believe affects how we behave? Of course we are proud to call ourselves Unitarian Universalists, to know we are connected by a set of values and encouraged to explore many paths to the holy. This morning’s reading ends with Gail being loved beyond belief into wholeness. The rest of the story goes like this….

This was the mid-1980s and during the Joys and Concerns one Sunday a woman spoke, and this is a direct quote from Gail, “a woman stood during Joys and Concerns to announce that all homosexuals had AIDS; all homosexuals were deviants who could not be trusted with children, public health, or civil society. All homosexuals should be quarantined, packed off to work camps to provide useful labor for society and keep their filthy lifestyle and deadly diseases to themselves” (UU World, Fall 2012, p. 14). The shine fell off the new penny. Gail’s rock-hard chip on her shoulder started reappearing.

Most of us come to a church, a congregation, this Fellowship wanting community. We want to be loved beyond belief into a wholeness that we don’t even know we are seeking. We come here seeking, not believing. We come here and when we find like-minded folks, speaking a language that is somehow familiar to us, we feel comfortable. Some of us feel comfortable for the first time in our lives. And we stay.

Then someone says something, behaves in a way that we disagree with, shares something in joys and sorrows that hurts us to our core and now the true test of community begins. We are no longer comfortable and all we want to do is leave. We didn’t know that when we were seeking community that we would find a religious community.

Another one of my colleagues, Erik Walker Wikstrom, writes that “At their best, churches can be – perhaps should be – a kind of spiritual total immersion environment…. From the pastoral prayer to the passage of a policy it’s all a school of the soul.” This is reminiscent of one of the most famous quotes by the 20th Century Unitarian theologian James Luther Adams: “Church is a place where you get to practice what it means to be human.” This is the difference between community and community modified by the word religion: we enter this community intentionally. We enter this community expecting to be changed by it.

When most of us think of intentional spiritual communities, we think monastery or ashram. Those of us who are birthright Unitarian Universalists or have chosen this as our faith rarely think of the congregation we have joined as an intentional spiritual community. We are not stepping off the world for quiet contemplation. Most religious traditions acknowledge that all of life offers us an opportunity to deepen and to grow. Joining an intentional community or visiting one for a retreat is not simply about spending hours in prayer and meditation. The monastery offers the monk work to be done in a prayerful manner; the ashram offers seva, selfless service to the community. Quiet contemplation is only one way to connect with spirit. Work done intentionally can bear the same fruit as other spiritual practices.  But it is work and sometimes it is hard.

When Erik Wikstrom writes that, “It makes sense to see church leadership as a spiritual practice because church leadership is hard” he is talking to each and every one of us. “But I’m not a leader,” you may be sitting there thinking. Ours is a tradition that affirms the “priesthood and prophethood” of all believers. Some are called to a ministry of transformational healing; others are called to a ministry of prophetic social justice. We are all called: believers and non-believers alike. We are all called to stay when leaving would be easier.

When Gail Geisenhainer was confronted with what we might call a non-believer from the vantage point of proudly referring to ourselves as the “love people,” she had a choice to make. When Gail heard anti-gay remarks in a church she had been told would be different, she could return the next week or she could add the experience to the rock-hard chip on her shoulder and leave religion once and for all. She chose to confront her discomfort, to face her pain and travel the harder path. She went back to church.

There she found people willing to stand up during Joys and Concerns and say that not everything said the week before was how everyone believed. Furthermore, the congregation did not shun the “non-believer,” the woman who had spoken so harshly during Joys and Concerns. After all, it’s not about what we believe but how we behave with each other that matters.

Asking what the church believes or does not believe falls into the theological category called ecclesiology from the Greek word ekklesia, meaning called together. Ecclesiology is the study of what it means to be a religious community. In the Christian community, the church is defined as the body of Christ. The marks of the Christian community are the sacraments and rituals, signifying the community’s relationship to each other and to all of Christianity. Christians participate in these rituals and sacraments, receiving grace in order to be of service to each other and to the world. In the Buddhist tradition, there are the three treasures: the Buddha, the Dharma and the Sangha. Sangha is the Pali and Sanskrit word for community. It most often refers to the community of monastics, ordained monks and nuns. In Western Buddhism Sangha refers to lay practitioners as well.

It is difficult to identify the marks of Unitarian Universalism. We don’t have readily identified rituals. We don’t even a common language. I liken this to a “branding” issue, to use marketing lingo. When you visit any 7-11 you know where you can find the toothpaste. It’s on the same aisle in every 7-11 convenience store. This is marketing by design. This is marketing that creates return visitors. When you visit a Unitarian Universalist church you may not even be visiting a church. You may be visiting a Fellowship or a Society. You may or may not hear the same opening affirmation, found as #471 in Singing the Living Tradition.  You may or may not hear the same closing words, found as #456. When someone asks us whether or not we believe in heaven, we had better be prepared to welcome them on Sunday morning to an unfamiliar experience with the familiar marks of a religious community.

So what then does it mean each Sunday when many Unitarian Universalists do recite #471 covenanting together that, “Love is the doctrine of this church, the quest of truth its sacrament…” This traditional affirmation, dating back to the 1930s Unitarian side of our tradition is said in enough of our congregations that it could be identified as one of the marks of our religion. I learned from my minister, the Rev. Dr. Laurel Hallman, that the inclusion of this phrase in our covenants and in our readings signifies that the spoken word is the sacrament of our worship services. If the quest of truth is a sacrament – an outward sign of an inner searching – then the sermon and all of the words uttered in the worship service proclaim some piece of that quest. The “word” creates insight, transformation and ultimately, access to grace. This invitation to be transformed by the spoken word is one of the identifying marks of Unitarian Universalism, whether we call ourselves a church, a Fellowship, a congregation or a Society.

There are many member churches of the Unitarian Universalist Association that do not affirm each Sunday that the quest of truth is their sacrament. This independence is the foundation of our congregational polity. Not only does each church create their own covenant, but each individual is encouraged to form their own theology. Remembering what I awkwardly said to the questioner who wanted to know if my church believes in heaven: “It’s really not fair to ask what the church believes; we have to ask first what each person believes.”

After we have asked what each person believes, we move beyond belief into experience. What does each person experience Sunday mornings in worship, Tuesday evenings in committee meetings and all during the week in our covenant groups? Answering this question, we can begin to discover how to move the conversation beyond belief into, “How do Unitarian Universalists live as religious people?”

From Fellowship to society, from church to congregation there are identifying marks of how Unitarian Universalists live as a religious community. These marks connect us to Unitarian Universalism and they connect us to a way of being that spiritual, intentional religious communities have been practicing for thousands of years.

·         We value regular attendance at worship. Here is where we are transformed and experience the spoken word as a sacred event.

·         We attend regular meetings of the congregation and vote. We practice discernment, discovering the needs of the whole rather than privileging the needs of the individual.

·         We make financial contributions to the work of the congregation and to the wider community. We learn that charitable giving changes our own lives, as well as the lives of others.

·         We contribute our time and talent. We give back from the depths of our own soul, not because of obligation.

·         We are intentional about our own spiritual growth and development. The missions of our churches, fellowships, societies and congregations directly reflect this commitment.

The woman who believes in heaven and I have a lot in common. We both love our church, these schools of the soul. We both love having a place to go where we get to practice what it means to be human.
