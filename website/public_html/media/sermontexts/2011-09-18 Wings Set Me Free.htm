Wings Set Me Free

By Rev. Lora Brandis

Preached September 18, 2011

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship

 

My intern committee at Horizon UU Church in Carrollton, Texas was burdened with an enormous task. A group of five dedicated church members with varying levels of expertise in the life of the congregation were tasked with walking alongside me on my journey as an intern minister. They had high hopes that we would all arrive at the end of our journey with a newly turned out minister suitable for credentialing, ordaining and being sent out to serve our faith. It was an enormous task.

The task was made more enormous by the fact that the committee was the servant of two masters. One: the Unitarian Universalist Association, the UUA. The other: Perkins School of Theology at Southern Methodist University. To become a UU minister I was required to complete a full-time internship in a church. It was also a requirement of my master of divinity degree program at Perkins. There were two internship manuals that the committee had to be familiar with. One: a 28-page electronic document from the UUA. The other: a 123-page binder given to all of us and my supervising minister at an orientation provided by Perkins. I had to file two learning serving agreements, called the learning covenant by the UUA. Each time a report was due, which was at the mid-year point and at the end, two sets of reports had to be filed on two different forms. The poor, overworked committee even had two names: the UUA called them the “Intern Committee” and Perkins called them the “Lay Teaching Committee.” As the servant of two masters, they had two manuals, two reports, two names and even two theologies adding to the enormity of their task.

For the final meeting with my intern supervisor from Perkins, (of course I had two supervisors – Dennis Hamilton, the senior minister at Horizon and Isabel DoCampo from Perkins)….For the final meeting the chair of my intern committee/ lay teaching committee was assigned, in the words of Perkins School of Theology, to open the meeting with a prayer or other devotional. The chair came to me in a panic, what did that mean? She didn’t pray. “Other devotional?” Where would she find that material? I had been leading our committee meetings for the last year according to the instructions given in the Perkins internship manual, Section 4, page 18. I was to have been modeling this behavior to her. Apparently I had not done a very good job. In all honesty, as a committee we had decided to skip the instruction that said I would open each meeting with a prayer or other moment of worship because we had just come from worship. We met once a month on Sunday afternoons after the worship service. We felt that it was redundant to have another worship moment to begin our meeting. Being the good Unitarian Universalists that we all were, we changed the rules.

We opened our meetings with Unitarian Universalist traditions. We lit the chalice and participated in a check-in, that sacred moment where we connect on a deep level. And yes, the tradition often includes a reading to accompany the chalice lighting. This is our living tradition’s way of praying or sharing a devotional moment. Once I translated this theology for my committee chair she was relieved and set off to find an opening reading for the chalice lighting to begin our final meeting of the year.

I had become adept at translating Perkins Christ-centered theology into my own UU theology by the end of my seven years in seminary. Assignments like “Analysis of Four Consecutive Lord’s Day Services in a Local Church” for my Word and Worship class became “Analysis of Four Worship Services in a Local Congregation.” Questions like “In this situation of ministry how was God’s intention to bless all of creation disclosed?” became “In this situation of ministry how is the person’s innate ability to ‘wake up’, to live more consciously, disclosed?” The most challenging translation came when I was required to write my “credo.”

 “Credo” is the final and very large project of the required, year-long systematics course at Perkins. Systematics is pretty much what it sounds like: a systematic approach to theology. In the case of the course at Perkins, it is a systematic approach to Christian theology. Credo is what it sounds like as well, being from the Latin for “I believe.” The word originates with the Apostles’ and Nicene creeds and you will often see it as the title of the Nicene Creed set to music. It is also what many of our youth produce at the end of their religious education “Coming of Age” class. Three of my daughters had gone through the Coming of Age program at First Unitarian in Dallas, learning about Unitarian Universalism and then producing heartfelt “I believe” statements at the end of the year shared with the entire congregation. Here was my chance to write my own Credo, all 9,000 required words of it, and I would be faced with a daunting task of translation made more difficult by the constraints of placing my beliefs in the context of the proscribed Christian doctrines.

In my first draft of the Theological Prolegomena, academic speak for “Introduction,” to my Credo, I discovered experience alone wouldn’t cut it as authoritative enough for my non-believer, turned Lutheran, turned Catholic professor who once stood in front of the class and told us we know what we know because of Christ….(He actually said “It’s Christ all of the way down” usurping the image that it’s turtles all of the way down from the primitive cosmological myth.)

I then attempted to base my entire “Credo” on the six sources of Unitarian Universalism. In a systematic approach to the claim, “This I believe” we are asked to ground our beliefs in a system. In other words, from where do we draw our authority to make truth claims? If experience alone, our first source, wasn’t authoritative enough, maybe the entire six sources could stand as the authoritative system that could support what I believed. Christians draw their authority to make truth claims from God as expressed in the life and death of Jesus Christ. I attempted to argue in my Credo that the six sources of Unitarian Universalism are a sort of system, just as the Christian doctrines are the system that ground Christianity in beliefs about human nature, God and salvation. It didn’t work. The six sources might ground my Credo statement of belief for Unitarian Universalism, but I wasn’t being asked to make a statement of my understanding of the content of Unitarian Universalism.

I was being asked to form a constructive statement of my own understanding of the content of the Christian faith. I then decided to base my credo on the crossroads of experience and reason, which was so unique to nineteenth century Unitarian Christianity. Historian David Robinson comments on this uniqueness in his book about Ralph Waldo Emerson. Robinson writes:

Yet if the concept of underlying divinity of the soul, or of the God within the individual, helps to explain the positive experience the Unitarians linked with self-examination, the concept itself raises another important question. Is it accurate to regard this concept as based upon the kind of experience of the divine which Western thought has usually regarded as mysticism? What may give pause at this idea is, of course, the long historical association of Unitarianism with rationalism. …Champions of rationalism, the Unitarians were also champions of religious experience. It is this uniquely complete merger of reason and experience in theology that…sets Unitarianism apart historically in American religion…. (David Robinson, Apostle of Culture: Emerson as Preacher and Lecturer, 20.)

 

That was it. That was what finally worked. After paring down my first draft of 9,821 words to 9,000, I rooted my Credo in our UU tradition of the merger of reason with experience. After the excruciating exercise of translating my experience through reason into the Christian doctrines of God, Jesus Christ, the Holy Spirit, Creation, the Church, Salvation and Eschatology I knew what my religious identity was. I am a Unitarian Universalist. I honor the Christian roots of our faith, just as I honor my own Christian roots having been raised as an Episcopalian.

Since the nineteenth century we have moved beyond Unitarian Christianity, through humanism, into a consolidation with another liberal Christian denomination and are now a faith that looks to six sources from which to draw inspiration. Yet, you can see our roots in many of our worship traditions. And as the Spirit of Life blows through the traditions of the past we move beyond our history on the winds of change. We light candles just as people have been lighting candles in places of worship for thousands of years. We take that tradition and create a new one: the flaming chalice has been a symbol of our faith for 65 years.

We reflect on readings and base our sermons on inspired texts just as Christian preachers base their sermons each Sunday on biblical texts. The Spirit of Life blows through that tradition creating a new one: our readings come from the words and deeds of prophetic women and men, all of the world’s religions, humanist teachings and Earth-centered traditions. We gather in community, just as people of faith have gathered for thousands of years. We call that tradition by many names: church, fellowship, society, congregation…. We continue the tradition of hymn singing. The Spirit of Life comes onto us and we change the words….

The story of Carolyn McDade’s “Spirit of Life” is a cautionary tale. We think of this hymn as ours, one written by a Unitarian Universalist. In the mind of its composer it’s more of a prayer than a hymn and she is not a Unitarian Universalist. It became a part of our living tradition even before it was published in our hymnal, “Singing the Living Tradition.”

Many congregations sing it every Sunday, just like a Christian churches sing the Doxology. In our free association of autonomous congregations we created a new tradition that expresses who we are today as Unitarian Universalists. And then we tried to change the words.

We took what to Carolyn McDade was a very personal prayer and added our own words to it. Not trusting in the power of her words or the power of being acted upon by something larger than ourselves, we thought more should be sung. Carolyn McDade asks us to find our own melody, not to lose what we want to sing but to find a way to make it our own. In finding our own melody, in creating new traditions we can use her prayer as a guide. “Roots hold me close.” Honoring our past gives us access to a rich history with roots that run as deep as the beginnings of Christianity.  “Wings set me free.” In heeding Emerson’s words, “not to do, but to let do; not to work, but to be worked upon” we are carried by the spirit of love and life into a future of our own imagining.

For the final meeting of my lay teaching committee/intern committee, the chair honored our Unitarian Universalist living tradition by lighting the chalice and reading the poem “A Gift” by Denise Levertov. I had never read the poem nor heard it read aloud, and as the chair began to read I realized she was offering a gift to me, the perfect summation of our year in ministry. This tradition of ours as Unitarian Universalists to treat poetry as scripture is rooted in the writings of the Hebrew psalmists and freed by the modern language of contemporary poet/prophets.

The medieval Christian tradition of Lectio Divina invites the practice of a way of praying with scripture that opens the reader or listener into a deeper experience. Translating Lectio Divina into our living tradition, it becomes deep listening, a way of listening for truth from many sources. Listen deeply again to

A Gift by Denise Levertov

 

Just when you seem to yourself

nothing but a flimsy web
of questions, you are given
the questions of others to hold
in the emptiness of your hands,
songbird eggs that can still hatch
if you keep them warm,
butterflies opening and closing themselves
in your cupped palms, trusting you not to injure
their scintillant fur, their dust.

You are given the questions of others
as if they were answers
to all you ask. Yes, perhaps
this gift is your answer.

 

As we begin our new ministry together I give you my questions to hold gently in your hands. I invite you to give me your questions. As Unitarian Universalists the questions we ask and the questions we hold safely for each other are the gift of our living tradition.
