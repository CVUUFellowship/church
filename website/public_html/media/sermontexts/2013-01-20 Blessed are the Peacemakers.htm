Blessed are the Peacemakers

© By Rev. Lora Brandis

Preached January 20, 2013

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship

 

At our Unitarian Universalist Association’s General Assembly in June of 2012 the seeds of an idea were planted. You have heard a lot about our General Assembly (GA) from last summer. You have heard that we declared it as a Justice GA in order to address the fact that we would be gathering as a faith in Phoenix, in a state that many were boycotting because of inhumane immigration laws. If you are new to our faith, or are visiting, you may not have heard that Unitarian Universalists did not join in the boycott of the state. Instead, we attended our annual gathering in an intentional way – a way meant to stand on the side of love with our brothers and sisters doing the work of peace and justice in Arizona. We went to Phoenix, nineteen of us from this congregation, to stand as allies for immigration justice.

For most of us, the primary social witness event was a candlelight vigil held the night of June 23 outside Maricopa County’s “Tent City” jail, where County Sheriff Joe Arpaio keeps prisoners outdoors in tents without air conditioning. On June 23, the high temperature in Phoenix was 109°F. About 2400 Unitarian Universalists loaded up on buses that took us from the downtown convention center to the jail site where we sang, chanted and held up battery-powered candles for a two-hour vigil. We listened to speeches from our leaders and interfaith partners who had just come from a tour of Tent City. Puente Arizona organizer Carlos Garcia thanked UUs for bringing national attention to the conditions at the jail. The Rev. Dr. William F. Schulz, president of the UU Service Committee and former executive director of Amnesty International USA read the list of 122 people who had died in detention in the U.S. between 2003 and 2011 without having been convicted of a crime.

We returned to our buses, walking past counter-protestors, some carrying firearms (legal in the state of Arizona). We also walked past local protestors who thanked us for our presence. We returned our battery-operated candles and placed our empty water bottles in trash bags. Sitting together on the quiet bus ride back to the convention center, the seed of an idea had been planted. We wondered what that idea would look like as it started to grow when we returned to our home congregations.

The idea of immigration justice has followed us home in our efforts to begin a detainee visitation program in collaboration with our interfaith partners in Ventura County. Immigration justice is one branch of the idea beginning to grow from the seed planted in the Phoenix heat. The idea is larger than immigration justice. It contains justice for those in our country without documents, but the idea that now drives many of us that attended Justice GA is larger, more staggering.

The revolution that followed us home is an emerging awareness that, in the words of Michelle Alexander, “We have allowed ourselves to be willfully blind to the emergence of a new caste system” (The New Jim Crow). Phoenix taught us that Joe Arpaio’s Tent City is not just a symbol of our broken immigration system. Tent City is a symbol of a new era – what Alexander identifies as “The New Jim Crow.” The mass incarceration of poor people of color in this country is a new caste system. It is the moral equivalent of Jim Crow.

Cornell West calls Michelle Alexander’s book, The New Jim Crow: Mass Incarceration in the Age of Colorblindness, “a genuine resurrection of the spirit of Martin Luther King Jr. amid the confusion of the Age of Obama” (from the preface). Alexander, a law professor, civil rights advocate and writer, had her eyes first opened to the emergence of a new caste system while she was helping former inmates re-enter into a society that, in her estimation, didn’t have much use for them in the first place. The trumped up “War on Drugs,” the increasing power of the prison-industrial complex and numerous government policies have created a system of discrimination that is similar to the Jim Crow laws put in place by a racist society responding to the end of slavery.

In her introduction to the book Alexander tells the story of Jarvious Cotton who, like his father, grandfather, great-grandfather, and great-great-grandfather had been denied the right to vote. She writes, “Cotton’s great-great-grandfather could not vote as a slave. His great-grandfather was beaten to death by the Ku Klux Klan for attempting to vote. His grandfather was prevented from voting by Klan intimidation. His father was barred from voting by poll taxes and literacy tests. Today, Jarvious Cotton cannot vote because he, like many black men in the United States, has been labeled a felon and is currently on parole” (page 1). The same core belief that created slavery and Jim Crow has created the new racial caste system: the belief that some members of our society are not worthy of the common care and compassion afforded to most.

Alexander’s astonishing and persuasive thesis is not the one we are used to, the one those of us of privilege can nod our heads to and say “oh yes, but things are so much better now that we’ve elected a black man as our president.” Yes, poor communities of color are burdened with crime and rising incarceration rates. Of course this is due to the very function of poverty and lack of access to quality of education. Alexander is not reiterating this idea. What she is proposing will lift the scales from our eyes. The War on Drugs is the New Jim Crow. Undocumented migrants lost in detention are the New Jim Crow. “Zero tolerance” policies in schools are the New Jim Crow. Three Strikes Laws are the New Jim Crow. Mass incarceration is, metaphorically, the New Jim Crow.

According to Alexander, “By the turn of the twentieth century, every state in the South had laws on the books that disenfranchised blacks and discriminated against them in virtually every sphere of life, lending sanction to a racial ostracism that extended to schools, churches, housing, jobs, restrooms, hotels, restaurants, hospitals, orphanages, prisons, funeral homes, morgues, and cemeteries. Politicians competed with each other by proposing and passing ever more stringent, oppressive, and downright ridiculous legislation (such as laws specifically prohibiting blacks and whites from playing chess together)…. The new racial order, known as Jim Crow – a term apparently derived from a minstrel show character – was regarded as the ‘final settlement,’ the ‘return to sanity,’ and ‘the permanent system’” (p. 34). The system started unraveling when the Supreme Court declared state-sponsored school segregation unconstitutional in Brown v. Board of Education in 1954. The remaining Jim Crow laws were overruled by the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the Voting Rights Act of 1965.

Before either of these was enacted, on April 16, 1963, Martin Luther King, Jr. wrote a letter from the Birmingham Jail. He wrote in response to eight of his colleagues who had published a statement in the local newspaper calling his recent nonviolent protests “unwise and untimely.” He started his letter by writing on the margins of the newspaper. It is a lengthy letter. When he ran out of room in the newspaper margins he continued writing on paper supplied by someone he identifies as a “friendly Negro trusty” and then concluded on a pad of paper his attorneys were finally permitted to leave him. The Letter From Birmingham Jail contains King’s famous quote: “Injustice anywhere is a threat to justice everywhere.”

In his long letter King schools his fellow clergymen on the difference between the two types of laws: just and unjust. He quotes St. Augustine saying that “an unjust law is no law at all.” “Any law that uplifts human personality is just. Any law that degrades human personality is unjust,” King wrote almost fifty years ago from jail. As segregation statutes robbed blacks of their inherent worth and dignity then, unjust laws now condemn one in three young African American men to time in prison (The New Jim Crow, p. 9). Segregation gave the oppressor a false sense of superiority over the “other.” Today’s mass incarceration perpetuates the same false sense of superiority. We have King’s words again echoing from the past to help us navigate from the present into a better future: “Sometimes a law is just on its face and unjust in its application.”

Several months ago this congregation started writing to prisoners in solitary confinement, to men held at the Pelican Bay State Prison in SHU – the Security Housing Unit. Outreach Director Maria Ornelas and several volunteers meet each Sunday after the second service and write to a list of inmates provided by Virginia Classick, a local representative of the National Religious Campaign Against Torture. Virginia advised us to include a note from me on letterhead to accompany the volunteers’ correspondence. My cover letter would include how we obtained their name, a bit about our church and, because it was on letterhead our return address if the men wanted to write back:

This card is being sent to you by a member of Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship, where I serve as the minister. Here we nurture spiritually courageous people who transform the world through justice and compassion. A member of California Families to Abolish Solitary Confinement provided us with your name and address. Your first name was attached to a card so that a member of our congregation could address and think of you by name.

 

As Unitarian Universalists we affirm the inherent worth and dignity of every person. At the national level we have been involved in the campaign to end prolonged solitary confinement. In fact, the board president of the National Religious Campaign Against Torture is a Unitarian Universalist. Locally we work with the UU Legislative Ministry of California to enact just laws that affect human rights issues.

 

As a people of faith we also affirm a deep respect for the interdependent web of all existence of which we are a part. We know that what affects one, affects us all.

We hope that in some small way this note can provide strength for your journey.

Thinking of my letter as just a cover letter, I never expected to receive correspondence back from the men our volunteers were sending more personalized notes to. I was surprised by letters addressed to me, directly in response to what I had said in what I had thought of as “just a form letter.”

The men I have heard from would like the entire congregation to know their gratitude. One wrote that he likes that we recognize the web of all existence – it is something he has come to realize along what he calls his rather “thorny” journey. One has been in solitary since he was fourteen years old, with “no end in sight.” One stated that his beliefs are similar to the Fellowship’s, adding that he’s not a religious person. Another, who has been in solitary for more than 26 years, wrote about his involvement in peaceful activism fighting for prison reform.

Virginia Classick received the following email expressing gratitude for our participation in this project. “This week I received from my loved one in Pelican Bay SHU a copy of the Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship letter from Minister Rev. Lora Brandis and two cards from her parishioners. He forwarded these to me, writing:  ‘nice of this church to send inmates in SHU X-mas cards on holidays. We need to also get churches here in CC community on our side.’ Thank you so much for this beautiful program. The cards from Anne and Tricia were lovely, perfectly written, and the church cover letter gets to the point exactly: That each person has worth and dignity, and that we are all interconnected and therefore responsible for human rights.”

Fifty years ago Martin Luther King, Jr. sat in a jail cell writing about just and unjust laws and just laws that became unjust upon application. He wrote to his fellow clergymen and to all who told his race to wait, to a group he rightly accused of being “more devoted to ‘order’ than to justice; who prefer[s] a negative peace which is the absence of tension to a positive peace which is the presence of justice.” “True peace is not the absence of conflict…but the presence of justice.” He wrote to put an end to racial discrimination codified by a Jim Crow South. His spirit can live again when we allow ourselves to see the absence of justice in a system of mass incarceration that locks up and locks away the generations King fought to free.

Blessed are the peacemakers, for they will know themselves and all others as children of God. Blessed are the letter writers, for their words will raise hope in the human heart. Blessed are those who are in prison, for they will lead us all to freedom. Blessed are the positive-peacemakers, for they truly know that “Injustice anywhere is a threat to justice everywhere.”
