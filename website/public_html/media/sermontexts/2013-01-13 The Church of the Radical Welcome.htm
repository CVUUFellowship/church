The Church of the Radical Welcome

© By Rev. Lora Brandis

Preached January 13, 2013

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship

 

We often sing, “Come, come, whoever you are, wanderer, worshiper, lover of leaving. Ours is no caravan of despair. Come, yet again come.” As Unitarian Universalists we sing these words as an offering of radical hospitality. We sing these words because we know what it is to wander among beliefs, building our own theologies from remnants of personal experience and the wisdom of the ages. We sing these words because many of us have left churches, temples and religions that no longer make sense to us. And we sing these words lighting the candle of hope against the darkness of despair.

The words we sing are supposedly adapted from the Persian poet and mystic Rumi. The original words, in one translation are:

"Come again, please, come again,

Whoever you are.

Religious, infidel, heretic or pagan.

Even if you promised a hundred times

And a hundred times you broke your promise,

This door is not the door

Of hopelessness and frustration.

This door is open for everybody.

Come, come as you are."

The adapted words we sing welcome the religious seeker into a community committed to radical hospitality, called “transformative spiritual hospitality” by Presbyterian Minister Nanette Sawyer. The original words of Rumi’s poem imply that all are welcome to repentance; all are welcome to return to right living. It is interesting to note that Rumi may not be the author who originally suggested that all are welcome to repent and return. Even if the original words belong to an earlier, Turkish writer, the message contained in them is clear: all are welcome and perhaps, more importantly, all are welcome to return again and again to our caravan of seekers, searchers, and lovers of life.

When you visit our congregation we offer you a red cup, a green nametag and promise these tools will help us identify you as a visitor. You enter into this mysterious system that, if you want it to, will move you from visitor to new friend, to friend and onto the ultimate goal, for “us” anyway, of member status. Along the way we stick a colored dot on your nametag, change the color of your nametag, and if a friend brought you with them for a visit, slap a “halleluujah” (yes, with two “U”s) sticker on their nametag. All through this process you might stand innocently in the corner of Fellowship Hall with your red cup feeling like Pastor Nanette Sawyer upon her first visit to a meditation center. “I stood there awkwardly,” she writes, “feeling a bit miffed that no one was speaking to me. Didn’t they realize that I was a newcomer? Couldn’t they see that I was feeling shy? Why wasn’t anyone welcoming me?” (Hospitality – the Sacred Art) Like Pastor Sawyer, you may be asking yourself, “Why aren’t these people aware of my vulnerabilities?” clutching your red cup anxiously. The transforming moment came for Pastor Sawyer when she noticed that “they,” those she had labeled as the confident, belonging, inside circle ones were just as vulnerable as she, the uncomfortable, new, outsider one. The transforming moment came when she realized her own power to be hospitable in any situation.

The greeters, the membership committee and the board all just breathed a collective sigh of relief. We’re not responsible for making visitors feel comfortable – they are, many are now thinking. Thank the g-word that many of us would refrain from using because we want to make sure that everyone feels welcome here, infidel, pagan and heretic alike. “They,” according to Pastor Sawyer, can just reach deeply inside themselves and bring out the receptivity, reverence, and generosity that they have to offer. We can just keep talking to the people we know. Whew!

The promise of the church of the radical welcome is that we will meet you in that vulnerable space between us. We will meet you in that space where the “I” begins to dissolve and the “we” begins to take shape. We see you in our own vulnerability and understand that welcoming the stranger is an invitation to welcome the stranger in our own souls. It is comforting to learn that you carry your own capability for radical hospitality, defined by receptivity, reverence and generosity. We carry this capability as well. And we need you to show up in order to discover our own capability. We need you to meet us in that space between us where we can hold your vulnerability along with our own.

In her TED talk on the power of vulnerability, researcher and storyteller Brené Brown talks about how we numb ourselves to vulnerability. She talks about how in numbing our vulnerability, we numb all of our emotions because it is impossible to select which terrifying emotion we want to numb when we reach for those three beers or that banana nut muffin. Dr. Brown has a PhD in social work. She would rather be gathering data, measuring it and fixing the world’s problems than admitting to her own vulnerability. In the end this is what she has discovered: “to let ourselves be seen, deeply seen, vulnerably seen; to love with our whole hearts, even though there's no guarantee…just to be able to stop and, instead of catastrophizing what might happen, to say, ‘I'm just so grateful, because to feel this vulnerable means I'm alive.’”(http://www.ted.com/talks/brene_brown_on_vulnerability.html??utm_medium=social&source=email&utm_source=email&utm_campaign=ios-share)

The church of the radical welcome is a place where we take responsibility for our own reaction to vulnerability. Think about that the next time you see a visitor wearing their green nametag, standing by themselves in Fellowship Hall holding a red cup. You can acknowledge your own terror at meeting someone new, thinking “I’m just so grateful, because to feel this vulnerable means I’m alive.”

“Come, yet again come.”

When I went back to church after a fifteen-year absence, I went to sing in the choir. A friend had invited me to her small Presbyterian Church whose theology was liberal enough for me: they had participated in the Sanctuary Movement of the 1980s, housing refugees from El Salvador. They were a “More Light Church,” the Presbyterian equivalent of our Welcoming Congregation designation, a congregation dedicated to LGBT rights at the beginning of the AIDS epidemic. I wasn’t there for the theology. I was there to sing. My first visit to the church was not for worship; it was for a choir rehearsal. I sat in the car for what seemed like hours, feeling terrified. Hoping I was at the right place. Waiting for my friend to show up. There were only a handful of cars in the parking lot. I must have been the last to arrive. Finally I summoned the courage and somehow found the right door leading into the choir loft. My friend was there, along with six strangers. It was a small choir. I was the second soprano – not A second soprano as opposed to a first soprano. I was THE second soprano. There were two sopranos, two of us, sometimes three if the altos could spare an extra voice. I had not sung in a choir since high school. Sharing my voice with six strangers was excruciating.  It was a vulnerability I was willing to bear. It was a shared vulnerability that allowed me to connect to strangers who changed my life: one who is now dead from AIDS, another who is my daughter’s godmother, one who is now suffering from breast cancer, another who continues to share her recovery from alcoholism with me, others who are still in my life despite the many miles that now separate us.

When I left the church, about three years later, the minister tried to convince me to stay. I was going through a divorce and I felt that the church did a better job at responding to our members who were dying from AIDS than it was doing responding to my struggles as a newly-divorced single parent looking for full-time work. She advised me that I should wait, that I shouldn’t make the decision during such a major life transition. Looking back I know that I was blaming the church for being uncomfortable with my pain. I failed to step outside myself into the space where I could hold my vulnerability along with theirs.

The risk we take when we hand someone the red cup, telling them “Come, come whoever you are,” is that they will not come back. In the church of the radical welcome we are willing to take that risk, knowing that the need for connection is fundamental to the life fully lived.

Pastor Nanette Sawyer writes that “transformative spiritual hospitality,” what some call radical hospitality, “flows from receptivity, reverence, and generosity – three qualities that reflect a basic pattern of movement: in-with-out.” We practice radical hospitality in ever-widening circles – with ourselves, our families, our communities and the world. Receptivity is the inner state we develop when we affirm that we are enough, we do not have to be perfect to deserve love, we can be open to receive the gifts that are available to us now.

Frances Pardee, our pledge co-chair, shares the old joke that UUs, even though they’re one of the wealthiest denominations, give the least because they’re not afraid of going to hell. Most of us are not motivated to generosity in order to be rewarded with heaven. Most of us, like Frances, are motivated by contributing to causes that make heaven on earth now. Giving at the level of the modern tithe, as Frances says, is radical generosity. If we follow the model of radical hospitality to ourselves and others, then receptivity comes first.

We can be the recipients of our own generosity. As members of this community, practicing radical hospitality, we receive the gifts of this congregation, sit in reverence with each other and give generously to the institution that holds us in our vulnerability. We affirm that we are enough. We affirm that this community is enough, even when people drink from the red cup and then move on, we know we have done nothing wrong. There is no one to blame, no faults to be fixed. We are good enough. We receive the love here for us. We are worthy of a permanent church home with enough room for our religious education classes. We are worthy of state-of-the art facilities that attract the paradigm-shifting conversations produced by our Community Forum. This church deserves high quality, transformative worship experiences. You deserve all of this and so do our visitors.

Wednesday night we moved the New UU class from the board room to the sanctuary because there were too many people to fit around the conference table. Meditation class was being held in the adult religious education classroom and the adult religious education class, “Dangerous Words,” was being held in Fellowship Hall. I love the energy of this church when it is filled with the sounds of people becoming spiritually courageous. At the end of the New UU class, I invited those who were interested to sign the book.  Someone joked that this meant he no longer had access to the red cup. No longer the uncomfortable, new, outsider he could now become the confident, belonging, insider. He was joking.

In the church of the radical welcome we practice a reverence for all of life. We share an intimacy with you that is not based on knowing your name, your occupation or your place of birth. Intimacy does not need time to develop from the comfort of getting to know you. True intimacy comes when we see our own vulnerability in your eyes. We promise to meet you in that space where the “I” begins to dissolve and the “we” begins to take shape.

In the church of the radical welcome our generosity naturally flows out of the depths of our being to offer physical, emotional, and spiritual care. Radical generosity comes from first dollars, not leftover dollars. Radical hospitality flows from receptivity, reverence, and generosity. On the in-breath we receive all that life has to offer. On the out-breath we give generously of ourselves. In that pause before the next breath we stand together trusting in our shared vulnerability, meeting in the space where the “I” begins to dissolve and the “we” begins to take shape.
