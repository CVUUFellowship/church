DREAM Sabbath

By Rev. Lora Brandis

Preached September 25, 2011

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship

 

“Loud voices urge us to choose fear, denial, reactionary nationalism, and racism. We must resist and choose the better way urged by every major religious tradition.” – Rev. Peter Morales UUA President

Communities from every major religious tradition have joined together for DREAM Sabbath 2011, a time for education, reflection, action and solidarity. In December of 2010, the DREAM Act was just 5 votes short of passing and ensuring that millions of immigrant children can stay in the United States, their home. This year, on June 28th, the hopes of immigrant children were revived by a hearing held on the DREAM Act, chaired by Senator Richard Durbin. In California, our version of the DREAM Act passed the state assembly in May. Public support for positive immigration reform is gaining momentum. Our witness is needed to keep moving forward to choose the better way.

Just as positive immigration reform is gaining momentum, the voices of fear and racism grow louder. Deportations, including deportations of DREAM-eligible youth and young adults, are continuing in unprecedented numbers. Our witness is needed to stop the deportations, calling on the Obama administration to grant deferred action to DREAM students.

Thousands of hard working young people who were brought to the United States as infants or children can be locked up in federal detention centers and deported to a country they‘ve never known. The DREAM Act would stop the injustice by giving students who have grown up and graduated high school in the United States the opportunity to earn legal status through college or military service. As people of faith, our witness is needed.

Gaby Pacheco witnessed her parents and her sisters taken away from her during a raid in her home by Immigration and Customs Enforcement agents.

Witness has many meanings including to “see and hear by personal presence and perception,” which is what Gaby did when her family was taken away. Witness also means to “testify to; give evidence of.” In Greek the word which became martyr was used similarly as we use the word witness today. As the Christian faith spread and people bore witness to their faith they were called martyrs, simply those witnessing to their faith. It was later when Christians became persecuted and killed for witnessing to their faith that the word martyr took on the meaning we ascribe to it today. The roots of witness contain the ultimate sacrifice of martyrdom.

The word witness retains the meaning of testifying to one’s faith, not something that most of us are comfortable with. In a religion that claims the supremacy of deeds over creeds, our testifying is done in public acts of witness. Gaby Pacheco’s public act of witness is made more powerful by what she witnessed privately when her family was taken away.

Gaby’s parents came to this country from Ecuador when she was 7. She was the highest ranked Junior R.O.T.C. student in her high school, and she received the highest score on the military‘s aptitude test. The Air Force tried to recruit Gaby but she was unable to enlist because she did not have legal status. Since then, Gaby has earned two associates degrees in education and a BA in special education. Gaby has also served as the president of her student government and the president of Florida‘s Junior Community College Student Government Association. Gaby‘s dream is to teach autistic children.

Gaby is also one of four students we just saw in the video, “Trail of Dreams.” These four students walked all the way from Miami, Florida, to Washington, D.C. – 1,500 miles – in order to build support for the DREAM Act in 2010. Despite the disappointment and the danger of deportation, Gabby continues to speak out for immigrant students who want to take part in the American dream. She bears witness to the truth “that we are connected, that we are all in this together” (Morales).

Unitarian Universalists are being asked to witness for immigrant justice in Phoenix at our annual General Assembly in June of next year. The issue of whether to boycott Phoenix as the site of the 2012 GA had been hotly contested among UUs after the passage of Arizona Senate Bill 1070, which empowers local police to check the immigration status of those otherwise detained. Many thought the appropriate action would be to “witness” with our money, refusing to spend it on a convention center, hotels and meals in Arizona. Others thought the best way to witness, to testify as a people of faith, was to go to Phoenix with acts of service and public witness. In the end the decision was made to create a special justice GA, one that would minimize the business of our association and maximize witness and service.

The Reverend Sean Parker Dennison, minister of our congregation in San Luis Obispo, commented last night on his blog about a recent meeting of the planning committee for the Justice GA: “One of our partners in this work B Loewe, Communications Director at

National Day Laborer Organizing Network, started our time together with a reflection on three goals we might share for this experiment we call ‘Justice GA’: 1. Build power for the local communities by using our resources, privilege, and access (especially to the media) to draw attention to the struggle in Arizona and the deeper inequities and injustices it reveals. 2. Shrink disbelief among our own people and among people throughout this nation who think and say, ‘I just can't believe our government would do these things" or "I didn't know it was so bad.’ 3. Enlarge compassion” (used with Sean’s permission).

What are the deeper inequities and injustices the law in Arizona reveals? Aren’t “these people” here illegally and shouldn’t they be punished for breaking the law? “Being in the country without documentation is actually a civil offense, much like exceeding the speed limit while driving. If you’re going 50 mph in a 35 mph zone, you are breaking the law, but does that make you an ‘illegal driver’?” (UUA curriculum “Immigration as a Moral Issue”) Our immigration system is broken. Visa quotas limit even those with family connections to 226,000 green cards a year. Due to application processing backlogs, the wait for a permanent residency visa for those with family sponsorship ranges from two or three years to over twenty, depending on other factors including one’s country of origin (“Immigration as a Moral Issue”).

Border enforcement tightened in 1994, the same time NAFTA, the North American Free Trade Agreement, was put in place and again after 9/11. Americans have been unwilling to accept that the economic integration agreed to in NAFTA needs to be extended to the labor markets as well. The U.S. continues to separate labor from free trade, spending large amounts of money to unsuccessfully control the border. There is a flawed guest worker program which favors the skilled worker and invites abuse of the unskilled worker. The deeper inequities and injustices the law in Arizona reveals are the twin prejudices of race and class. Most undocumented migrants are poor and uneducated. Could it be that the U.S. is more willing to offer temporary visas to those who look more like the majority of our population?

Loewe’s second point, “shrink disbelief,” happened to me when I learned that it was restrictions put in place during the Clinton administration that caused migrants to start taking new crossing points including more dangerous parts of the Rio Grande and more isolated areas. Once migrants began to take nontraditional routes into the country, they also spread out to different parts of the U.S. Clinton’s “prevention through deterrence” did not work. Yes our government does these things and yes, even our liberal lawmakers enact policies that seriously compromise human rights.

Last year while serving a congregation in Salt Lake City, I received a call from a man who wanted to learn more about the church. He was excited to have found us. He was a gay man who had grown up as a Southern Baptist. He had read our web site and was excited to find an accepting religious community in Salt Lake City, Utah. We made an appointment to meet and I looked forward to telling him more about our liberal faith. And then I received this email:

Subject line: One question

Rev. Lora: I'm looking forward to our meeting Monday. However, I've been doing some online research about Unitarian Universalists, and I have one question:

 

Do Unitarian Universalists, either corporately or South Valley Unitarian Universalist Society as an individual church (if I may use the term),  engage in any type of ministry or program to provide any kind of aid, humanitarian or otherwise, to illegal aliens? I am absolutely opposed to any person or group aiding and abetting illegal aliens as they violate our immigration laws and procedures.

 

The United Church of Christ, a welcoming and affirming Christian denomination, does engage in such activities, which is why I declined getting involved with them or any of their churches.

 

I look forward to your reply.

 

Before I could reply, the following email came from him less than 30 minutes after the first:

Subject line: Please cancel our meeting

I just read this page and its materials on the UUA website:

 

http://www.uua.org/justice/issues/immigration/index.shtml

 

As I just explained in my previous email, I am absolutely opposed to any person or group aiding and abetting those who enter our country illegally, in open and willful violation of our immigration laws and procedures, because doing so is tantamount to supporting criminals (those who violate the law) while engaged in criminal activities (violating the law).

 

I cannot agree with or condone Unitarian Universalists in their activities, ministries, and/or programs that aid and abet illegal aliens.

 

Therefore, there is no reason for us to meet. Thank you for your willingness to sit down with me and discuss my faith journey, but a UU congregation will not be an option for me now or in the future.

 

This is what I wrote back: “Thanks for letting me know. I wish you the best on your continued journey.” That’s it. I didn’t fire back with material from the UUA’s “Immigration as a Moral Issue” explaining why criminal is not the right description for those who risk their lives to cross the border to find work to support their families. Being in the country without proper documentation is a civil offense, not a criminal one. Labeling undocumented workers as criminals fuels the denial about our broken system and lacks compassion for 11 million of our brothers and sisters who are here to escape political and economic hardship.

I also did not point out that homosexual acts were once considered illegal and many gays were labeled as criminals and even mentally ill. I did not direct him to our Standing on the Side of Love Campaign with its two causes of marriage equality and immigration reform. I just wished him well, breathed a sigh of relief that he had not paid a visit to me in person and then wondered if I should have done more. I had failed to engage this man in a conversation that would enlarge his compassion. I had not witnessed to my faith.

For the most part witnessing to our faith no longer requires the ultimate sacrifice. There are still places in the world, like Africa, where Unitarian Universalists take risks standing on the side of love with our GLBT brothers and sisters. We may get arrested when protesting an unjust law or an environmentally dangerous decision by the government, but for the most part we are not asked to die for our faith. There is a sort of death that we can experience when we are asked to witness. It is the death of the self that clings to the thought that who we are and what we are doing is not enough. Our actions are too small; the work needed is too overwhelming. If we allow ourselves to experience this death, this ego-death, truly embrace that who we are is enough, what we offer the world is enough we can stand as a witness to a faith that affirms the inherent worth and dignity of every person and know that we are all connected; we are in this together.

The most effective witness comes from this place of self-acceptance; an acknowledgment of where our passion meets the world’s need is true compassion. Find out what’s happening in this community with the so-called “Secure Communities” Program or the treatment of migrant farmworkers or respond to a friend’s uninformed email with the facts. Go to Justice GA in Phoenix in June of 2012. I’ll be there. I hope you’ll go with me.
