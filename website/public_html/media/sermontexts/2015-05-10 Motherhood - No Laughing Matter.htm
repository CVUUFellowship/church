Motherhood: No Laughing Matter

Rev. Anne Felton Hines

May 10, 2015 (Mother’s Day)

 

          I’ve never been a big proponent of Mother’s Day.

          When I was a child, I approached it with dread, knowing that I hadn’t saved enough of my allowance to buy anything nice for my mother like my friends were buying for theirs, and that if I asked her what she wanted, she’d simply suggest something cheap like a spatula. “That’s exactly what I need,” she’d insist with an earnestness that allowed me to believe her.

          When I became a mother myself – especially after feminism began questioning some of our traditions, I began to view Mother’s Day not only as a huge marketing campaign for Hallmark and other merchants, but also as a cheap trick to get mothers to feel appreciated for one day, so we might forget how unappreciated we felt the other 364 days of the year!

          At some point I’d learned about the very first Mother’s Day, on June 2nd, in 1872, when Julia Ward Howe led a "Mother's Day" anti-war observance in New York City, at which she read a Mother's Day Proclamation calling on all women to denounce war.

          I also learned that the precursor to our current Mother’s Day was begun by Anna Jarvis, following the death of her mother on May 9, 1905. Jarvis went on to campaign to establish Mother's Day as a national holiday. Five years later, Congress passed a law designating the second Sunday in May as Mother's Day, and President Woodrow Wilson issued a proclamation declaring the first national Mother's Day as a day for American citizens to show the flag in honor of those mothers whose sons had died in war.

          All of this might have been lovely, had the idea not almost immediately gone the way of commercialization. Apparently in less than a decade after Jarvis’ first commemoration, the merchandizing of it had become so prevalent that Jarvis herself said she wished she’d never started it. She hated Mother’s Day cards, contending they were a lazy replacement for writing a real note. (I shutter to think what she would have said about e-cards!) In 1948, Jarvis was even arrested for disturbing the peace while protesting the holiday’s commercialization.

And that’s how I came to feel about Mother’s Day, too; it never should have been started. Sometime during my Seminary days, I declared a war on it, and told my kids not to bother getting me anything or doing anything special for me on that day. And so they didn’t.

But over the years I’ve softened a bit. For one thing, I got tired of hearing friends tell me what wonderful things their kids had done for them on Mother’s Day, while I’d spent the day alone. But I also realized that it was a perfect excuse to spend some time with my kids, and eventually grandkids! I can pretty much count on a lunch or dinner now every Mother’s Day – combined, of course, with honoring my daughter as a mom. But I still deplore the commercialization of it.

And, I refuse to treat Mother’s Day as a Unitarian Universalist High Holy Day. I’ve warned the Worship Committee of every church I’ve served not to automatically expect a service related to Mother’s Day every year if I’m in the pulpit. You might just as likely have been listening to a sermon this morning on racism in America, or the importance of prayer in our lives – nothing related to Mother’s Day necessarily…

…had it not been for the number of jokes I’ve begun noticing over the past couple of years directed at mothers and mothers-in-law. Maybe I’d simply become acclimated to them over the years; but I truly hadn’t noticed them for a long time. I’d come to really believe we’d gotten past stereotyping moms as overbearing, simple-minded, and/or creators of guilt, or mothers-in-law as judgmental, caustic, and just downright evil.

          Yet I’ve been noticing more and more comics – especially males – making jokes, for example, about their mother phoning them over and over for no good reason except to make them feel guilty. On one of my favorite NPR shows, “Wait Wait, Don’t Tell Me,” the message that Carl Sagal was supposedly going to leave on the game winner’s home answering machine, in honor of Mother’s Day, was, “Hello, this is your mother. I can’t answer the phone right now because I’ve just died of shock that you’ve called!” (Funny, I know; but if there really are so many moms complaining about their children not calling that this can be a common joke, then maybe adult children need to start calling their mothers more often!)

          It’s not just men making the jokes, of course – though it’s probably most often the male writers of comedy shows. On one TV show a while back, a young woman refers to her mother-in-law by saying, “She’s in Arizona – or on the phone, gently telling us how we should live our lives!”

          Of course, mothers and mothers-in-law aren’t only joked about. Many TV dramas I’ve seen at some point have the mother of a main character visit, and she’s always either demeaning to the character, or meddling in their life, is alcoholic or mentally ill. I don’t know when the last time was that I saw a mother or mother-in-law depicted as someone I’d want to spend time with.

          And so I wonder: Is this how it’s always been, and for some reason I’d just stopped paying attention? Or is it some kind of new backlash, and if so, against what?

          Is it part of the more serious attack on women that we’re seeing in the political attempts to control women’s health care and our ability to make our own choices about that care? Or is it that which is causing me to flinch more when I hear these jokes against mothers and mothers-in-law – another form of discounting and disempowering women?

          All I ever wanted to be when I was growing up was a mom. I used to imagine finding an abandoned baby on our family’s doorstep, and my parents allowing me to be its primary caregiver. But when I finally did have a child of my own, I learned how incredibly challenging it was – and that no one – not even Dr. Spock – could really tell me how to do it.

I was overwhelmed with my first child, having just turned 21 the week before she was born. My husband was a full-time student with a full-time busboy job at night, and with several years of grad school ahead; we were very poor and very stressed out. And I was simply too young to be a mom; I’d had no idea how difficult it could be.

By the time I gave birth to our son – over seven years later, we were better off financially, and could afford for me to stop working and be a stay-at-home mom. And I did that for a while, and was a much better mother to him than I had been to my daughter. But finally I returned to work in an office part-time, as I just wasn’t cut out to stay home.

A year after our son was born, my husband and I separated, and I got to find out what it’s like to be a single

mother, working outside the home full-time, and juggling that with picking up the kids from school, child care, etc.; again, a huge challenge!

          So, like many of you, I’ve experienced being a married mother with a paid job outside the home, being a stay-at-home mom, and being a single mom with a full-time job outside the home. All of them carry their own challenges.

          My mother was always a stay-at-home mom with five children. Twice she hired a cleaning woman to help handle the large 6-bedroom, 5-and-a-half bathroom house she and Dad raised us in; and each time it lasted just a few months before Dad decided we couldn’t afford the cleaning woman. So Mom would go back to doing all of the cleaning, laundry, and cooking, while raising five children pretty much on her own – and in her spare time, taking on leadership roles at our church and in the Diocese, and walking the neighborhood collecting “Dollars for Democrats!” She was actually not a “stay-at-home” mom; she just wasn’t paid for all her work outside the home! She was – like many of our young mothers here – pretty amazing.

          Yet when I think back on how my siblings and I treated her, I’m reminded of all the jokes we made about her – all the ways in which we didn’t take her seriously. It wasn’t just affectionate jokes or pranks we did – those she usually appreciated for their cleverness and humor.

No; it was jokes we made – usually behind her back, but sometimes in her presence – that were an undermining of her strength, intelligence and wisdom that we just didn’t seem to get. And for that I feel sorry. It doesn’t seem much different from the jokes I hear from comics on the radio and elsewhere about their mothers and their mothers-in-law.

          And so, on this Mother’s Day, I wish I could turn some of those jokes into thank-yous.

          Like the laughing my siblings and I did when we learned she’d been appointed to the Human Sexuality Commission of the Episcopal Diocese, to explore the ordination of gay and lesbian aspirants to the priesthood. I remember us saying to one another in disbelief, “They’ve asked Mom to be on the sexuality commission?! Don’t they know how naïve she is – how many jokes about sex she doesn’t get?!”

          But she was the perfect person for that Commission, because she came to it with a humility – and yes, even a naiveté – that kept her open to new ideas and new relationships, even when they made her uncomfortable.      She learned everything she could, and became a strong supporter of civil rights for bisexual, gay, lesbian and transgender people. Almost until the day she died at age 94, she could be heard arguing these rights with other residents of the nursing home; it was inspiring.

The fact that the Episcopal church has many gay and lesbian priests now, at least a couple of gay Bishops, and transgender deacons, is in thanks to my mother and the few others courageous enough back then to take those steps.

I would want to say “thank you” to her for that.

          Or all those times I rolled my eyes as my mom lectured me and my friends about the need for honesty. “Oh what a tangled web we weave, when first we practice to deceive,” she would recite; and I would cringe and think to myself, “Oh my God, she’s not going to start with that again, is she?!” I’d look at my friends and roll my eyes with a smirk, just so they’d know I was properly embarrassed.

          But in fact, honesty has become one of my deepest values; I’ve taught it to my children, and now to theirs as well.

And when I look back on my life to see where I got that insistence for honesty, it is only my mother to whom I can point.

And so I would want to tell her “thank you” for that.

          I wish my sibs and I hadn’t laughed after one of her birthday parties, when Dad had proudly presented a cake with the peace symbol on it, and Mom and her friends had yelled, “Yay, Peace!” My siblings and I thought it sounded so silly, or shallow, or who-knows-what?!

          But we were wrong. Because Mom was our role model for activism; she was the one who taught us that to be a Christian meant caring for the poor and oppressed, speaking out for the voiceless, demanding a safe and peaceful world. Eventually, she would fully support my moving away from Christianity into the broader faith of Unitarian Universalism, in large part because she’d come to admire so many UUs who had worked alongside her in their common endeavors for peace and justice. (She knew our District Executive, Ken Brown, before I did!) And she co-founded the Interfaith Peace Ministry in Orange County, which brought together many different faith communities in their common quest for peace.

It is because of my mother that my four siblings and I are so committed to that vision as well. And so I would want to say “thank you” to her for that.

          What is it that you would thank your mother for? What actions seen once in a negative light, do you now see as a gift to you – as a “blessing of light in which you now dance?”

There are numerous other things for which I could thank my mother if I had the chance. But I also don’t think she ever needed specific “thank-yous” from us, because she knew deep down how much we kids loved and respected her. She had too much humility to acknowledge this, but my siblings and I all now know that we “dance in her light.”

          My mother was not perfect; no one’s mother is. None of us who are mothers really knows how to do it. We all fail our visions, over and over and over again, of the kind of mother we had hoped to be. And so ultimately, we must be able to laugh not at worn-out stereotypes, but at our own foibles – with tenderness, and joy, and amazement; we must be able to laugh, and to forgive.

          And so, in the words of Reverend Victoria Weinstein, “On this day…, let us honor all who have mothered us – who from somewhere in their being have freely and wholeheartedly given life, and sustenance, and vision to us.”

          Amen.
