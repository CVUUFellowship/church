It is What it Is

© By Rev. Lora Brandis

Preached March 17, 2013

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship

 

When was it that the phrase “It is What it Is” became our catch-all phrase in response to suffering? It seems to have seeped into our language like “like,” “you know” and my favorite, “yeah, no.” Listen carefully to the next conversation you have with a friend experiencing loss, heartache or temporary troubles. Habitually, one of you will say, at some point in the conversation: “it is what it is.”

This pseudo-philosophy we have collectively adopted signifies our willingness to get comfortable with the notion that objects have their own distinct nature. This circumstance is a fact distinct from what we think of it, where we think it will take us, distinct from how we think it will turn out. This can be productive when our minds want to rush ahead to the conclusion. “It is what it is” reminds us to stay here now, allowing the present to be what it is.

In response to suffering, “it is what it is” can be a sort of “there, there” pat on someone’s arm in the absence of something better to say. Yes, my suffering is what it is and that “isness,” that distinct nature, is painful. Acknowledging “it is what it is” is not an invitation to denial of gut-wrenching feelings. It is an invitation to respond to life’s tragic surprises with a willingness to fully inhabit this moment now.

In If You’re Lucky Your Heart Will Break James Ishmael Ford writes, “As I experience it all, it seems we are all part of a great current flowing from some unknown source to some unknown end, like a river on its way to an ocean. All we know with anything even approaching certainty is this moment itself. And we need to notice what we find here….” Rev. Ford is a Zen priest and a Unitarian Universalist minister. The two traditions complement each other, he assures us. The one offering a method to explore what it is to be human; the other offering a reminder that reason and rationality always inform his exploration.

Steeped in forty years of Zen, guided by a rational liberal religious tradition, Rev. Ford recommends a deep agnosticism. This deep agnosticism is not a response to a question about belief in god, the sort of agnosticism that lives comfortably somewhere between theism and atheism. His recommended deep agnosticism is a response to all of life. When we are rushing ahead to the next moment. When we are sitting in the depths of despair. When we are desperately controlling the chaos of our lives. “I suggest a deep agnosticism; truly engaging not-knowing is the universal solvent,” he writes. “It will release us from our hurt and fears by showing us, not in some abstract cognitive therapy sort of way, but in the deepest, most visceral way, who we really are” (p. 84). Declaring “it is what it is” is another way of saying, “I don’t know.” I don’t know how to fix this. I don’t know what to say or to do. I don’t know how this will turn out.

When applied superficially, “it is what it is” can be a welcome buffer to our feelings. When applied correctly, acknowledging what is calls us more deeply into our suffering and into more compassion for ourselves and all who suffer. Because all suffer.

The Zen aphorism “pain is inevitable, suffering is optional” warns us that suffering comes from our attachment to pain. “Pain is inevitable, suffering is optional” is another one of those phrases that has wandered into our vocabulary on a superficial level from a way that is beyond words. This way beyond words, be it Zen or some other spiritual path, requires a practice. Rev. Ford breaks the Buddhist practice into three categories: 1. Meditation, 2. Nondual insight and 3. How we live our lives. For the Unitarian Universalist without a Zen practice, one might consider the categories this way: 1. Taking time for reflection 2. An experience of the transcendent and 3. Living from our seven principles.

The three phrases: “it is what it is,” “pain is inevitable, suffering is optional” and Ford’s “Just don’t know. Only don’t know…” are best understood within the context of a spiritual practice that creates a lived experience.

“It is what it is.” Experience directly, in this moment now what is.

“Pain is inevitable, suffering is optional.” You are not your pain. It is not your fault.

 “Just don’t know. Only don’t know.” Feel this wisdom. Experience it viscerally. Live these words beyond the surface of their meaning. Live these words from a way that is beyond words.

Ministry continues to call me into a new relationship with suffering, a way of being that is beyond words, an acceptance of living from a deep agnosticism. I have learned from serving various congregations, filled with the wisdom of many thoughtful souls wrestling with the “isness” of their own suffering. I started in ministry as the intern minister for Horizon Unitarian Universalist Church, but before I started my internship I visited the congregation for four weeks as an assignment for my worship arts class.

In the four weeks that I attended Horizon the congregation experienced a member’s suicide and the murder of a member couple’s adult daughter, a victim of domestic violence. One Sunday I was there, the service began with an announcement that there would be a memorial service that afternoon for the member of the congregation who had recently committed suicide. During the sharing of “joys and sorrows” Reverend Hamilton acknowledged the congregation’s loss. Also during the sharing an older man stood and spoke on behalf of his wife and himself, invoking the memory of their adult child who had died eighteen months before. He reflected on the difficulty of the passing of that time and, as he looked around the light-filled worship hall, he said, “The light still comes through the windows.”

Poet and Unitarian Universalist Kathleen Sheeder Bonanno writes about her daughter’s murder in a collection of poetry entitled Slamming Open the Door. In “Poem About Light” she writes,

You can try to strangle light:

use your hands and think

you’ve found the throat of it,

but you haven’t.

You could use a rope or a garrote

or a telephone cord,

but the light, amorphous, implacable,

will make a fool of you in the end.

 

The unsettling effect of Bonnano’s book is that you sit with her in her suffering. There is no looking away, no escaping the raw feelings. “Poem About Light” is the final poem in the book and even as the writer is finding hope, as light is finding its way in, there is a reminder of how Bonnano’s daughter died – strangled by a former boyfriend. Ironically, this last poem was the first poem written about her daughter’s death, read as her victim’s statement at the trial and at her daughter’s memorial service.

Who among us would say to a parent who has lost a child, “it is what it is?” Yet we say such awkward mush when people are suffering. We make the mistake of comparing our suffering to theirs. Either ours is worse or theirs is. Employing “it is what it is” accurately may mean that we remain silent when confronted with our neighbor’s suffering. To have compassion, to suffer with, could be as simple as being with another in the present moment of what is. To sit quietly, to listen deeply – these are the gifts of being with what is. Most of us cannot manage our own anxiety when someone we love suffers. We want to do something to alleviate their suffering, to fix them. We offer a solution, demonstrating how much we know. Only don’t know, Rev. Ford tells us.  Sit in an intimacy that knows no boundaries. Sit in the present moment of what is and your anxious self will drop away into the great current of endless creation.

Afterwards you can take action. Make a casserole. Bake a cake. Write a card. All of which the good people of the Unitarian Society of Germantown did for Kathleen Bonanno and her family. In response to her family’s horrific tragedy, the congregation formed seven committees. There was a funeral to plan, dogs to be walked and food to be provided. No platitudes about god’s will were offered, only the open-heartedness of a people used to facing life’s hard questions with a deep agnosticism.

In the poem “The Unitarian Society of Germantown,” Bonanno pays tribute to her church describing it as “a big wooden boat” holding her with her husband as they are tossed about by rain in the dark. The reassuring words of the minister alone aren’t enough to calm them. She writes that

One by one, the others

press their bodies against us,

until finally,

we tire and lean in

to their patient animal breath,

to wait it out together.

 

I finished my assignment to describe the Horizon UU Church worship style. I was charged with analyzing the congregation’s piety noting how it is reflected in their informal, noisy gathering, their deep sharing, their willingness to laugh at themselves, their surrender to the silence after prayer, and as they hold hands during the benediction. I was also charged with discovering where the congregation most clearly shows the “face of its soul.” A good question to ask of any congregation, I think.

When I returned to Horizon to serve as their ministerial intern, I listened to joys and sorrows and answered back with a pastoral prayer. Some Sundays there was so much to pray for; I wondered how the small prayer I offered could hold back the darkness. One Sunday there was so much cancer, another Sunday no one shared a thing – we prayed for all that was unspoken and that which remained in shadow.

It is in those moments that I discovered how that beloved community clearly showed the “face of its soul.” It is the same as The Unitarian Society of Germantown’s. It is the same here. What does it look like, this face of a congregation’s soul? I imagine it is a face tilted slightly upward, bathed in light.

This is the stubborn sun, (the poet writes)

choosing to rise,  

like it did yesterday,  

like it will tomorrow.  

You have nothing to do with it.  

The sun makes its own history;  

light has its way.

 

This is what is: the stubborn sun will choose to rise again and again. Rain falls on the just and unjust alike. Suffering happens and when we acknowledge suffering for what it is – a thing, a circumstance, an event distinct in and of itself, we are set free.

Here is what is in this moment now: you are sitting next to someone whose daughter, or son, or spouse is in recovery. You are sitting next to someone whose daughter, or son, or spouse just relapsed…or brother, or sister, or mother, or father. You are shoulder to shoulder with someone whose sister died recently, or brother, or mother, or father. You are sitting with a cancer survivor. You are suffering with someone who lost their job. Your suffering is intimately connected to your neighbor’s. You are not alone.

As you go through this week, listen carefully for how you respond to your own suffering, your neighbor’s suffering and to the world’s. Do you say, “It is what it is?” If you do, are you proclaiming it as an act of denial or as an act of acceptance? If it is acceptance, is it an acceptance born of awareness that moves you toward action?

There is no magic pill, no special ingredient that will save you from your suffering. There is only this moment now. Just this. Only this. Leaning in, breathing as one. What we find here in this moment now is each other. Just this. Only this.
