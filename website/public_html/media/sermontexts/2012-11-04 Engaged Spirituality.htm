Engaged Spirituality

©Rev. Lora Brandis

Preached November 4, 2012

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Congregation

 

I have not watched any of the debates, presidential or otherwise. I have preferred experiencing the debates via posts from friends on Facebook, headlines from Democracy Now and Internet memes about Big Bird, binders and bayonets. It has been entertaining at times. Primarily it has been disheartening.

Whatever the results of next week’s election there will still be work to be done. There will always be work to be done. What better time of year to ask ourselves what an engaged spirituality looks like then the time of year when we are asked what an engaged citizenship looks like? As we cast our vote we can embody our values and allow our hearts to open wide as an engaged people of faith. You may be reluctant, like I am, to allow your heart to open wide given the current climate of attack-ads, half-truths and empty promises. It is challenging to remain engaged when we fear the outcome, grow angry at the opposition and mostly just want to turn away in disgust. We are disheartened.

Dis + hearten. Dis meaning “apart,” “asunder,” “away,” “utterly,” or having a privative, negative, or reversing force. Hearten meaning to give courage or confidence to. I am not encouraged by the current climate of political exchange in our country. My heart is asunder, broken into a million pieces by not only the political process but also by the lack of compassion so many of us in this country have for others who do not hold our same views, look the same way we do, or have access to the same resources as the wealthiest among us have.

I have not watched any of the debates. Instead, I have been reading Parker J. Palmer’s Healing the Heart of Democracy: The Courage to Create a Politics Worthy of the Human Spirit. Palmer is a Quaker.  He is also an author, educator, and activist who focuses on issues in education, community, leadership, spirituality and social change. In his book he distinguishes between the heart broken into a million pieces (the way I feel right now) and the heart broken wide open to the possibility of transformation (the way I hope to feel once the election is over).

He also reclaims the original definition of “heart,” which “comes from the Latin cor and points not merely to our emotions but to the core of the self, that center place where all of our ways of knowing converge – intellectual, emotional, sensory, intuitive, imaginative, experiential, relational, and bodily, among others,” he writes. He also writes that, “The heart is where we integrate what we know in our minds with what we know in our bones, the place where our knowledge can become more fully human” (page 6). This is similar to the Buddhist concept of locating the mind in the center of the heart. Palmer proposes that there is a heart of democracy in this country, one that we can more fully connect to when we are connected to our own hearts.

Indiana Republican Senate candidate Richard Mourdock refused to apologize for saying that when pregnancy results from rape then that is "something God intended." His comments can be added to a long list of disturbing pronouncements about reproductive rights coming from people, mostly men, currently running for office. At a press conference held to defend his remarks, Mourdock said "I spoke from my heart. And speaking from my heart, speaking from the deepest level of my faith, I would not apologize. I would be less than faithful if I said anything other than life is precious, I believe it's a gift from God.” He spoke from his heart.

Former Arkansas governor and ordained Southern Baptist minister Mike Huckabee recently created a video entitled “Test of Fire” to encourage Christians to vote their values. In his narration, cast against fiery images suggesting eternal damnation, Huckabee states that many of the issues this November are non-negotiable for Christians including the right to life, the traditional definition of marriage and a religious liberty challenged by government-mandated health insurance that covers abortion and contraception. The video ends with this invitation from Huckabee: “Your vote will affect the future and be recorded in eternity. Will you vote the values that will stand the test of fire?” Here is a man, I would imagine, who thinks that he is also speaking from his heart.

I would also imagine that most of the candidates running right now would claim they are speaking from their hearts. I would imagine, also, that the religious leaders currently claiming to know the heart of God are speaking from the deepest level of their faith. Rather than judge another person’s faith we turn away in disagreement. Depression covers anger. Disempowerment visits the disheartened. Our hearts ache for a politics worthy of the human spirit.

The poet Mary Oliver, perhaps at her most prophetic, writes on empire declaring, “We will be known as a culture that feared death and adored power, that tried to vanquish insecurity for the few and cared little for the penury of the many…. And they will say that this structure was held together politically, which it was, and they will say also that our politics was no more than an apparatus to accommodate the feelings of the heart, and that the heart, in those days, was small, and hard, and full of meanness.”

The role of the religious community is to develop our inner lives so that when we face adversity our hearts are a muscle made stronger, a space expanded by the possibility of healing. The heart we develop here is not “small, and hard, and full of meanness.” We develop the heart here for two reasons: to nurture spiritual courage and to transform the world through justice and compassion.

Here we affirm love as our guiding principle. Here we show an election-season video called "Stand with Love" created by Elliott Cennamo, a motion graphic designer and Unitarian Universalist from Columbus, Ohio. In that video’s narration we hear statements like: “You know the religious right. Well, we’re the religious left. You might not have heard as much about us, but that’s about to change.” The narrator of this video makes a different invitation than Mike Huckabee does, the narrator of “Stand with Love” invites us to stay in the conversation, that “This November, amid the fear and hate remember the one thing that can truly make a change, the one thing that can bring clarity to our disagreements and justice to the offense, take a stand but never forget what side you stand on and when someone asks for your vote stand with love.”

In contrasting the two election videos, it might be easy for us to become smug. We’re standing with love and Mike Huckabee’s Christians are insinuating that our values mean that we’ll go to hell. We’re better because we vote with our hearts wide open and they vote with hearts hardened by literal translations of scripture and minds narrowed by judgment. Standing with love means staying in the conversation when we are being called wrong. Standing with love means staying in the conversation when all we want to do is yell, “You’re wrong!” in return. Standing with love means listening deeply to another’s broken heart and finding common ground.

When the religious right defines God as a force that causes everything in the universe including rape, pregnancy and hurricanes, the religious left can define God as a force of love that arises out of an unnamable mystery that inspires awe. When those whose hearts have been broken apart by life make inaccurate, unscientific statements about weather and women’s bodies, those whose hearts are broken wide open can stay in the conversation with compassion.

I would like to imagine a conversation with Mike Huckabee, or Richard Mourdock or Todd Akin or perhaps Joe Walsh. I will probably never have the opportunity to talk with any of them, but I like to imagine it anyway. I like to imagine this conversation so I can practice conversations that move me out of my usual comfortable liberal oasis.


Last month I sat across the table from a Catholic lay leader who was working with his congregation to make sure that Propositions 34 and 36 are passed. These Propositions, the repeal of the death penalty and the revision of the three strikes law, are very important to his mostly Hispanic congregation. These Propositions are also supported by Progressive Christians United and the Unitarian Universalist Legislative Ministry of California. In that conversation the Catholic and the liberal religious can find common ground.

In another recent interfaith conversation I found myself sitting at a table with a Hindu, a Buddhist, a UU, a Jew, two Catholics and two Mormons. When the Jewish woman sitting next to me turned to the Mormons and the Catholics and said, “I think we probably share some views on abortion,” I decided that I needed to connect with a colleague across the room. Some conversations are harder than others.

Parker Palmer writes that “Abortion is one of the many issues that generate what some people have called the ‘politics of rage.’ And yet rage is simply one of the masks that heartbreak wears.” He writes about daylong dialogue programs where people are not allowed to tell their position on a difficult issue like abortion until the end of the day. Instead people are instructed to share the personal stories that created their beliefs. Listening to each other’s stories is one way to stretch our hearts. When we see ourselves in the pain in another’s eyes, our hearts expand, soften and fill with compassion.

There are spiritual disciplines such as the Buddhist practice of lovingkindness (metta) that invite us to an intentional opening of the heart. A yoga practice offers physical ways to open our heart and to release held pain. An ongoing practice of meditation, with the intention of cultivating compassion in our thoughts, speaking, and actions, also leads to the way of the open heart. In our own tradition, practicing democracy in our congregations is a spiritual discipline that can also lead to a large and open heart, filled with love. Practicing democracy in our congregations takes us through difficult conversations where we are asked to hold our differences within the context of a covenant. Being together in a religious community becomes a spiritual discipline when we move past surface conversations about the details of our lives and tell our stories to each other in ways that open our hearts wide to an intimacy that keeps are hearts open when life is conspiring to tear them apart.

As Unitarian Universalists we affirm our fifth principle: the right of conscience and the use of the democratic process within our congregations and in society at large. Who we are as a people of faith makes it difficult for us NOT to participate in the public square. When we vote we can vote with hearts open wide with a hope held deep in our bones.

How will our hearts feel November 7th? It can depend on the outcome of the elections. It can also depend on our capacity to keep our hearts open. According to Parker Palmer, “Politics in the hands of those whose hearts have been broken open, not apart, helps us hold our differences creatively and use our power courageously for the sake of a more equitable, just, and compassionate world” (page 18). May it be so and may we be the people to make it so.

Practical tools for opening the heart: Thich Nhat Hahn, the Vietnamese Buddhist monk and peace activist, offers tools for the engaged spiritual life. An engaged spirituality is ultimately an embodied spirituality. One’s engagement with the world arises out of one’s body and its unique experience of emotion, imagination and intuition. One simple practice that Thich Nhat Hahn promotes might help us get through the days leading to the election. He advises that before we get out of bed in the morning we smile. Just take a few moments before we sit up in the morning to notice our breath, pay attention to the stirrings of our waking body and simply smile. Another technique he teaches is to imagine we are holding a rose, breathing in we imagine the beautiful aroma and we smile. Breathing in, the beauty of the imagined rose fills our thoughts; breathing out, we extend beauty and lovingkindness to all beings.

Another practical tool comes from the book we are studying together this year, Theology Ablaze by Tom Owen-Towle (page 146-147). Tom shares a Nepalese body prayer that he received from UU minister Orlanda Brugnola. This body prayer is Tom’s morning practice:

·         Begin by standing, planting your feet firmly on the floor and bending your knees slightly. Cup your hands right above your navel, in a receiving posture. Affirm being alive with words of your choosing. Here are Tom’s: “I am a child of the universe. I belong here. It’s good for me to be alive.”

·         Now lift your hands in a prayerful gesture towards the sky and say out loud words of gratitude. Tom uses the E.E. Cummings’ line “I thank You God for most this amazing day.”

·         After your hands are stretched to their highest point open them wide into the shape of a chalice to receive the gifts the day brings, affirming in your own words what Tom says: “Into my hands are received today’s delights and difficulties, sorrows and joys.”

·         Then circle your hands back to the beginning position and offer yourself to the day using Tom’s or similar words: “I promise to spread these blessings to every living entity that I greet this precious day.” Repeat the body prayer three or four times.
