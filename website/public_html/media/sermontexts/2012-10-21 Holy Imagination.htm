Holy Imagination

©Rev. Lora Brandis

Preached October 21, 2012

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Congregation

 

This is the photo of Balpreet Kaur that was taken and then posted to the web site Reddit, without her knowledge. Reddit is a social news web site where registered users submit content either as images or words for others to rate. Viewers’ ratings determine where the post is placed on either the front page of the site or in subcategories on the site. This photo was placed by a Reddit user in the subcategory labeled “Funny” with this caption: “I’m not sure what to conclude from this.”

The Internet being what it is – our daily reminder of the interdependent web of all existence of which we are a part – a “funny” thing did in fact occur. The girl from the picture responded. Her friends had seen the post, re-posted it on Facebook and then Balpreet responded.

She explained how her religion reveres the body as a sacred gift given by the Divine Being. To alter her body would be to reject the gift and to separate herself from the Divinity within her. “By transcending societal views of beauty, I believe that I can focus more on my actions,” wrote Balpreet Kaur, teaching the Reddit poster and the rest of us, a different definition of beauty.

And then another “funny” thing happened. The original poster responded: “I know that this post ISN'T a funny post but I felt the need to apologize to the Sikhs, Balpreet, and anyone else I offended when I posted that picture… It was an incredibly rude, judgmental, and ignorant thing to post” (http://jezebel.com/5946643/reddit-users-attempt-to-shame-sikh-woman-get-righteously-schooled).

We are taught that beauty is in the eye of the beholder. How we see this picture of Balpreet Kaur teaches us to see beauty from the perspective of a devout Sikh woman who values action over appearance. Balpreet’s appearance is a radical act in a world that values appearance over action, a world that values decoration over beauty.

Beauty is usually not one of those religious words that we think of as being divisive like God or salvation. Yet, even beauty, like God and salvation or truth and justice, is subject to interpretation. Even the word beauty forces us to engage in a respectful dialogue that honors the “other’s” culture, religion and personal story.

We are increasingly becoming a world that communicates in images rather than words. Take, for example, a quick search of the word beauty on Google images. The search brings up mostly women and then mostly women using “beauty” products. In this clipped image, taken from hundreds of items that came up from the search, there is one image that can be considered sacred or religious.

This is a photo of the Rayonnant Rose Window in Notre Dame in Paris. In the Gothic style, this window would have been created as a way to experience the “light of God.” Its inherent beauty assumed to have directly come from an experience of the divine.

Trey Ratcliff, a travel photographer who “love(s) using technology to create beautiful things” recently wrote an essay on the universal language of images. I read this essay eager for an answer to the challenging conversation we have entered into this year with theological words such as covenant, forgiveness, beauty, evil, God, grace and more. As we develop our language of faith as Unitarian Universalists we bump into walls of misused words that many of us have rejected and stubbornly refuse to revisit. The conversation we have among ourselves is difficult enough, unfortunately creating barriers to how we describe our faith to others in a multi-faith world. The conversation we have with outside faiths becomes almost impossible as we seek common ground with words that hold multiple meanings, depending on who is speaking. Wouldn’t it be so much easier if there were universal images that conveyed the same thing to all people across the globe?

This is Ratcliff’s thesis: “Imagery is a universal language that has no borders and describes truths and stories that all humans can recognize” (http://www.facebookstories.com/stories/1937/essay-a-universal-language-of-images). He asks us to imagine a world that developed without written words, one that developed instead with a device capable of capturing images. In this imaginary world, everyone easily creates images to communicate concepts and stories. This is a much more efficient way to communicate in his view.

He is passionate about this way of communicating, a way that is not so hard to imagine given that many of us now have cameras we hold in our hands every day. This is a revolution equivalent to the invention of the printing press. Only now we don’t need a printing press to instantly share an image captured of a beautiful sunset, a loved one’s smile, or the arresting appearance of a stranger. “Billions of people now have a totally new way to communicate, and we will all discover this new visual literacy together,” Ratcliff writes. “Now, finally, our ideas and thoughts and feelings and stories can effortlessly travel across borders, cultures, and time,” he rather naively concludes.

The other “funny,” as in peculiar, part of Balpreet Kaur’s photo can be seen in the logo emblazoned on the t-shirt she is wearing. One might even call this beautiful, rather than “funny” or peculiar. The Better Together t-shirt is from Interfaith Youth Core (IFYC), an organization that focuses on storytelling and engagement between different faiths. Balpreet has attended the Interfaith Leadership Institutes of IFYC, the Chicago-based organization that trains college students nationwide to be interfaith leaders. She also mentors other student interfaith leaders as a coach with IFYC. In contrast to the photographer’s conclusion that we will develop a universal language using only images, Balpreet’s story strongly illustrates the power of the spoken and written word. It takes words to understand this image. It takes Balpreet’s words stating, “I believe there is no difference between a man and me. Just as a Sikh man, I too can adorn myself with a turban, choose to keep my hair, and live by the same discipline and love of the scriptures” (http://www.huffingtonpost.com/balpreet-kaur/turning-ugliness-into-beauty-through-interfaith-leadership_b_1943244.html#slide=more255107).

We need Balpreet’s words to understand her experience of inner beauty because of her radical expression that resists conventional images of outer beauty. Ultimately beauty is in the eye of the beholder. We choose to experience beauty when we step outside of the confines of a depleted imagination fed on stereotypes and prejudice.  We choose to see beauty when we use our imaginations in a holy and creative manner. Immersing ourselves in beauty, art and creativity becomes a spiritual practice that takes us out of our small selves into the realm of higher purpose and deeper insight.

“What would happen if I heeded the admonitions of beauty?” the poet Baron Wormser asks. What would happen if we heeded the admonitions of beauty? First of all we would balance our need to be analytical with an appreciative mind. We would balance our left and right brains. We would slow down, “take the time to drive to a pond and spend an hour watching swans” (Wormser). We would look out the window, past all of the debates, the to-dos and the “shoulds,” and we would notice the snow, or the ocean, or the color of the sky. We would appreciate the spaces in between, the spaces given to us each day out of a beauty that knows no direction and has nothing to accomplish.

German liberation theologian Dorothee Soelle wrote about these spaces in between from a life experience steeped in social activism. In contemplating the couplet: The rose has no why; it blooms because it blooms. It notes not itself, asks not if it be seen, Soelle wrote, “For what is beautiful is holy all by itself.” She also wrote that, “For many years I have been engaged in the peace movement, and the question I am asked most often is, of course, the one about success: What is the point of it all? But I have come to realize that any thinking that is oriented solely toward success is essentially cynical. For the rose has no why, and one has to do some things, even when they meet with no success now.” (The Rose Has No Why by Dorothee Soelle)

I am often asked how we can keep going when there is still so much to be done. We can surround ourselves with beauty. This is not to say that we embrace attachment and materialism, this is to say that we surround ourselves with the beauty of the natural world and the beauty that we find in our social justice efforts. We can become the artists of our own lives.

Julia Cameron created a tool for this about twenty years ago called the artist date. The basic idea is to take a consistent block of time, perhaps two hours a week, to nurture your inner artist, to surround yourself with beauty. The majority of us don’t even think of ourselves as being creative at all. Haunted by kindergarten teachers admonishing us to color within the lines and tortured by memories of being shamed publicly for compositions badly written, songs sung off-key and stick-figures awkwardly drawn, many of us pit our intellectual self against our feeling self. The left brain wins and the right brain is only for creative types. Beauty does not only belong to those who create it. Beauty also belongs to those who appreciate it. Julia Cameron’s artist date is designed as a spiritual practice for those of us too busy fretting, planning, texting, instant messaging, Facebooking, working, child rearing, complaining about balanced budgets, losing sports teams, foreign policies, lost debates, won debates, upcoming elections, traffic…too busy to notice the beauty in the spaces in between.

We can “see the world in a grain of sand, and a heaven in a wildflower,” as the poet William Blake would have us do, when we share in the divine power of creation that emanates through beauty into our open hearts.

It might be easier if there were universal images that conveyed all of the religious words we are exploring together this year. The language of faith is far more complex. Research does show that facial expressions are universally interpreted. A smile is a smile in any language. Balpreet Kaur’s smile requires words to tell its whole story, its holy story.

We are a people of the word and because of our Puritan roots we rely heavily on word and space stripped of symbol. We are suspicious of word and worship space highly decorated, suspicious of being emotionally manipulated. We have also been hurt by well-meaning teachers and critical parents. Our impulse to create, to beautify has been thwarted. We grade ourselves and each other on a surface performance of acts meant to please. The spiritual practice of noticing beauty, appreciating the story behind the smile, connects us to each other and the ongoing act of creation in deep soul-satisfying ways.

The realm of worship was once also the realm of the creative arts: sanctuaries were painted with bold frescoes and adorned with stained glass windows. The roots of contemporary commercial drama go back to liturgical drama that was a regular part of the worship experience. And of course the tradition of sacred music still crosses over from worship into majestic concert halls.

As we leave our Puritan practices in the past and as more of our congregations incorporate the arts into worship, our over-emphasis on the rational becomes balanced with appreciative mind. Temporary art installations grace our once-plain walls. Dramatic performances re-enter the sacred space. Liturgical dance, celebratory music…beauty resides alongside our precious word.

“Inside you there’s an artist you don’t know about…. Say yes quickly, if you know, if you’ve known it from the beginning of the universe.” (Jalai ud-Din Rumi) Say yes to being the artist of your own life. Say yes to beauty. Say yes to your own holy imagination. Say yes and Amen and may it be so.
