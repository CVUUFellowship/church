The Seventh Day

© by Lora Brandis

Preached December 16, 2012

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship

 

“And on the seventh day God finished the work that he had done, and he rested on the seventh day from all the work he had done. So God blessed the seventh day and hallowed it, because on it God rested from all the work that he had done in creation” (Gen 2:2-3). The poetic language of these verses was specifically chosen by the priestly writers of Genesis, writing to the Jews of the Babylonian exile. Creation stories were used in Babylonian worship to affirm a created order in the face of the chaos of the ancient world. As we face the chaos of a world we have created, a world we take responsibility for creating, we gather in worship to make sense again of violence that is in fact senseless. We gather in worship to affirm that together we create order in the face of chaos. In worship we share an intimacy that reminds us of our common humanity, our common heartache, and our ability to heal. In worship we learn to love.

This morning’s sermon is the second exploring the theme of Sabbath, specifically the Judeo-Christian tradition of Sabbath that includes both rest and worship. Last week we considered what it is to practice a personal Sabbath in a way that ritualizes the chaos of modern life. This week we look to worship, the communal ritual that asks us to enter into a time set aside for contemplation, renewal and if we dare, transformation.

I learned early on in my ministry that worship is one of those words that irritate some Unitarian Universalists. For some it implies subservience to a god that orders all of life, a god that decrees that some should live and some should die.  I was going to unpack that word for those among us who are battered and bruised by worship experiences that tell us what to believe. I was going to remind us all that the word worship is from the Middle English woerscippen, the root of the word worth. Worship simply asks us to consider things of worth.

As I sat before my blank page Friday, reports started coming in from Newtown Connecticut where a 20-year-old man killed his mother, drove her car to a local elementary school and killed 26 people, including 20 young children, and then took his own life.

We are shocked and we are saddened. And some of us are heading for church. Friday night, in that small Connecticut town, vigils were held in many houses of worship. Across the nation places of worship opened their doors Saturday for people to sit and pray, to weep, to be held in the quiet of a place set aside for honoring what we value. Today the President is attending an interfaith vigil.

Of course we have learned that even places of worship are not safe. On August 5th of this year a white supremacist who associated with neofascist groups walked into a Sikh temple killing six and wounding four others before turning the gun on himself. On July 27, 2008, a man fired a shotgun at members of the Tennessee Valley Unitarian Universalist Church during a youth performance of a musical, killing two people and wounding seven others. He stated to police “that he had targeted the church because of its liberal teachings and his belief that all liberals should be killed because they were ruining the country” (http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knoxville_Unitarian_Universalist_church_shooting).

The motivation of the young man who killed in Connecticut is still not clear. It has been reported that his brother said he suffered from some sort of “personality disorder.” The debate rises up again and bounces back and forth between access to guns and gun owners’ rights, the need for background checks, and the dismantling of the mental healthcare system more than 20 years ago that has left a vulnerable population without proper care. Preliminary reports indicate that the guns used in Connecticut belonged to the troubled man’s mother. The issue of background checks may not even apply in this case. Easy access to guns may be more the issue in this case. Access to assault-type rifles that can be reloaded often and quickly may be the real point in this case and other recent massacres.

Some have named this evil. If by evil they mean a society that condones revenge and violent responses to violence, then yes let’s name this evil. If by evil they mean the mentally unstable do not get proper care, then yes let’s name this evil. If by evil they mean the constant barrage of mind-numbing violence that dominates our headlines, then yes let’s name this evil. Last year handguns killed 48 people in Japan, 8 in Great Britain, 34 in Switzerland, 52 in Canada, 58 in Israel, 21 in Sweden, 42 in West Germany, and 10,728 in the United States of America. If this is what they mean by evil, then yes let’s name this evil.

Some have said that their god could have protected the victims from this evil, but their god does not go where he isn’t wanted. If we feared a god that judges us, some have said, then we would not commit acts of violence. If we allowed prayer in schools, their god would protect our children, some have said. What we need, some have said, is a change of heart, not a change in the law.

Ralph Waldo Emerson wrote that, “A person will worship something, have no doubt about that. We may think our tribute is paid in secret in the dark recesses of our hearts, but it will out. That which dominates our imaginations and our thoughts will determine our lives, and our character. Therefore, it behooves us to be careful what we worship, for what we are worshipping we are becoming.” When we worship a god that doesn’t show up to protect children from a violent death, we are worshipping a false god, an idol. I agree that we need a change of heart. We need to replace our hardened, judgmental hearts with a heart broken wide open by the pain of our failure to protect our children. Yes, we need a change of heart.

We also need to be careful what we worship as a nation. When we worship individual freedom at the cost of interdependence and interconnectedness, we are worshipping a false god, an idol. When we worship the god of corporate profit, we worship a gun lobby that only answers to gun manufacturers who only answer to shareholders. When we worship fear, we worship at an altar built by a small and diminished humanity. “It behooves us to be careful what we worship, for what we are worshipping we are becoming.”

It has been said that when all of the concrete gods have been destroyed, the divine remains. When we stop worshipping false, concrete gods, many of us stop worshipping altogether and we stop coming to church. Many outside our faith ask, “What do Unitarian Universalists worship?” in the same way they ask, “What do Unitarian Universalists believe?” Here are better questions to ask. These are questions we can ask ourselves on a regular basis: “How do Unitarian Universalists worship?” and “Why do Unitarian Universalists worship?”

Poet, prophet, humanist and UU minister Kenneth Patton invites us to “worship with our eyes and ears and fingertips.” We worship leaving room for mystery. We worship looking to each other for answers. “Let us worship,” Patton wrote and “let us learn to love.” We are not so arrogant to assume we already know how to love when we bring our entire selves to the worship experience. We do not worship a god who abandons young children as punishment for some perceived collective sin. We worship, wanting to learn how twenty murdered children can teach us to love.

UU minister the Rev. Dr. Victoria Weinstein tells a funny story about a phone call she received one morning just prior to the church service. The caller wanted to know what the topic was for the day. She blurted out, “Come to Church Anyway!” and then lamely had to admit what the actual topic was. “Come to Church Anyway” would have been my central theme for this sermon before Friday morning, before a gunman shattered the quiet of a small New England town.

Come to church when you are too tired to come to church, I would have said. Come to church when you cannot stand the topic. Come to church, anyway. Come to church when your life is falling apart. Come to church when your life isn’t falling apart. Come to church anyway, I would have said before the phone call from my daughter telling me to watch the news coming out of Newtown. You do not know what pain hides behind your neighbor’s smile, I would have said. Come to church because how we worship and why we worship is lessened by your absence. Come be a part of the shared silence of our meditation. Come be a part of an intimacy that reminds us of our common humanity, our common heartache, and our ability to heal.

Now even the category called the “nones” are probably showing up for church today – those who check the box “none” when asked to identify which religion they practice. According to a recent study one in five adults have no religious affiliation at all. According to the Pew Research Center the number of Americans who do not identify with any religion continues to grow at a rapid pace. One-fifth of the U.S. public – and a third of adults under 30 – are religiously unaffiliated today, the highest percentages ever. I could tell you that that is what is wrong with our country, that if more of us were affiliated with a religion that there would be less violence in the world. This is simply not true. I know that violence done in the name of religion is what keeps so many people away from church. I know that naming god as an omnipotent, omnipresent, punishing parent keeps people away, too.

I hope “the nones” are attending a house of worship today. It is dark days like these that we need to light a candle and sing a song of hope. On a day like today, it is appropriate to say, despite all of the flaws of religion and a mixed-up humanity who continues to worship false gods, “Come to Church Anyway!”

It is dark days like this one that answer the question, “why do we worship?” We worship because we need to learn how to love. What we worship we are becoming. When we worship, we are learning to love.

Today we remember all of the victims of the tragedy in Connecticut. We remember those who died and those who mourn. We mourn for our nation as we make sense of the senseless and speak out for the defenseless. We remember that what we worship we are becoming. When we worship we are learning to love.

We remember the children: 12 girls and 8 boys, all only six or seven years old. Their lives cut short before they could learn all that life had promised. We who are still living carry the promise of their lives into our becoming, into the world we now create.

What we worship we are becoming. When we worship we are learning to love.

We remember the six women: the teachers, the principal, the school psychologist. They teach us now to honor their memory by remembering all of the victims of violence, all those who die each day from gunfire.

What we worship we are becoming. When we worship we are learning to love.

We remember the perpetrator of this crime and his mother. We mourn with their family. We know the worth and dignity of every individual and affirm the interconnected web of our existence. What affects one of us, affects us all.

What we worship we are becoming. When we worship we are learning to love.

We are sad. We are shocked. We are angry. We cry out against the false gods of easy answers and arrogant declarations.

What we worship we are becoming. When we worship we are learning to love.

 We remember the first responders. We give thanks for all of the helpers of humankind: the police, the firefighters, the medical professionals, the teachers who protected children while listening to the sounds of violence and loss.

What we worship we are becoming. When we worship we are learning to love.

We remember our elected officials: the powerbrokers and those who share power. May they hear the voice of the people rise up and call for just laws, compassionate budgets and abundant healthcare.

What we worship we are becoming. When we worship we are learning to love.

Finally, we look to ourselves. We look into our own hearts, into the dark recesses of our own hearts. May we be the people we proclaim we are. May we deeply know that,

What we worship we are becoming. When we worship we are learning to love.
