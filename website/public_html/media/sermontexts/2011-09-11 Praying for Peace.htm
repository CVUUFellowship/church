September 11, 2011: Praying for Peace

By Rev. Lora Brandis

Preached September 11, 2011

Conejo Valley Unitarian Universalist Fellowship

 

We have been moving towards this moment all week. Hearing the stories on the news. Stories of survivors and of lives cut short. Telling our own stories about how the tragedy on that day affected our own lives. Where we were when we heard the news. How the day seemed to stop as we stood in shock in front of images of horror. Here is one of the stories from September 11, 2001. It is the story of two mothers.

Phyllis Rodriguez’s son, Greg, died in the attacks on the World Trade Center. Aicha el-Wafi’s son, Zacarias Moussaoui, was convicted of conspiring in the attacks.

Phyllis Rodriguez and Aicha el-Wafi met about one year after the events of September 2001. Aicha asked that a human rights organization put her in touch with some of the families of the victims. Phyllis was the only mother in the group of five families that Aicha met. As mothers, they felt an instant connection.

Aicha was married when she was 14. She lost children when she was 15 and 16. The suffering she had already felt as a mother was increased beyond comprehension when she learned that her son was an Islamic extremist accused of being a part of the attacks on the World Trade Center. Some might blame Aicha for raising her son as an Islamic extremist. To blame Aicha would be to ignore what it is like to be married against your will when you are still a child, and then be asked to mother sons and daughters in a way that doesn’t value the spirit of mothering. Today Aicha remains firm in her belief that her son was not directly responsible for the attacks. She also remains firm in her belief that extremist thinking like his created a climate that made the attacks possible.

Aicha tells her story of moving to France from Morocco when she was a young wife and mother at the age of 17. She emphasizes that she was accepted and welcomed in France. Her son Zacarias was not. While he was being called “dirty Arab” by peers, at home he was violently treated by his father who eventually abandoned the family.  Aicha knows that she is not responsible for the choices her son made as an adult; she feels guilty just for giving birth to him. Out of a mixture of guilt, anger and compassion Aicha tells her story to anyone who will listen. She tells her story to young Muslim girls so that they will fight being married so young and against their will. She reaches out to other mothers with a message of peace and tolerance. Aicha stands before us with the face of the “other” and reminds us that our enemy has a mother.

Greg’s mother Phyllis was listening to a phone message from Greg saying there had been an accident at the World Trade Center but he was alright when she saw on television the second plane crash into the second tower. She didn’t hear from Greg again, and it wasn’t until the next evening that his death would become official. In the first days after the attacks and from the depths of their grief, Greg’s parents reached out with a letter to President Bush asking for peaceful, rational solutions to terrorism. Several weeks later they spoke out against imposing the death penalty for Zacarias even before they met his mother.  When they did meet it wasn’t until Phyllis felt strong enough to face the enormity of her grief. Standing face-to-face with Aicha, she knew that it was easy for people to sympathize with her own grief but who would sympathize with Zacarias’ mother? Phyllis realized that, as mothers, their suffering was the same. She could sympathize with Zacarias’ mother.

As mothers they have been friends for nine years. As friends and mothers they have worked together for a world in which forgiveness is a process that places fallible human beings in a context for which we are all responsible. Phyllis continues to support Aicha in her campaign to bring Zacarias back to France to serve out his sentence closer to his mother and to the rest of their family. In telling the story of her first meeting with Aicha, Phyllis comments how hard it was to meet someone so different, from such a different culture and who’d led a life that was almost beyond her comprehension. How afraid we are of the other, Phyllis realized. After taking the first step and conquering our fears, we can go on to meet all sorts of “others,” those who are completely different from us. For Phyllis and her husband this awareness has taken them out of the lives they knew before their son was killed. They both continue to work with Aicha and now they also work with convicted murderers, facilitating peace circles and forging new paths to justice and reconciliation.

Just days after the attacks that killed their son, Phyllis and Orlando Rodriguez wrote a letter to President Bush, which was published in the New York Times. “This is not the time for empty gestures to make us feel better,” they wrote. “It is not the time to act like bullies. We urge you to think about how our government can develop peaceful, rational solutions to terrorism, solutions that do not sink us to the inhuman level of terrorists” (http://inoursonsname.com).

Just thirty-four hours after the first plane struck the World Trade Center, the late Rev. Dr. Forrest Church delivered a sermon at All Souls Unitarian Church in New York City. He predicted that, in the years following the tragedy, we would be known for our nation’s resolve in leading the war on terrorism and as individuals we would be known for how well we could see ourselves in the faces of the “other.” He warned that we cannot choose justice over mercy, anger over compassion or war over love. He also warned that we cannot pray only for peace. When Rev. Church wrote those words our country had not taken the war on terrorism into Iraq. When he wrote those words, he admonished us to balance our prayers for peace with action.

When it looked like our leaders were gathering evidence to rationalize taking the war on terror into Iraq, I joined with a small group of peaceful protestors near the JFK memorial in Dallas. About a week later I joined with another small group on a darkened hillside in a park near my home, holding candles and silently praying for peace. When the United States invaded Iraq I read about an effort to call attention to what some termed an “unjust” war by lighting a candle every night until the war was over. We were to light this candle in our front window, a visible light against the darkness. I was the only one in my neighborhood with a candle burning each night. The war dragged on. The candle didn’t last. When there wasn’t enough wick left to hold the flame I gave up and threw the candle away. I didn’t replace it with another candle. I was backing up my prayers for peace with action that mattered little. It has been ten years since September 11, 2001, some of us are still praying for peace. What do we do now?

What do we do now that Osama Bin Laden is dead? What do we do now in response to those in our own country who burn the Koran and block mosques from being built? What do we do now knowing that we cannot pray only for peace?

Unitarian Universalists have historically supported the “just war” tradition. We have also supported pacifism as is evidenced by over eighty resolutions written about peace since the merger of Unitarians and Universalists in 1961. We are not historically a peace church like Quakers or Mennonites. Our sixth principle is the goal of world community with peace, liberty, and justice for all. Just as we live with an uncomfortable ambiguity about the inherent worth and dignity of terrorists and mass-murderers, we also live with an uncomfortable ambiguity about establishing peace through violent means.

You can hear it in our 2010 Statement of Conscience called “Creating Peace” when we state: “Many of us believe force is sometimes necessary as a last resort, while others of us believe in the consistent practice of nonviolence.” This is our faith at its best: embracing the contradictions and agreeing to stay in a diverse conversation without any easy answers.

What can we do on this 10th anniversary of the loss of our country’s innocence? What can we do on this day and every day? We can stay in the conversation. Back up our prayers with action. Even in the face of little progress and passive despair, we can live knowing that “the common good is best served when each community has a chance to make its unique contribution” (Eboo Patel, Acts of Faith). Our unique contribution is a faith built on reason, freedom and tolerance. May we remember that, this day and every day. May we pray for peace, be peace, and bring peace into all that we do. May it be so. Amen.
