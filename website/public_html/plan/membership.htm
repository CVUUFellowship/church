The adult membership count of the Church refers to the number of people who have joined the Church and who continue to meet the membership guidelines as outlined in the Church bylaws. Traditionally, the number grows through the church year and then is adjusted end rectified by the beginning of the following year. Obviously, there are many reasons for the variance in the number as some members die, move away, lose interest etc. History tells us that a Church will lose approximately 10% of their membership each year. The important fact in dealing with the number of members is that ours is a real number. Each member annually makes a conscious decision as to the status of their membership and only those that wish to continue are counted.

 

Church consultants talk about ‘plateaus of membership’, a membership number that when reached stretches a church’s resources to its limits. When reaching these plateaus a church either creates opportunities for growth or creates conscious or unconscious obstacles to growth. Once reaching a plateau a church either grows to a new plateau or eventually reduces its membership number to a previous level. As of November 2000, our membership is 605. This is a net increase from 2 years ago of 60 members.

 

There are many factors, which affect the membership number. Neighborhood Church recently spent two years involved in an aggressive capital campaign that might have scared some newcomers off. Secondly, the past year was a transition year with all of our children’s programs taking place off campus. On the positive side, for the past two years our ministers incorporated a systematic method of acclimating newcomers to Church life through the well-attended ‘bridging groups”. The continued high quality of Sunday Services, adult programming and well-coordinated outreach efforts have also been factors in our Church’s growth.

 

The committee believes that with the enhancement of our physical plant and with a professional staff at full strength the Church will grow to the next plateau of membership, accommodating 750 adults and 400 children by 2005. It is important to note that the committee is not advocating growth but rather planning for it. A membership of 750 adults should translate into a Sunday Church attendance of about 500, which is an increase of 50 adults per service over the average current Sunday attendance. Physically, our current campus configuration is equipped to handle these numbers however, there will have to be adjustments to the infrastructure and resources so that we can continue to meet the needs of our religious community. This is the basis of the need for planning for the next five years and beyond.
