Social justice and community outreach are important to Neighborhood Church, its ministers, members and friends. It is considered the tangible means of expression of the seven Unitarian Universalist principles. Long-standing efforts include Friends in Deed, sandwich-making for Union Station during the summer, Funds for Human Needs, the annual Alternative Gift Market (organized by the junior high school class and their leaders).

 

In January of 1997, Fran Neumann was hired as a part-time Outreach Director (ten hours per week) and is now viewed as indispensable by many in the congregation. She has developed and coordinated many additional local outreach opportunities. These include on-campus efforts as well as opportunities for Congregants to donate their time, talents and money in the community. Members are involved in outreach leadership through the Social Justice Community Outreach Council, which meets bi-monthly to share information, coordinate activities where appropriate and plan events. The youth of the church are directly involved through the fourth Sunday of the month Religious Exploration programs, which are aptly named “Justice Sundays”. These are joint ventures of the outreach council and the Religious Education Council. Outreach opportunities currently appear in the Sunday Order of Service, posted on the kiosk, and web page. There is an “outreach table” set-up on the patio each Sunday morning with explanations and sign-up forms for each program. The newsletter has a page devoted to Outreach and Social justice and the calendar posts imminent events. Organized outreach tends to be hands-on or take on the form of monetary donations. Fran (who is retiring in February of 2001) has done a wonderful job during her four years in the position and it is largely through her efforts that we can begin to see the benefits of an organized and coordinated approach to the church’s outreach efforts.

 

During the next five years we project that a full time outreach director will lead an effort that will be made up of three components.

1.                Hands-on opportunities- these will be a continuation of many of the types of opportunities that we are currently engaged in the community.

2.                Advocacy- we will become a strong presence in our local, state, and national political scene. Building relationships with decision makers will be a key aspect of the outreach program ensuring that our Unitarian Universalist values are being promoted in a legislative agenda.

3.                Institution Building- We will create long-term solutions to community challenges by creating on-going self-supporting programs. Some examples of successful existing programs in our community are Young & Healthy and the Pasadena Aids Service Center.
