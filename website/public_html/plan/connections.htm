One of the fears of people who belong to a growing Church is that the institution will be become so big that ‘1 won’t know anyone anymore”. Of course the counter to that is who would join a Church or continue to come to a Church where no one knew each other? Obviously, a member in a large church will not know every other member but it has been a long time since that was actually the case at Neighborhood Church. Ironically, we have learned in discussions with other larger Unitarian-Universalist Churches that as a church grows there can be more opportunities to ‘connect” with people of like interests then in smaller churches because there are simply more people. The concept of ‘growing larger by becoming smaller” should be the method that we choose to follow as we reach the next plateau of membership. We recognize a need for building ‘community” and ‘connections” because of societal stress and the sometimes madness of daily living.

 

Currently our Adult Religious Education Committee has produced an aggressive schedule of opportunities for people to come together, learn and discuss a wide variety of issues. The programs are well organized and mostly well attended. The Membership Committee is working on a more inviting method of dealing with visitors and removing possible obstacles to return visits. The Personal Concerns Committee does a good job of providing opportunities for members to take care of each other. As mentioned earlier in this report, the bridging groups have been successful in bringing new members together and offering a forum where people can tell their stories and connect with one another.

 

If there is a void, it may come for people who have been here for a while and would benefit from something beyond “committee work” or just attending Sunday Services. A few years ago a committee, Neighborhood Connections, was formed to plan and organize opportunities for people to come together. This may be the natural committee to re-vision what an effective ‘connection” committee might develop. Instead of the one or two inter-generational activities planned for large groups this committee should look at other small group activities that might include:

  • Setting up tables and chairs after services so people have a comfortable place to sit and talk.
  • Assign floaters every Sunday whose job it is to engage people who don’t seem to be engaged.
  • Pair old and new members-for mentoring.
  • Set-up ‘relational sessions” where people share their story in small groups and learn about others.
  • Sponsor more social/community activities where the goal Is for people to have fun together. Possibilities would be Sunday lunches or brunches and evening activities that would offer opportunities for different age groups to get together.
  • Develop a dynamic program for young married or partnered couples with or without young children.
  • Develop, as a part of small group ministries, small common interest groups of 10-15 people that attract families and their children as well as single persons or couples without children.
  • Develop a program for young adults with intentional outreach to area colleges and universities.
