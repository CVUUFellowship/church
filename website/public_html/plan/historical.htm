A Historical Perspective

 

Whatever Improvements we create we will protect them so that those who follow will find the same beauty and utility that we have seized for ourselves today.

A plan for the future, even so brief a span as five years, gathers meaning through historical perspective. Consider our location, 301 N. Orange Grove Boulevard in the City of Pasadena, where the Church now plans it future.

 

The City of Pasadena, incorporated as recently as 1887, was certainly not the first to recognize these fertile lands as special and preferred. They overlook the grand arroyo to the west, and have a magnificent view of the San Gabriel Mountains to the north.

 

Native Americans, over hundreds of years before Europeans arrived, built their communities here. It was then, as now, the high ground in this region and therefore a prized location. Archeologists continue to find remnants of those early settlers along the arroyo.

 

A Tract Map filed with the City of Pasadena in 1904 is the first document of record naming the Westmoreland Place, showing it adjacent to what has become the Orange Grove Boulevard of today. This Tract map shows the first subdivision of lots for houses on Westmoreland Place.

 

Early citizens of Pasadena realized the value of this special location. This land attracted people from the East with high expectations and the money to realize them. Six fine mansions were built on Westmoreland Place during the period 1904 —1910 and four of these survive today.

 

In the late 1960’s, three lots on Westmoreland Place, each with a fine house, were purchased by Neighborhood Church for $3.00 a square foot of land. The houses were free. This shrewd purchase was due to a confluence of circumstances which has become legendary. It was an act of providence and has inspired the leadership of this Church ever since. We, the current faithful successors to the Neighborhood Church of Pasadena founded in 1887 at a much different location, now find ourselves the inheritors of this good fortune.

 

Neighborhood Church is the owner of NO.2 Westmoreland Place, known to us as Neighborhood House, and to architectural historians as the “Cole House”, after the original owner. Both No. I and No. 3, considered less distinguished than the Cole House were demolished by the Church to make way for its new buildings.

 

No. 4 Westmoreland Place, “The Gamble House” is listed on the National Register of Historic Places. The Gamble family deeded it in 1960 in joint-trust to the City of Pasadena and the University of Southern California.

 

Pacific Oaks College occupies No. 6 and No. 7 Westmoreland Place, making it the third institution to share the beauty of this special location. Now, after some thirty years of work and promise, we are looking for ways to further make this place our own. We have re-built ourselves for the present as well as the future.

 

At this point, it is relevant that we pledge ourselves to be responsible tenants of this land as long as we are able. We will continue to use this site with respect. We will preserve the beauty we have found here. Whatever improvements we create we will protect so that those who follow will find the same beauty and utility that we have seized for ourselves today.
