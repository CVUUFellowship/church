Love Came Down at Christmas
by Rev. Lora Brandis
December 21, 2014

The story of Christ's nativity makes sense on a mythological level. Joining the story of one lowly-born in a manger among the animals with the stories of other gods and heroes celebrated at the time of the Winter Solstice makes mythological sense. In the nineteenth century poet Christina Rossetti wrote, "Love came down at Christmas, love all lovely, love divine," capturing the essence of our deep yearning for the transcendent.

The Work of Loving the World
by Rev. Lora Brandis
December 7, 2014

The sermon description goes here."My work is loving the world," writes the poet Mary Oliver. It is easy to love the world of the poet: "the phoebe, the delphinium, the sheep in the pasture, and the pasture." It is difficult to love the world when it is torn apart by injustice, especially when we are unable to come to consensus as to what justice looks like. Our work is to love the world, no matter what.

The Gospel According to Kurt
by Merlin Snider
November 30, 2014

Gospel is one of those words that do double duty. It means a life story, like the gospels in the Bible. It also means some incredibly important kernel of truth. The "gospel truth." Fellowship member Merlin Snider tells the tale of how in graduate school he found the life and work of Kurt Vonnegut, self-described as "simultaneously a humorist and a serious novelist." Like Billy Pilgrim, his famous character from Slaughterhouse Five, Vonnegut came unstuck in time because of the horrible things he had witnessed, and in Vonnegut's heroic, anti-heroic way, managed to describe our human condition with dark humor, compassion, and ultimately hope.

by Rev. Lora Brandis
November 23, 2014

Gratitude and reverence are important to humanist teachings. Reverence is not just about a feeling associated with a deity. Meister Eckhart supposedly said, “If the only prayer you say in your life is thank you that would suffice.” Cicero’s quote works better for those who don’t pray: “Gratitude is not only the greatest of virtues, but the parent of all others.” Reason does not preclude gratitude and reverence, particularly during this time of year.

We Are All Humanists
by Rev. Lora Brandis
November 2, 2014

A neighbor asked William R. Murry what he meant when he said he was a humanist. He replied that, “humanism refers to the affirmation of the worth and dignity of every person, a commitment to human betterment, and the necessity for human beings to take responsibility for themselves and the world.” With that definition we are all humanists, but there are also different kinds of humanists who attend UU congregations – religious humanists.

Words and Deeds of Prophetic Women and Men
by Rev. Lora Brandis
October 12, 2014

Our religion has a long history filled with the words and deeds of prophetic women and men. We are known for our acts of social justice because of the words and deeds of women and men like Susan B. Anthony, Clara Barton, Horace Greeley, John Marshall, Joseph Priestly, and others who are remembered for speaking truth to power. Inspired by the past, we are challenged to transform the world with justice and compassion.
